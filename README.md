## What This Course Is

Prof. Jiang Xueqin uses game theory (博弈论) not as abstract math but as a lens for reading the real world — dating markets, immigration policy, geopolitics, revolution, finance. Each episode applies the same three-part framework to a different domain. This course gives you a structured daily guide for each episode so you watch with intention, absorb the key ideas, and practice applying them.

---

## How to Use This Course

**Recommended sequence for each day:**

1. **Read the daily guide first** (5 min) — prime your brain with the concepts and questions before watching
2. **Watch the episode** (variable, typically 20–40 min) — you'll notice more because you know what to look for
3. **Return to the guide** (5 min) — check off your learning objectives, answer the reflection questions, note your own examples

If you're short on time, read the guide without watching the episode — the guides are written to stand alone. Watch the episode when you have a longer block.

## How Professor Jiang Thinks

Prof. Jiang states his method explicitly in Episode 1. Every situation is a game with three components:

### 1. Players
Who are the actual participants? Not who is nominally in charge, but who has real power to move the game. In the school game, it's parents — not students or governments. In the immigration game, it's employers and voting blocs. Identifying the real players often overturns the obvious answer.

### 2. Rules
What are the constraints — the boundary conditions of the game? These are the things players cannot change unilaterally: laws, geography, demographics, economics, history. Rules define what moves are even possible. When you misread the rules, your strategy fails even if your analysis of players and incentives is correct.

### 3. Incentives
How do players win? What do they actually get from the game? Prof. Jiang's core premise: *"People are lazy and greedy — they want the best result with the least work."* What players say they want (learning, democracy, fairness) almost never matches what they actually optimize for. Map the real payoffs, not the stated ones.

> *"If you figure out who the players are, if you understand the rules, you understand the incentives — then you understand how the game works and therefore you can predict how the game will turn out."*
> — Prof. Jiang, Episode 1

### Applying the Framework

For any situation, ask:
1. **Who are the real players** — ranked by actual power, not formal title?
2. **What are the rules** — what constraints can't be changed, and what do they force players to do?
3. **What are the real incentives** — what does each player actually benefit from, regardless of what they say?

## Key Concepts Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Game theory (博弈论) | The study of strategic decision-making where your outcome depends on others' choices |
| Nash equilibrium (纳什均衡) | A stable state where no player can improve their outcome by changing strategy alone |
| Prisoner's dilemma (囚徒困境) | A game where individual rational choices produce collectively bad outcomes |
| Signaling (信号) | Using costly actions to credibly communicate information |
| Signaling theory (信号理论) | The idea that many social behaviors exist to communicate quality, not to produce value directly |
| Commitment problem (承诺问题) | The difficulty of making promises credible when you can always defect later |
| Coordination game (协调博弈) | A game where players benefit from matching each other's strategies |
| Security dilemma (安全困境) | Arming for defense that appears offensive to neighbors, triggering an arms race |
| Credential race (证书竞争) | Arms race in qualifications where everyone upgrades but relative positions stay the same |
| Involution / lying flat (内卷 / 躺平) | Internal competition with diminishing returns; giving up on competing |
| Dominant strategy (优势策略) | A strategy that is best regardless of what others do |
| Zero-sum game (零和博弈) | A game where one player's gain exactly equals another's loss |
| Repeated game (重复博弈) | A game played multiple times, enabling cooperation strategies like tit-for-tat |
| Assortative mating (同质匹配) | Tendency for people to pair with others of similar status/traits |
| Elite overproduction (精英过剩) | Too many ambitious people competing for too few elite positions — a structural instability |
| Network effects (网络效应) | Value that increases as more people join (or leave) a network |
| Positional good (位置性商品) | Something whose value depends on having more of it than others, not on absolute quantity |
| Bandwagon / balance (跟风 / 制衡) | Joining the winning side vs. joining against the dominant power |
| Structure (结构) | Deep, slow-moving forces that constrain what players can do |
| Incentive (激励) | What a player actually benefits from, regardless of stated preferences |
