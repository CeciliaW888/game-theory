# Day 22: Twilight of the Nation-State
**Episode:** #22 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=txgPfnXgzcE | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang argues that the US–Iran war is the first true 21st-century war, and uses it as a lens to trace how warfare has evolved across three eras. His central claim is that each era of warfare reflects what it takes to make an enemy stop fighting — and that answer changed at each transition. In the 18th century you killed soldiers; in the 20th century you destroyed industrial capacity; in the 21st century you turn a population against its own government through economic strangulation, ethnic fragmentation, and infrastructure collapse.

The deeper argument is about the nation-state itself. The French Revolution produced the first citizen armies — people willing to die for liberty — which drove an unprecedented population boom, because states needed as many soldiers and workers as possible and invested in medicine and welfare to grow them. That same logic now has a fatal endpoint: too many people, concentrated wealth, unsustainable debt, and war as the only reset. The episode closes with a student Q&A in which Prof. Jiang identifies what he calls the only game-theoretically viable counter to 21st-century warfare: eschatology.

## Core Concepts

### Three Eras of Warfare

Prof. Jiang organizes the evolution of war around one question: what must you destroy to make an enemy stop fighting?

**Era 1 — Kill soldiers.** For most of history, wars were decided on the battlefield. Defeat the enemy's professional army and you won. The state's fighting capacity was measured by its soldiers.

**Era 2 — Kill civilians.** The 20th-century nation-state changed the math. States could now mobilize millions of citizens, replenishing losses indefinitely. Defeating the army was no longer enough — you had to destroy the state's capacity to produce. The target shifted to the civilian population and industrial base. Prof. Jiang illustrates this with the Allied firebombing campaigns: roughly 40% of Tokyo and Nagoya were destroyed, 60% of Yokohama, and most of Dresden was leveled. Japan and Germany lost not because their armies were defeated in the field, but because their productive capacity was gone.

**Era 3 — Turn the population against the state.** Nuclear weapons and sheer population size make both prior strategies unworkable. You cannot kill enough soldiers and you cannot kill enough civilians. So in the 21st century the goal is to use the population as a weapon against its own government: engineer economic ruin, stoke ethnic discord, destroy infrastructure, and let civilian anger do the work.

### The Nation-State and Population Growth

Prof. Jiang makes a direct causal claim: the explosion from roughly 1–2 billion to 8 billion people was not caused by science and technology. It was caused by the nation-state. The nation-state needed as many soldiers and workers as possible for war, and so it deliberately invested in medicine and sanitation to grow and maintain the population. Science and technology were instruments the nation-state deployed in service of that goal — not independent drivers.

He reads directly from Rousseau's *The Social Contract* to establish this: Rousseau writes that the clearest sign of good government is population growth. A government under which population increases is the best; one under which it shrinks is the worst.

### Three Theories of the Nation-State

**The French theory — social contract (社会契约).** Jean-Jacques Rousseau — Swiss-born but the intellectual architect of the French Revolution — argued that human beings are born free because God gave them freedom. The question is why we surrender that freedom to join society. His answer: we join to become *more* free, not less. From many individual wills emerges what Rousseau calls the general will (一般意志) — not merely a majority vote (which he calls the common will), but the collective expression of what a people could become at their ideal state. This is grounded in Kant's categorical imperative: act according to universality (imagine your action replicated across the universe), respect free will (no one can be compelled), and treat every human being as an end, never merely as a means.

This framework made French citizen-soldiers willing to charge professional armies without military experience. Prof. Jiang's reading from Rousseau's *Social Contract* makes the logic explicit: a slave who surrenders freedom surrenders humanity itself — "there can be no way of compensating someone who gives up everything." Give me liberty or give me death. France became the dominant power in Europe because it could field unlimited citizen armies against mercenary forces whose members simply wanted their paycheck and were not willing to die.

**The German theory — iron and blood (铁与血).** When the nations conquered by France sought a counter-theory, Johann Fichte provided one: not liberty but language, race, and blood are what bind a people and are worth fighting for. Otto von Bismarck unified Germany into the second Reich on this foundation. Prof. Jiang reads Bismarck's famous Iron and Blood speech: "Not through speeches and majority decisions will the great questions of the day be decided — but by iron and blood."

Bismarck also understood that war required healthy workers, not just brave soldiers — hence Germany's early welfare state: health insurance, accident insurance, old age pensions, worker and child protections. The nation-state was organized around war, and war required the whole population. Mussolini made the endpoint explicit: "Our myth is the nation. Our myth is the greatness of the nation. And to this myth we subordinate everything else."

**The American theory — the game.** America, as a multicultural nation of immigrants, could not organize around a single language, race, or Rousseau-style general will. Instead, the nation-state became a game: anyone can play, anyone can get rich through hard work, and the Constitution and government are the game masters. More players make the game richer. After World War II, America spread this game globally through capitalism and consumer society — it generated, for example, the enormous wealth China built over the past 40 years.

But the game has an inherent endpoint. Once it runs long enough, only a few players capture all the wealth and everyone else falls into debt. The system becomes unstable and unsustainable. Prof. Jiang's framing: "the way to reset the game is to destroy it in the first place — have World War III, burn everything down, and start the game over again."

### The 21st-Century War Strategy: Three Components

Prof. Jiang describes what he expects the US to deploy against Iran after the shock-and-awe phase fails — which he argues it already has, because Iran spent 20 years decentralizing its military, leadership, and production into mountains:

1. **Economic strangulation (经济扼杀).** Destroy the target state's ability to generate income. For Iran: blockade oil exports (90% of which go to China via Kharg Island) and seize control of the Strait of Hormuz to eliminate toll revenue.

2. **Ethnic tension and divide-and-conquer.** Position forces near minority-populated regions in northwest and southeast Iran to encourage local populations to rise up, forcing Iran to disperse its forces across multiple simultaneous insurgencies.

3. **Destroy civilian infrastructure.** Target dams, reservoirs, road and rail networks, and power plants strategically — not all at once, but enough to make it difficult for a city like Tehran (10 million people who depend entirely on food transported from the countryside) to feed and power itself. The goal is not to kill civilians directly but to make the population angry enough to turn against the government — the accessible target — rather than against the US military.

Prof. Jiang is explicit: these tactics are war crimes under the Geneva Convention. His argument is that as the US position becomes more desperate, it will abandon those constraints.

### The Color Revolution Playbook

Alongside infrastructure and economic warfare, Prof. Jiang describes the "color revolution playbook": organizing and funding young people in target countries to protest and destabilize governments, controlling social media to foment unrest, and bribing security services not to intervene. He cites the Arab Spring (2010–2012) and discusses Nepal as a recent case, noting that protest signs in English — using American phrases like "OK boomer" — in a country where most people don't speak English signal that the protests are staged for Washington's audience, not local citizens. "They're doing it for their masters in Washington."

### The Only Counter: Eschatology (末世论)

A student asks how Iran can respond. Prof. Jiang's answer is framed explicitly in game-theoretic terms: when your economy is strangled, your infrastructure destroyed, and your population divided, there is only one viable response — **eschatology (末世论)**. Create fanaticism. Tell your people that their suffering is part of God's plan, that martyrdom leads to heaven, and that the courage to die is the only path to victory.

He is clear that most people don't want to do this. But history shows — he uses the Iran-Iraq War of the 1980s as his example — that you only need 10–20% of the population to commit to this ideology to survive. Iranian volunteers with rifles ran at Iraqi tanks (backed by both the US and USSR). Their fearlessness made the Iraqis afraid. They turned the tide of the war; only American use of chemical weapons against them forced them back. His conclusion: expect a global surge in religious extremism and eschatological movements as 21st-century war strategies spread, because eschatology is structurally the only effective counter.

### Population Management as the Endgame

Prof. Jiang ends with a stark framing. 21st-century warfare is ultimately about **population management (人口管理)**. The attacker tries to provoke your population into unrest. The defender responds by managing — which can mean suppressing, starving, or killing — its own population to prevent that unrest. This is what makes AI surveillance states not a choice but an inevitability: the only scalable tool for the level of population control that 21st-century defense requires.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Social contract (社会契约) | Rousseau's theory that individuals voluntarily surrender some freedom to the collective in order to become more free; the philosophical foundation of the French nation-state and its citizen armies |
| General will (一般意志) | For Rousseau, not a mere majority vote ("common will") but the collective expression of what a people could become at their ideal state; what gives the sovereign its legitimacy |
| Categorical imperative (绝对命令) | Kant's three-part highest moral law — act by universal principles, respect free will, treat humans as ends not means — which Rousseau uses to ground the general will |
| Iron and blood (铁与血) | Bismarck's phrase for the German theory of the nation-state: the great questions of history are decided not by speeches and votes but by military force and racial-national solidarity |
| Economic strangulation (经济扼杀) | Prof. Jiang's term for the first component of 21st-century warfare: blocking a state's ability to generate income through blockades, sanctions, and seizure of revenue choke points |
| Color revolution (颜色革命) | Prof. Jiang's term for the US playbook of funding protest movements, controlling social media, and bribing security services to destabilize foreign governments from within |
| Eschatology (末世论) | Prof. Jiang's term for the only game-theoretically viable counter to 21st-century warfare: creating religious fanaticism and the willingness to commit martyrdom so that a population resists despite economic ruin and destroyed infrastructure |
| Population management (人口管理) | Prof. Jiang's term for the defender's response to 21st-century war: controlling, suppressing, or eliminating segments of one's own population to prevent externally-provoked unrest |
| Shock and awe | The US doctrine of rapid, overwhelming force to decapitate leadership and destroy military capacity simultaneously; Prof. Jiang argues it fails against a decentralized, prepared adversary like Iran |
| Operation Popeye | Confirmed US weather warfare during the Vietnam War (1967–1972): cloud seeding to extend the monsoon season and slow Vietnamese resupply; Prof. Jiang cites it as evidence that weather manipulation is an established military tool |

## In Your Own Life

The deepest game-theoretic insight in this episode is not about warfare — it is about what makes people willing to sacrifice. Prof. Jiang's argument about mercenaries versus citizen-soldiers maps directly onto any organization or team.

Mercenaries (people who show up for a paycheck) will not take real risks. Citizens (people who believe in the mission) will charge tanks. The professional armies of Prussia and Austria lost to French revolutionary forces not because of strategy or numbers but because their soldiers wanted to survive and collect their pay, while the French were willing to die. The question this raises for your own work: what is the actual incentive structure you are operating in?

If the people around you — including, possibly, yourself — are mercenaries, no amount of strategy will produce commitment under real pressure. Rousseau's point, filtered through Prof. Jiang, is that people fight to the death only when they believe their freedom or meaning is genuinely at stake. If you are building a team, a company, or a movement, the question is not "how do I pay people enough?" but "what is the general will — the vision of who we could become — that makes people willing to risk something real?"

## Synthesis

**Eschatology as a commitment device.** Prof. Jiang's argument that eschatology is the only viable counter to 21st-century economic warfare connects to a classical strategic insight. In Sun Zi's *Art of War* (孙子兵法), there is the concept of 置之死地而后生 — "place soldiers in a position of certain death and they will live." Remove the exit option, and fighting becomes the only rational choice. A force with no retreat is more dangerous than one with one, because it cannot be threatened. Iran's 1980s martyrdom doctrine worked for exactly this reason: the willingness to die was real and visible, which made the threat credible and made the adversary afraid.

The practical implication is double-edged. For the side deploying eschatology, perceived and genuine desperation is a strategic asset — but only when the commitment is authentic, not performed. For the side facing it, the standard toolkit of threats and incentives stops working, because the opponent has already discounted survival as a goal. Prof. Jiang's prediction that eschatological movements will rise globally as 21st-century warfare spreads is therefore also a game-theoretic prediction: wherever economic strangulation is deployed as a weapon, it generates its own counter-force.

**Rousseau and the nation-state as a population technology.** Thucydides, writing about Athens and Sparta, observed that what distinguishes a durable power from a momentary one is the ability to mobilize the commitment of its own people. The French Revolution was the most radical modern answer to that problem: make the state an expression of the people's freedom, and the people will die for it. Prof. Jiang's account shows that this was not idealism — it was a military technology. The nation-state was a machine for producing soldiers willing to die, and everything else (medicine, welfare, sanitation) was infrastructure for that machine.

Prof. Jiang argues that the nation-state — not technology — caused the explosion from 1–2 billion to 8 billion people, because states needed large populations for war and invested in medicine and welfare accordingly.
