# Day 25: Trump Visits China
**Episode:** #25 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BIl5vJn6ohI | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang records this episode in real time as Trump arrives in Beijing for a state visit — the first by a US president in nine years. Rather than reacting to headlines, he uses the occasion to demonstrate the core method of the entire series: ignore the theater, identify the structural incentives, and predict the outcome. The episode weaves together three threads. First, he walks through the events of the preceding month to show that what looked like escalation in the US-China trade war was in fact positioning before a deal. Second, he offers his theory of how the global economy actually works — a layered system of mutually reinforcing "hallucinations" rooted in the 1971 Nixon shock. Third, he uses that theory to predict the specific contours of the US-China grand bargain being negotiated at that moment, including what each side wants and why each side will agree.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Reading the Delegation, Not the Headlines

Prof. Jiang's first analytical move is to look at *who came with Trump* rather than at diplomatic statements. The presence of Elon Musk, Tim Cook, Jensen Huang (added last-minute mid-flight), and the heads of Blackstone, BlackRock, Goldman Sachs, Citigroup, Mastercard, and Visa — companies collectively worth over $12 trillion — is the signal. His reasoning: people this busy and this powerful do not gather in one room unless a deal is already substantially agreed upon. The announcement is not the event; the delegation composition reveals what has already been decided behind the scenes.

### 2. The Preceding Month as Positioning, Not Escalation

Prof. Jiang walks through a sequence of events — China blocking a US acquisition of an AI company, the Chinese blocking order against US sanctions compliance, Nvidia chip restrictions, the Iranian foreign minister visiting Beijing, US-China war games in the Philippines — and argues that each appears to be escalation but is in fact theater: leverage being established before a negotiation concludes. His key interpretive principle: when you understand structural incentives, surface friction stops being informative. What matters is the direction of structural forces.

### 3. The Hallucination Theory of the Global Economy

This is the theoretical centerpiece of the episode. Prof. Jiang argues that after 1971 — when Nixon removed the dollar from the gold standard — the US needed to manufacture demand for a currency that was now backed by nothing. It did this by creating two new systems:

- **The petrodollar:** Gulf states sell oil exclusively in US dollars, anchoring global demand for the currency.
- **China as manufacturing base:** China manufactures goods for the world and prices them in US dollars, creating a second massive source of demand.

Prof. Jiang frames both as *hallucinations* — collectively maintained fictions whose value depends entirely on continued collective belief. China, in his account, is a "hallucination of a hallucination": it exists as a wealthy economy only because the dollar system made it possible. This creates three structural weaknesses for China: (1) no genuine creativity, because a shadow of a shadow has no independent foundation; (2) wealth extraction, because Chinese holders of 人民币 will always prefer dollars when they can access them; and (3) fragility, because the instability of the underlying dollar system is amplified in China.

He uses Plato's allegory of the cave explicitly: the financial elite (including the Bank for International Settlements, the Federal Reserve, Wall Street, and the City of London) cast the shadows; institutions like the UN, WTO, and World Bank play the role of apparently neutral arbiters; and media, education, and culture enforce belief in the system's reality.

### 4. Why the Trade War Started — and Why It Is Ending

When China joined the WTO in 1999, it agreed to two conditions: protect US intellectual property, and open its financial sector (with the goal of 人民币/US dollar convertibility). China did neither fully. Failure on IP produced Huawei overtaking Apple. Failure on financial openness was rational self-preservation: a fully open capital account would cause immediate capital flight, collapsing the domestic banking system. The trade war from 2018 onward was the US attempting to enforce these conditions. Trump's visit, in Prof. Jiang's reading, signals that China has now agreed — under structural duress — to open its financial sector in a new way.

### 5. Stable Coins and Financial Repression: The Mechanism of the Deal

The specific mechanism Prof. Jiang identifies for "opening" China's financial sector without triggering capital flight is **stable coins**. He explains the underlying logic in three steps:

1. The US has $39 trillion in debt, costing roughly $2 trillion per year in interest at 5%. This is unsustainable.
2. The solution — **financial repression** — is to drop the interest rate on US treasuries toward zero, letting inflation erode the real value of the debt over decades.
3. But at 0%, no one voluntarily buys treasuries. So you *force* people to hold them by passing laws (he names the Genius Act and the Clarity Act) that require stable coins to be backed by US treasuries. Stable coins can be sold to Chinese consumers without requiring formal 人民币/dollar convertibility, circumventing the closed capital account.

The business executives accompanying Trump — BlackRock, Blackstone, Visa, Mastercard — are present precisely because this is the product they are selling: financial instruments that allow Chinese savers (who currently hold 40% of income in cash, the world's highest savings rate, in largely idle bank deposits) to buy into the dollar-denominated financial system through digital currency.

### 6. What Each Side Gets in the Grand Bargain

Prof. Jiang summarizes the exchange:

**China wants:**
- Access to cheap, stable energy from the Western Hemisphere (with the US now controlling Venezuela, China prefers one reliable counterparty over many corrupt ones)
- Nvidia chips to fuel AI development
- Continued access to US consumer markets

**The US wants:**
- Access to Chinese financial markets and savings (to finance the debt via stable coins)
- Influence over China's AI development (China as a testing ground for surveillance technology that cannot be deployed domestically)
- Chinese manufacturing, both to continue supporting dollar demand and to bring into the Americas (particularly Venezuela's resource base)

### 7. Taiwan as a Bargaining Chip

Prof. Jiang argues that Taiwan is strategically expendable for the US — and that Trump declaring support for reunification would in fact be strategically rational. His reasoning: if Taiwan returns to China, it creates a new maritime bottleneck that splits Southeast Asia into two zones, cutting off Japan and South Korea from energy supply routes through the Strait of Malacca. This transfers the Taiwan problem from Washington to Tokyo and Seoul. Japan and South Korea would then have every incentive to resist reunification — removing the burden from the US while still containing China's naval expansion. He notes this would be a radical departure from decades of "strategic ambiguity" (美国的战略模糊).

### 8. Jensen Huang as a Signal in Real Time

Prof. Jiang gives a specific case study in reading business negotiation signals. Jensen Huang was initially announced as *not* coming to China — a deliberate withholding of something China wants (Nvidia chips). Then, as Trump's plane refueled in Alaska, Huang was added to the delegation at the last minute. Prof. Jiang interprets this as the negotiating sequence: impasse in South Korea (Bessant and He Lifeng), then a breakthrough, then Huang's addition as a visible "sign of goodwill." The move was not diplomatic — it was business negotiation technique, made legible only if you understand that the entire visit was an economic negotiation led by the Treasury Secretary (Scott Bessant) rather than by the Secretary of State.

## In Your Own Life

**Synthesis**

Prof. Jiang's episode offers two analytical habits worth practicing directly.

**Read the delegation, not the statement.** In any negotiation — a job offer, a partnership discussion, a family dispute — look at who is actually in the room and what their presence costs them. Expensive signals (sending a senior executive, flying someone across the country, clearing a full calendar for a meeting) reveal the true priority. Cheap signals (press releases, official statements, expressed enthusiasm) are not evidence of commitment. Before your next important meeting, ask: who came, and what did it cost them to be here?

**Distinguish theater from structure.** When a negotiation looks like it's collapsing — angry statements, walkouts, escalating demands — check whether the structural incentives still point toward a deal. If both parties need the outcome, the conflict is probably positioning. If one party has a structural exit option, the conflict may be real. Prof. Jiang's move throughout this episode is to bracket the noise (sanctions, war games, chip bans) and ask: given their structural dependencies, what *must* these players do? Apply this to any high-stakes situation you are navigating.

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang argues that the US-China trade war was ultimately caused by China failing to meet two WTO accession conditions — IP protection and financial openness. Given China's structural logic (opening the capital account would cause collapse), was there any path where China could have complied? Or was the conflict inevitable from 1999?

2. The "hallucination of a hallucination" framing implies Chinese economic prosperity is less real than American prosperity. What would falsify this claim? What evidence would show that China has genuine independent economic foundations?

3. Prof. Jiang says China doesn't care who controls Venezuela "as long as stability and cost are controlled" — and explicitly sets aside morality and sovereignty. Is this actually how states behave, or does the framing omit something? What game-theoretic concept might capture what he is leaving out?

4. The stable coin mechanism (forcing Chinese savings into US-treasury-backed digital currencies via the Genius Act) is presented as a solution to the US debt problem. What are the conditions under which this could fail? Who are the players who might defect from this system?

5. Prof. Jiang says this episode is speculation and asks viewers to verify his predictions. This is a methodological commitment, not a hedge. What would it mean to genuinely hold your predictions to this standard in your own reasoning?
