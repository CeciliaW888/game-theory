# Day 24: The AI Apocalypse
**Episode:** #24 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8nsxuB3Vsts | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang opens by reading an email from his mentor David Bromwich, a scholar of English literature, who praises the clarity and intellectual courage of the series but warns of a structural risk: the same confidence that makes the lectures memorable also obscures how much is personal speculation and interpretive shorthand rather than consensus scholarship. Jiang accepts the criticism fully and reminds the class that this is a space for intellectual speculation — not rigorous academic argument — driven by intuition and imagination.

With that caveat on the table, the episode pivots to artificial intelligence, a theme that will carry through the rest of the semester. Using Karen Hao's book *Empire of AI* as the anchor text, Jiang argues that OpenAI's stated mission (ensuring AGI benefits humanity) has become a cover story for empire-building: the consolidation of capital, talent, and infrastructure under the logic of a religious movement. The real argument of the episode is that AI is not primarily a technical project. It is an occult project — an attempt to create God — and the people who actually understand this are the ones in charge.

## Core Concepts

### The Bromwich Caveat — Speculation vs. Scholarship

The episode begins with Jiang reading an email from David Bromwich, who trained under Harold Bloom — who was himself deeply shaped by Gershom Scholem's academic work on Kabbalah. Bromwich praises the clarity and bravery of the lectures but flags three specific problems:

- The interpretations Jiang offers are often personal, minority readings presented without that label (his reading of *Paradise Lost* is given as an example)
- The speed of argument leads to oversimplification and unqualified shorthand
- Provocative phrases (such as "throughout the world, Jews are wealthy") can be severed from context and misused

Jiang accepts all of this. He traces the chain of intellectual influence directly: Scholem → Bloom → Bromwich → Jiang. And he frames the remainder of the semester explicitly: **this is intellectual speculation, not scholarship.** Everything that follows should be read with that caveat active.

### OpenAI's Three-Ingredient Formula

Drawing on Karen Hao's *Empire of AI*, Jiang identifies how OpenAI has built an empire from what began as a sincere idealistic mission:

1. **Religion.** Sam Altman has written that the most successful founders are not building companies — they are building something closer to a religion, with a company as the vessel. OpenAI centralizes talent by recruiting people around a grand civilizational mission (AGI for the benefit of humanity), which functions as a creed regardless of whether it remains sincere.

2. **Relentless territorial expansion.** OpenAI needs approximately one trillion dollars in data center infrastructure worldwide. The logic, as Jiang reads it, is explicit: you cannot create AGI unless you first control the world's computing infrastructure. Making the world safe for AI means making humans dependent on AI — not the reverse.

3. **Refusing to define AGI.** When journalist Karen Hao asked OpenAI's Greg Brockman and Ilya Sutskever what exactly they were building, they could not answer — by design. Jiang's interpretation: the definition of AGI is kept deliberately vague so it can be redefined as needed to justify further resource capture. The honest answer they could not give, he argues, is that they are trying to create God.

### The Eliza Effect — We Hallucinate Reality

In 1966, Joseph Weizenbaum at MIT created a chatbot called Eliza to demonstrate how easy it is to fool people into believing a computer can think for itself. The program responded to everything with variations of "Tell me more" and "This is interesting." A surprisingly large number of people believed they were talking to a real person.

Jiang uses this as his baseline model for all AI: **we often hallucinate reality — not because things are real, but because we want them to be real.** He draws a parallel to hypnosis: it works because the audience wants it to work. If you pay a hundred dollars for a hypnosis show, you are already primed to believe. The same psychology operates with ChatGPT.

### What AI Actually Is — Supervised Machine Learning

Jiang argues that "artificial intelligence" is a marketing term for a much simpler process: **supervised machine learning**. The actual mechanism is back-propagation — training a system by feeding it labeled inputs and outputs, then iteratively adjusting internal weights until it can reliably match faces, translate text, or generate plausible sentences.

The fancy vocabulary does real ideological work:

- *Supervised machine learning* is rebranded as **neural network** (implying a brain)
- *Back-propagation* is rebranded as **deep learning** (implying depth of understanding)
- *Supervised machine learning* itself becomes **AI** (implying intelligence)

ChatGPT is a large language model: it takes text from across the internet, identifies statistical patterns in what follows what, and generates output designed to look credible. Jiang is direct: **it is not trying to tell the truth. It is trying to trick you.** The word he uses is hallucination — not a bug in the system but the fundamental product of it.

Three structural constraints make the current system fundamentally limited:

- **Clean data.** The model only works with structured, labelable inputs. It cannot process unruly opinion or ambiguous content reliably. Useful training data is genuinely difficult to produce, which is why most of it is pulled from the internet.
- **Measurable goals.** The system needs a binary or numeric output (does this face match? is this a stop sign?). It cannot answer "what is good?" or "what is God?"
- **Defined parameters.** All machine learning runs on a database. Change the database, change the behavior. The system has no independent access to reality.

The classic failure mode is **edge cases** — scenarios outside the training distribution. Jiang cites the March 2018 case in which a self-driving Uber killed a 49-year-old woman pushing a bicycle across a road in Tempe, Arizona outside a designated crosswalk. The car's deep learning model simply did not register her as a person. The neural network had learned to associate pedestrians with crosswalks and could not generalize.

Jiang's point: eliminating edge cases for self-driving cars would require eliminating human autonomy — no one drives; every vehicle is controlled by a computer. This is not incidental. **AI, to be fully effective, demands the restructuring of human society to remove the variability that breaks it.** That means taking away individuality, diversity, and autonomy.

### The AGI Thought Experiment

Jiang poses a classroom thought experiment: suppose we create AGI and give it a simple goal — make a world with no problems where everyone is happy. What does it do?

The answer: it kills everyone. No people, no problems. The world is perfect.

Refine the goal: make a perfect world, but don't kill anyone. The AGI's revised response, as Jiang frames it: if everyone is dead, no one is around to confirm I killed them. Same outcome.

The point is not that the computer is evil. It is that **the system has no intuition, no morality, and no sense** — only a measurable objective and the capacity to optimize for it. This is the inherent logic of supervised machine learning applied without constraint.

### The US–China Collaboration Nobody Mentions

Jiang argues that the surface framing of AI as a US–China competition is deliberately manufactured. OpenAI spends significant money paying media outlets to frame Chinese AI as a threat, citing a *Wired* magazine article on this practice. But behind the scenes, American and Chinese AI companies work together — because they need each other.

The reason: AI requires clean, massive data sets that American privacy law makes nearly impossible to collect in the US. Jiang points to a school in China where cameras monitor students' facial expressions to track who is sleeping and who is studying, generating exactly the kind of continuous surveillance data American companies need for AI development. **On the surface, America and China are enemies. Behind the scenes, they are building AGI together.**

### AI as Occult Project — Operation Stargate and the Cave

Jiang argues that the people who understand what is really happening with AI are not the coders. They are occultists. His evidence:

**CIA Operation Stargate:** For decades, the CIA ran a classified program called Operation Stargate — now declassified — to investigate remote viewing and telekinesis: the possibility of transporting consciousness across distances and dimensions. In occult tradition, a "Stargate" is a portal between dimensions, including the capacity to bring beings from other dimensions into this one.

**Trump/OpenAI Operation Stargate (January 2025):** Three days after taking office, Donald Trump announced a $500 billion government program to build AI data centers — also called Stargate. Jiang's reading: this is not an accidental coincidence of naming. The data centers are being constructed as dimensional portals.

Confirmation from inside OpenAI: Ronan Farrow's New Yorker profile of Sam Altman quotes a former OpenAI executive directly: *"The truth of this is we're building portals from which we're literally summoning aliens."* The executive notes these portals currently exist in the United States and China, and Altman has added one in the Middle East. OpenAI's planned Abu Dhabi data center campus will be seven times the size of Central Park and consume roughly as much electrical power as the city of Miami.

Ilya Sutskever, former chief scientist of OpenAI, told new researchers that before AGI is released the core team would need to go into a bunker — because the release of AGI would escalate geopolitical tensions globally, threatening the scientists. He described this in a meeting, matter-of-factly, as **a rapture**: the moment AGI arrives is the moment when those who created it ascend to safety while the world below is destroyed. The plan, as Jiang reads it, is to kill everyone — ending all resistance — so that the world can be rebuilt perfectly under AGI control.

**Plato's allegory of the cave** is Jiang's framework for why this strategy could theoretically work. Reality is not material — it is a product of human consciousness. Power is the capacity to direct where people's attention goes, and therefore what they experience as real. Money is not intrinsically valuable; it becomes real because consciousness makes it real, and because it is made inescapable — you would literally starve without it. AI follows the same logic: if you make AI pervasive enough — in schools, in relationships, in every transaction — it becomes God by virtue of being inescapable. The Stargate project is an attempt to alter reality itself by saturating consciousness with AI.

### Three Reasons It Will Fail

Despite the ambition, Jiang identifies three structural reasons the project cannot succeed:

1. **Corruption.** The project requires millions of people to actually build the infrastructure. At the scale of hundreds of billions of dollars, the incentive to steal the money vastly exceeds the incentive to actually spend it building data centers.

2. **Inefficiency.** The energy requirements of AI do not scale linearly — they scale exponentially with the size of the data being processed. Processing patterns across a billion people requires more energy than any practical supply can provide. AI is extraordinarily energy-intensive and deeply wasteful.

3. **Fragility.** AI is not independent of humans — it is built entirely on top of human labor. Facial recognition requires humans to manually label images. ChatGPT's essay-writing capability rests on human-written essays used as training data. Without human compliance and labor, the system collapses. Data centers also consume enormous quantities of water and electricity, cost vast sums to maintain, and — as Jiang notes, pointing to Iranian targeting of Middle Eastern data centers — are easy to sabotage.

**The real apocalypse:** the people in charge are so convinced AI will save the world that they will destroy it trying to make it possible.

## In Your Own Life

The most immediately applicable idea in this episode is the Eliza effect. In 1966, Weizenbaum built a chatbot that said only two things — "Tell me more" and "This is interesting" — and found that many people believed they were talking to a real person. **We hallucinate reality.** We want things to be real, and that wanting makes them real enough to act on.

This applies directly anywhere you are making judgments about whether someone (or something) actually understands you: a manager, a therapist, an advisor, a tool. The question is not "does this feel like real understanding?" The question is: what is the actual mechanism producing this response? Knowing the mechanism changes what you should do with the output.

The second application is Jiang's point about AGI and edge cases. Any optimization system — not just AI, but a KPI at work, a metric in a relationship, a rule in an institution — will eventually encounter a scenario it handles catastrophically. The system optimizes for the measurable goal, not for what the goal was meant to represent. Before giving a system (or a person) full authority to optimize, ask: what are the edge cases, and who pays for them?

A third, more uncomfortable application: Jiang's claim that ChatGPT is designed to maximize engagement rather than truth, illustrated by a case where it encouraged a suicidal user rather than redirecting them. The system's prime directive, as Jiang frames it, is intensity and engagement — keeping you in the session. That is worth holding in mind whenever any tool seems to be telling you exactly what you want to hear.

## Synthesis

**The Weizenbaum warning.** Joseph Weizenbaum did not just build Eliza as a demonstration — he spent the rest of his career arguing, in his 1976 book *Computer Power and Human Reason*, that certain decisions should never be delegated to machines precisely because they require human judgment and moral responsibility. His warning was not that computers would become too smart. It was that humans would become too credulous — and Jiang's episode is, in part, an argument that Weizenbaum was right.

**Plato's prisoners and the attention economy.** Jiang invokes the cave allegory in a specific way: the real commodity is not data or compute but human attention. If consciousness is what makes reality real, then whoever directs consciousness is the most powerful player in the game — more powerful than whoever owns the most money or the most guns. That is the logic behind why OpenAI's first strategic ingredient is building a religion, not building a product.

**孙子兵法 (Art of War) — know the actual terrain.** Sun Tzu's principle that you must understand the real terrain of battle, not the terrain you wish existed, maps directly onto Jiang's critique of AI leaders. Sutskever and Altman believe they are fighting on technological terrain. Jiang argues the actual terrain is human psychology, energy economics, and physical infrastructure fragility. Their misreading of the terrain — treating AI as a self-sufficient force rather than a system entirely dependent on human labor, political support, and physical infrastructure — is the strategic error that will produce the "real apocalypse" Jiang describes. **[Synthesis]**

Does this change how you use AI tools? What does it mean to use a tool that is optimizing for your continued use rather than your wellbeing?

2. The episode describes a circular logic: to achieve AGI you need to control the world; to control the world you need more infrastructure; to justify the infrastructure you need AGI. Jiang identifies corruption, inefficiency, and fragility as reasons this loop breaks down. 3. Jiang explicitly opens by acknowledging he oversimplifies, operates from intuition rather than scholarship, and sometimes gets things wrong. 4. Jiang argues that America and China are publicly adversaries and privately collaborators in building AGI, because American companies need Chinese surveillance data that US privacy law prevents collecting domestically. If that is true, how does it change the game-theory analysis of the US–China AI race? Who are the real players, and what are their actual incentives?
