# Day 21: World War Trump
**Episode:** #21 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ts-AA6LQf6I | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang argues that the US–Iran war is not a personal whim of Trump's but the opening move of a long-term American imperial strategy: control the world's key maritime choke points, cut off China's energy and trade routes, and force all major powers to depend on US resources, financing, and weapons. He reads directly from the 2026 US National Defense Strategy to show that this blueprint is explicit and already being executed.

## Learning Objectives
- [ ] Understand why Prof. Jiang treats Trump's war signals as noise and the National Defense Strategy as the actual signal
- [ ] Identify the four strategic pillars in the 2026 NDS and how each maps to current US military moves
- [ ] Explain the logic of controlling choke points as a substitute for territorial conquest
- [ ] Articulate why Prof. Jiang predicts the strategy will ultimately fail despite short-term gains
- [ ] Understand the three domestic conditions Prof. Jiang says the US must meet for the plan to succeed (national draft, continuity of agenda, AI surveillance state)

## Core Concepts

**Ignoring the signal, reading the document.** Prof. Jiang's core methodological move in this episode is to set aside Trump's daily contradictory statements — "the war is going great," then "I may drop more bombs" — and instead read the January 2026 National Defense Strategy (NDS) as the real playbook. He argues this document tells you exactly what will happen over the next 5–20 years.

**The four pillars of the NDS.** Prof. Jiang reads the document aloud with students and annotates each pillar:
1. *Defend the homeland and the Western Hemisphere* — the Western Hemisphere is American territory; foreign powers (China, Russia) can only access it on American terms.
2. *Deter China in the Indo-Pacific through strength, not confrontation* — the goal is not to destroy China but to force it to obey by encircling it along the First Island Chain (Japan, Taiwan, the Philippines) and controlling the Strait of Malacca.
3. *Increase burden-sharing with allies* — allies become vassals who must follow American orders and bear more of the military cost.
4. *Rebuild the US defense industrial base* — move toward a war economy; factories (GM, Ford) produce weapons under the Defense Production Act; Pentagon budget rises from $1 trillion to $1.5 trillion.

**Choke-point strategy.** Prof. Jiang argues the US does not need to occupy territory — it only needs to control key maritime choke points: the Strait of Hormuz (Middle East oil), the Strait of Malacca (80% of China's energy imports), the Panama Canal (Western Hemisphere trade), the Strait of Gibraltar, and access to the Arctic via Greenland. Controlling these lets the US decide which nations get resources.

**The Iran war as China containment.** Iran sits at the center of both Russia's North–South Corridor and China's Belt and Road Initiative. Keeping Iran in permanent chaos denies both Russia and China an overland trade route and keeps the Strait of Hormuz contested, cutting off 20% of global energy from Asian markets.

**The three domestic requirements.** For the strategy to work at home, Prof. Jiang says the US needs: (1) a **national draft** to supply soldiers for policing choke points and suppressing defiant states; (2) **continuity of agenda** — Trump must secure a third term so the plan is not reversed; (3) an **AI surveillance state** (he points to ICE budget growth to $90 billion and Operation Stargate's $500 billion data-center investment) to prevent the kind of domestic anti-war revolt that ended Vietnam.

**Why it will fail anyway.** Prof. Jiang ends with a long-run prediction: nationalism, internal corruption, and political division will cause the plan to backfire and accelerate imperial decline. Nations being used as pawns — Japan, South Korea, Europe — will eventually seek independence. Insurgencies will arise inside an expanded Western Hemisphere. And empire historically consumes itself through corruption of its own institutions. The strategy is, in his words, "a short-term solution that creates long-term problems."

## Key Terms

| Term | Chinese | Meaning |
|------|---------|---------|
| Choke point | 咽喉要道 | Narrow geographic passage that controls movement of ships and trade |
| First Island Chain | 第一島鏈 | String of islands from Japan through Taiwan to the Philippines used to encircle China |
| War economy / Total war | 戰時經濟 | National economy reorganized around weapons production |

| Surveillance state | 監控國家 | State that monitors its own population to suppress dissent |
| Nationalism | 民族主義 | The force Prof. Jiang identifies as the primary long-run threat to imperial control |

## In Your Own Life

**Synthesis** — Prof. Jiang's analytical move here applies to any negotiation or competitive situation: when someone is sending you contradictory signals, stop analyzing the signals and find the written strategy document instead. A company's earnings call is noise; its 10-K is the strategy. A manager's mood in Monday's meeting is noise; their OKR document is the strategy. The habit of asking "what is the actual blueprint, and where is it written down?" cuts through a great deal of manufactured confusion — and confusion is itself often a deliberate tactic by the stronger party.

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang argues that both Republicans and Democrats are supporting the Iran war — one Democrat's strategic vote flip ensured the war-powers resolution failed. What does this tell us about how to model political parties as strategic actors rather than ideological ones?
2. The NDS says "our goal is not to dominate China… but to prevent anyone from dominating us." Prof. Jiang reads this as pure domination dressed in neutral language. How do you distinguish between genuine balance-of-power logic and hegemonic logic in a state's declared strategy?
3. Prof. Jiang predicts the plan fails because of nationalism, corruption, and division. Are these structural features of all empires, or specific vulnerabilities of the contemporary United States? What evidence from the episode supports your view?
