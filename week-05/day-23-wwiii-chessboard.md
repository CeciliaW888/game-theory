# Day 23: The WWIII Chessboard
**Episode:** #23 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6aNh6sBpqvQ | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang opens the final seven-episode arc of the course by introducing what he calls World War III — a conflict he argues is already underway. Rather than surveying recent news events, he maps the structural chessboard: the four major players (United States, Russia, Iran, Israel), the internal forces driving each toward war, and the grand strategy each nation uses to compete. The central claim is that this conflict is not primarily about interstate disputes. It is driven by an internal civil war inside each country between transnational capital and a coalition of nationalists, religious traditionalists, and tech power. The episode gives each nation a chess-set metaphor — king (political system), queen (grand strategy), bishops/knights/rooks (attack vectors), pawns (sacrificial tools) — and uses literary texts to explain the civilizational worldview underpinning each player's strategy.

## Core Concepts

### The Internal Engine of WWIII: Transnational Capital vs. Three Challengers

Prof. Jiang's first major move is to reframe what is driving this conflict. The commonly assumed cause — great-power rivalry between nation-states — is secondary. The primary driver is an internal civil war playing out simultaneously inside each of the four nations.

The current rulers of the global order are **transnational capital**, represented by Wall Street and the City of London. They are secular, multicultural, and globalist — because those conditions allow capital to move freely and maintain dominance. Three forces are rising to challenge them:

1. **Nationalism** — the belief that globalism damages the nation and its people. Trump and Putin are the named champions.
2. **Religion/Orthodoxy** — opposition to capital's secular worldview. Capital treats money as God; religion offers an alternative organizing principle.
3. **Artificial intelligence** — AI threatens to replace money as the force that structures society, so it is structurally opposed to financial capital's dominance.

In the United States, Prof. Jiang argues that all three of these forces have converged behind Trump: the tech lords (Ellison, Musk, Thiel), nationalist politics, and religious conservative constituencies form a single anti-capital coalition. This coalition's goal is to displace transnational capital and reorganize society around a different logic.

### The Two Environmental Forces

Alongside the internal civil war, Prof. Jiang identifies two external drivers that push nations toward conflict regardless of who wins domestically:

- **Elite overproduction**: too many people competing to join the elite class, creating structural internal conflict in every society
- **Environmental/ecological collapse**: approaching cycles of extreme weather, food scarcity, and resource competition — a return to conditions in which famine is common and nations must fight for survival inputs

### Why Only Four Players? The Grand Strategy Filter

Prof. Jiang makes a specific argument about why China, India, Europe, Japan, and South Korea are secondary or spectator players rather than primary agents, despite their economic size or military capacity. The distinguishing criterion is the **grand strategy** — a coherent vision of geopolitics and a plan to dominate it that can mobilize a population for total war.

China is explicitly called out. Prof. Jiang argues that China's cultural identity — 中国 (Middle Kingdom) — is fundamentally isolationist. The worldview is: "We are a universe unto ourselves; others may pay tribute to our cultural superiority, but we don't seek to dominate the outside world." He acknowledges China's recent decades of globalization but calls it a historical aberration; in his reading, China will return to isolationism over the long run. India follows similar logic. Neither has a grand strategy in the relevant sense.

The four nations that do have grand strategies are the ones capable of driving history.

### The Chess-Set Framework

Prof. Jiang introduces a chess metaphor to structure how each nation-state operates:

| Piece | What It Represents |
|-------|-------------------|
| **King** | The political system — the target to put in check |
| **Queen** | The grand strategy — the master vision for geopolitical dominance |
| **Bishop / Knight / Rook** | Attack vectors — tools and mechanisms for executing the strategy |
| **Pawns** | Sacrificial instruments — actors or assets that can be discarded to advance the strategy |

The key insight from this framework: **you win a war not by destroying the enemy's military but by putting the king in check — attacking the political system itself**. Military victory is instrumental, not terminal.

### The United States: Democracy, the Technate, and the Faustian Tradition

**Political system (King):** Liberal democracy. Its strength is openness, innovation, and creative competition. Its vulnerability is **polarization**: if political parties reach a point where they hate each other more than they fear any foreign adversary, the system paralyzes itself. Prof. Jiang notes that Trump currently hates domestic Democrats more than he hates Russia, China, or Iran — which is exactly the dynamic an adversary would want to exploit. The way to put America in check is to deepen domestic polarization until civil war becomes the primary political reality.

**Grand strategy (Queen):** Greater North America / the **Technate**. North America as a self-sufficient continental fortress — sufficient resources, no vulnerability to invasion — combined with a transition from volatile democracy to an AI-governed technocracy (an AI surveillance state). Once internal cohesion is achieved via the technate, America can generate chaos everywhere else, force the rest of the world to buy American weapons and resources, and eliminate the $39 trillion debt. The tech figures implementing this (Ellison, Musk, Thiel) are named as the agents of the technate project.

**Attack vectors (Pieces):** Technology and military supremacy (GPS, precision weapons, best air force), propaganda (control of global media narrative via outlets like the New York Times and CNN), and the US dollar as global reserve currency providing unlimited financing.

**Pawns:** Allied vassal states — they can be sacrificed when convenient.

**Civilizational text:** Prof. Jiang reads from *Paradise Lost* (John Milton). Satan's argument to Eve — that God forbids the tree of knowledge only to maintain power over humans, that ambition and rebellion against a tyrant are honorable — is presented as the DNA of Anglo-American civilization. The Faustian drive for individual achievement, the celebration of pride, the insatiable appetite for godhood: this is what unifies Milton, Shakespeare's Hamlet, Goethe's Faust, and Ayn Rand's *The Fountainhead*. Anglo-American strategy flows from this: achievement, dominance, and the belief that humans can become gods through will and intellect.

### Russia: Autocracy, the Third Rome, and Dostoevsky

**Political system (King):** Autocracy. Strength: long-term planning and strategic direction with one person in charge. Vulnerability: succession crisis. The American strategy against Russia is not military defeat (impossible — Russia has nuclear weapons and the world's best land army). It is to make Putin so politically illegitimate that the elite turn on him and a succession crisis destroys the state from within.

**Grand strategy (Queen):** The **Third Rome** strategy. Rome (first) → Constantinople (second) → Moscow (third) — and there shall be no fourth. Russia sees itself as the heir to the Roman Empire, charged with unifying all religious traditions to defeat the anti-Christ system — which is Western liberal, individualist, consumer democracy. The strategy involves pulling Greek Orthodoxy, the Catholic Church, Israel (as a religious tradition), Iran, and potentially Hindu India into a unified front against Anglo-America.

**Attack vectors (Pieces):** Orthodox religion, the geography of Eurasia (Russia spans the entire landmass), and superior conventional land warfare (artillery, ground forces as demonstrated in Ukraine).

**Pawns:** Soldiers. Russia is willing to absorb massive casualty rates to achieve strategic objectives.

**Civilizational texts:** Virgil's *Aeneid* (duty over glory), St. Augustine's *Confessions* (pride is sin, not virtue — it moves you away from God toward Satan), Dostoevsky's *Crime and Punishment* (the protagonist who tries to transcend morality and become a superhuman by committing murder is ultimately redeemed only by submitting to love and to God), and Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*. Prof. Jiang reads from *Anna Karenina* at length: Anna's love for Vronsky is not love but a will-to-control — she wants him to surrender completely to her. Her failure and suicide illustrate Tolstoy's argument that the human desire to become God — to possess another absolutely — produces only tyranny and self-destruction. Levin, the contrasting figure, finds genuine happiness by submitting to duty and tradition rather than seeking passionate self-completion.

The core Russian civilizational argument: the American/Miltonic view of Eve's disobedience as progress is wrong. Eve fell because of pride — the failure to recognize human limitation. Perfection and godhood are impossible for humans. What is good is to recognize our fallibility and submit to God. This is why the conflict between America and Russia is not just geopolitical but civilizational — they hold opposite views on the story of Adam and Eve, and each is fighting to impose its version of reality on the world.

### Iran: Theocracy, Shia Exceptionalism, and Martyrdom

**Political system (King):** Theocracy. Strength: social cohesion under existential pressure — the Iranian people remain united despite the destruction of infrastructure. Vulnerability: extremism. If the most radical factions gain dominance they can alienate the broader population.

**Grand strategy (Queen):** **Shia exceptionalism**. Shia Islam holds that only a blood descendant of the Prophet Muhammad can legitimately lead the Muslim world as caliph. The Sunni majority (centered in Saudi Arabia) rejects this. Iran's goal is to topple Saudi Arabia and become the center and leader of the entire Muslim world — a destiny it sees as ordained.

**Attack vectors (Pieces):** Terrain, faith, and proxies (Houthis, Hezbollah, Hamas) plus drones and missiles as the primary tactical instruments.

**Pawns:** Drones and missiles.

**Civilizational foundations:** Two traditions fuse in Iranian strategic culture:
1. **Zoroastrianism** — the world's first eschatological religion. History ends at a judgment day; those who lived truth (Asha) ascend; those who lived the lie descend. This makes earthly defeat irrelevant — what matters is fidelity to truth and God.
2. **Shia martyrdom** — rooted in the Battle of Karbala, where Hussein (grandson of the Prophet) fought to the death against an overwhelming force rather than submit to an illegitimate caliph. To fight to the death for one's faith is not failure; it is the highest act.

The strategic consequence: Iran will not surrender regardless of military or economic damage. The Americans and Israelis may have superior weapons, money, and soldiers. It does not matter. The frame is eschatological, not tactical.

### Israel: Democracy + Theocracy, the Greater Israel Project, and the Kabbalah

**Political system (King):** Both democracy and theocracy simultaneously — a fractured combination that makes it highly vulnerable to internal civil war. Prof. Jiang notes it is "not that hard" to push Israel into one because Israeli society is already deeply divided.

**Grand strategy (Queen):** The **Greater Israel Project**. The Biblical promise to Abraham covers territory stretching from the Nile to the Euphrates — encompassing all of present-day Syria, Jordan, Lebanon, parts of Turkey, Iraq, and Saudi Arabia. Achieving this is Israel's stated civilizational destiny.

**Attack vectors (Pieces):** The Bible (many Christians worldwide believe Israel has a God-given right to the land), the Mossad (described as the world's most effective intelligence agency, skilled at infiltration, co-option, bribery, and blackmail of foreign elites), and the global Jewish diaspora (financing and intelligence recruitment). False flag operations — creating conflict between other parties and attributing it to enemies — are named as a specific Mossad instrument. Prof. Jiang argues Israel benefits from maximum chaos in the Middle East because it advances territorial objectives.

**Pawns:** Everyone else — other nations, groups, and actors manipulated through Mossad operations.

**Civilizational foundation — the Kabbalah:** Prof. Jiang introduces the Kabbalistic "tree of life" as the philosophical underpinning. Three claims from this tradition matter for strategy:
1. Only two things matter — the Creator and the Created. Israel does not concern itself with world opinion; only its relationship with God counts.
2. The universe operates on the logic of thesis → antithesis → synthesis. This is the fractal that underpins all existence.
3. **Sin is necessary for redemption.** One cannot skip from thesis to synthesis without passing through antithesis — through evil. Committing sin provokes divine punishment, which provokes repentance, which produces redemption. You cannot arrest this process; you can only accelerate it. This places Israel's Kabbalistic tradition in explicit opposition to the Russian "katechon" tradition (the idea that the role of the righteous is to restrain evil and hold back the antichrist). For Kabbalah, restraining evil is theologically wrong — it delays redemption.

The strategic implication Prof. Jiang draws: Israel's behavior in the Middle East — what others perceive as disproportionate or self-destructive violence — makes sense within this frame. World opinion is irrelevant (only God's judgment matters). Evil acts are not a departure from the path to God but part of the necessary process. The goal is to accelerate the cycle so that Israel is ultimately humbled, repents, and is redeemed by God at the end of days.

### The Three Active Battlefronts

Prof. Jiang identifies three current theaters:
1. **Ukraine** — NATO/US vs. Russia proxy war
2. **Iran** — US and Israel attacking Iranian infrastructure and attempting to force surrender
3. **Cuba** — a less-discussed front where the US is maintaining a blockade/embargo and attempting societal collapse, with Russia working to sustain the Cuban state

He anticipates more fronts will open, including conflict over **ocean trade routes** as the US continues seizing Russian shadow-fleet tankers and Russia eventually arms its ships in response.

### The Emerging Proxy Architecture

To fight Russia and China, the US is re-arming historical proxies: **Germany** (historically Europe's dominant military power, defeated in WWII) to counter Russia; **Japan** (historically East Asia's dominant military power, defeated in WWII) to counter China; **Israel** to counter Iran. Opportunistic powers — **North Korea, Poland, Turkey** — will try to exploit the chaos to accumulate power for themselves.

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's chess-set framework — king, queen, attack vectors, pawns — is directly applicable to competitive situations well below the geopolitical level. Consider any competition you are currently in: a job market, a business rivalry, a negotiation, an internal office conflict. Most people focus on tactical tools (the rooks and bishops — skills, resources, arguments). Prof. Jiang's insight is that **the real game is about the king: the underlying system of legitimacy and decision-making**. If you can change how decisions get made, or undermine the legitimacy of how your opponent is perceived, you win at a deeper level than any tactical exchange allows.

**Synthesis:** The same logic applies to persuasion. Arguing against someone's specific position (attacking their pawns) is far less effective than understanding what worldview — what "grand strategy" — gives that position its coherence. People do not change their minds when you defeat their arguments; they change their minds when the underlying civilizational logic shifts. This is why Prof. Jiang spends so much of this episode on literature: Milton, Tolstoy, and the Kabbalah are not decorative — they are the queen pieces. To understand how a player will behave under pressure, you need to understand what story they are living inside.

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang argues that the US–Russia conflict is not merely geopolitical but civilizational — two opposed readings of the story of Adam and Eve (pride as virtue vs. pride as sin). If that is right, what would "winning" actually look like for either side? Is there a conceivable end state, or is this a conflict that can only be managed, not resolved?

2. The Kabbalistic argument — that sin is *necessary* for redemption, and that accelerating the cycle of evil is theologically correct — is Prof. Jiang's explanation for Israeli behavior in the Middle East. Apply the same logical structure to another domain: can you think of a personal, organizational, or historical situation where someone (or an institution) operated on the belief that things must get worse before they can get better, and that deliberately making them worse was therefore justified? What were the consequences?

3. Prof. Jiang's filter for "primary WWIII player" is the grand strategy — a coherent civilizational vision capable of mobilizing a population for total war. By that standard, does any non-state actor (a tech company, a religion, a transnational movement) qualify? What would it mean for the chessboard analysis if the relevant players are not nation-states at all?
