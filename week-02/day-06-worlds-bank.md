# Day 6: The World's Bank
**Episode:** #6 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CbamEcNuDXo | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang opens with a puzzle: why do the brightest Chinese students spend their energy learning English, chasing US dollars, and dreaming of immigrating to the West — even though, as he argues, they would have much higher status and better prospects staying in China? He uses this behavioral puzzle to reveal a game that was deliberately designed, first by the British Empire and now inherited by the United States. The episode traces how Britain built the world's first financial empire — not through territory alone, but through three interlocking tools: finance (the Bank of England and offshore money laundering), schooling (English-language indoctrination of local elites), and naval power. The "world's bank" of the title is Britain itself — the original architect of a global system that still runs today, now with US dollars at its center.

## Core Concepts

### The Opening Puzzle: Strange Behavior, Rational Game

Prof. Jiang opens with three things Chinese students do that, on the surface, look irrational:

1. They spend more time learning English than Chinese
2. They are obsessed with earning US dollars
3. Their life goal is to immigrate to the United States or Britain

His argument: if you think about it strategically, these are bad moves. Given their family connections and status in China, they would be much better off mastering Chinese and climbing the Chinese hierarchy. In the United States, "the game is rigged against you" — the most they can hope for is engineer or professor; they will not gain political power or become the CEO of a major company. So why do they play this game?

His answer: **because the British designed the game that way.** Understanding why these students behave as they do requires understanding how the game was constructed. This is the core game-theory move of the episode — behavior that looks irrational is actually a rational response to the incentives built into the system.

### The Spanish Empire and the Logic of Hubris

Prof. Jiang starts the historical explanation with Spain. The Spanish Empire was built on silver extracted from the Americas (from the Aztec and Inca civilizations). This sudden wealth made Spain powerful — but it also made the Spanish lazy, insular, and arrogant. They subcontracted their labor (to Britain, France, and the Dutch Republic for textiles and manufacturing), their trade (to the Dutch for voyages to the East Indies), and eventually became corrupt and started too many wars. Spain went bankrupt.

Meanwhile, the English and Dutch — who were poor and had to work hard — became energetic, open, and cohesive. Prof. Jiang frames this as a pattern: **wealth creates hubris, hubris creates overextension, overextension creates collapse.** This is the same trajectory he will later apply to the modern West.

One British tactic during this period: state-sponsored piracy. Sir Francis Drake, working directly under Queen Elizabeth I, stole Spanish silver shipments. Britain grew wealthy through piracy before it grew wealthy through empire.

### The Glorious Revolution: The Game Is Set

The pivotal moment in the episode is the **Glorious Revolution of 1688**. The British nobility invited William of Orange — leader of the Dutch Republic — to become the monarch of Britain. The official reason was religion (the existing king was Catholic; Britain was Protestant). The real reason, Prof. Jiang argues: the nobility wanted to protect capital.

Here is the actual deal: almost all Dutch wealth was transferred to England. In exchange, Parliament became the sovereign power (not the king), and the king agreed to be a figurehead. This created the concept of national sovereignty as a protection for capital.

The key innovation was the **Bank of England (1694)**. Before this, if you lent money to a king, three things could go wrong: the king could lose the war, the king could die, or the king could simply refuse to repay. There was no recourse. The Bank of England solved this by shifting the borrower from the king to the nation. Now you are lending to the nation — which means the people are always liable for the debt, even if the king dies. Creditors could feel safe.

England was also physically safe: as an island, it was protected by the Royal Navy, which no European power could breach.

### The Three Tools of the British Financial Empire

Prof. Jiang names three mechanisms that allowed Britain to extract wealth from India, China, and the rest of the world — and, crucially, to get local elites to voluntarily cooperate with that extraction:

**1. Finance (money laundering and offshore financial centers)**

Britain developed contract law and private property protections — explicitly, the legal system did not ask where money came from. If you were a local elite in India or China who had stolen from your own people, Britain would protect your capital, "clean it up" through its legal and financial system, and help you invest it abroad. This is what Prof. Jiang calls money laundering. The offshore financial centers — Hong Kong, Singapore, the Caribbean, Panama — that exist today are the remnants of the British Empire, still running the same function. "The British Empire is still around today, but it has transformed from one that is overt and uses military power to one that is covert and secret, and uses financial and legal means."

**2. Schooling (English and soft power)**

When Britain colonized India and traded with China, it set up schools. In these schools, students learned English — and through English, they learned that British culture was superior. The school system also offered something rare: **mobility**. In hierarchical Chinese or Indian society, being smart rarely led anywhere if you were born outside the elite. But if you mastered English and excelled in the British system, you could win a scholarship to Oxford or Cambridge, become a "world scholar," and join the British Empire's network. Prof. Jiang calls this a secret society — an indoctrination system for local elites that worked precisely because it offered real upward mobility in exchange for loyalty to the British system. This is why, he argues, Chinese students today still obsessively learn English: the game was designed to make that the path to success.

**3. The Navy**

"If they didn't like you, they would come and destroy you." The Royal Navy enforced the financial system with hard power. The Opium Wars against China are his example — nothing could resist the navy.

### The Drug Trade and the System Running Today

Prof. Jiang uses the global cocaine trade as a concrete present-day example of how the same financial architecture operates. The drug trade, he argues, could not exist without the British-inherited offshore financial system: people only take the risk of dealing drugs because they can be guaranteed they will be able to use and protect the profits. The offshore financial centers — still concentrated in the former British Empire (Caribbean, Panama, Singapore, Hong Kong) — provide the money-laundering infrastructure. Australia, Canada, and the United States then absorb this cleaned-up money through real estate and banking.

### Over-Financialization and the Empire's Self-Destruction

The episode closes with the argument that **over-financialization destroys empires from within** — the same pattern as Spanish hubris applied to the modern West. Stealing wealth from the third world has worked in the short term, but it has made Western populations "lazy, corrupt, and arrogant." The result: inequality (1% holds most wealth), political corruption (the system serves the rich, not the people), and immorality (people care only about money, not community). Prof. Jiang's conclusion: "The western world — they are a victim of their own success."

He answers a student's question about why anyone plays this game if it leads to self-destruction: because in the short term, it wins. He uses a sports analogy — doping destroys your body, but once one competitor dopes and wins, others are forced to as well. It is a zero-sum game where refusing to play gets you eliminated. "If you don't play this game, then you get eliminated."

The episode ends as a setup for Day 7: the American Empire added US dollars to this inherited British game, making the system universal and even harder to escape.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Hubris (傲慢) | Arrogance that follows success — overconfidence in one's invincibility that leads to overextension and collapse. Prof. Jiang applies this to Spain, and later to the modern West. |
| Bank of England (英格兰银行) | Founded 1694 — the key innovation was lending to the nation rather than the king, making repayment guaranteed by the people rather than dependent on any individual ruler. |
| Glorious Revolution (光荣革命) | The 1688 political settlement in which Parliament became sovereign and Dutch capital transferred to England, creating the foundation of the British financial empire. |
| National sovereignty (国家主权) | In Prof. Jiang's account, sovereignty was invented not as a democratic principle but as a mechanism to protect capital — Parliament guaranteed creditors' property rights in perpetuity. |
| Offshore financial center (离岸金融中心) | Jurisdictions where capital is accepted without scrutiny of its origins — originally created by Britain, still concentrated in former British Empire territories. |
| Money laundering (洗钱) | The process of disguising the origin of stolen or criminal capital through the legal and financial system, making it appear legitimate. |
| Soft power (软实力) | The use of culture, language, and schooling to shape the beliefs and loyalties of foreign elites — Britain's English-language education system is Prof. Jiang's primary example. |
| Over-financialization (过度金融化) | A state where too much wealth flows into finance rather than productive activity, leading to corruption, inequality, and societal decay — Prof. Jiang's diagnosis of the modern West. |
| World scholar | Prof. Jiang's term for the elite scholarship system (modeled on the Rhodes Scholarship) that brought top students from across the British Empire to Oxford, creating a loyal transnational network. |
| Energy, openness, cohesion (活力、开放、凝聚力) | The three qualities Prof. Jiang says make an empire strong — and which wealth and hubris eventually destroy. |

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's analysis of why Chinese students chase English and US dollars is a direct challenge: **examine which games you are playing, and ask who designed them.**

Think about the credentials and signals you pursue. A prestigious degree, a job at a famous company, a salary measured in a particular currency — these feel like personal choices, but they are also the outputs of a system someone else designed to attract and reward certain kinds of loyalty. The British designed their schooling system to make Oxford the pinnacle of ambition so that the smartest people from colonized countries would voluntarily serve the empire. That system is still running.

The questions to ask yourself:
- Who benefits when I optimize for this particular credential or currency?
- What is the mobility this system actually offers me — versus what mobility I might build outside it?
- Am I the Spanish, getting rich and becoming lazy? Or am I the Dutch, building skills and cohesion under pressure?

This doesn't mean you should refuse to learn English or avoid US dollars. It means understanding that these are moves in a game, not natural facts — and that understanding the game is the first step to playing it deliberately.

## Synthesis

**The Art of War (孙子兵法) on winning without fighting:** Sun Tzu's highest principle is 不战而屈人之兵 — "to subdue the enemy without fighting." The British financial empire is a near-perfect historical instantiation of this: Britain extracted wealth from India and China not primarily through military occupation (though the navy was the backstop) but through financial architecture and educational soft power that made local elites willing participants. The navy was the threat; the Bank of England and the schooling system were the actual mechanism. The genius of the system is that it made cooperation rational for the very people being exploited.

**Thucydides on the strong and the weak:** In the Melian Dialogue, the Athenians tell the Melians plainly: "The strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must." Prof. Jiang's analysis of the British financial system is in this tradition — he is not morally endorsing the system, but he is saying that once it exists, weaker players are rationally compelled to participate. Chinese students are not stupid for chasing dollars; they are responding correctly to the incentives in front of them. The trap is built into the rules.

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang says the British invented the Bank of England to solve the problem of lending to sovereigns — you couldn't trust a king to repay. By shifting liability to the nation (i.e., the people), the debt became permanent. Apply his Players / Rules / Incentives framework to this: who are the players, what rule changed in 1694, and whose incentives does that rule serve?

2. Prof. Jiang argues that schooling in English was a mechanism of empire — it offered real mobility to individual students while serving British imperial interests. Where do you see similar structures operating today — systems that genuinely offer individuals advancement while simultaneously reinforcing a larger power structure?

3. The episode ends with a student asking: if success leads to corruption and collapse, what is the meaning of success? Prof. Jiang's answer is that the game is zero-sum in the short term — you play or you get eliminated — but destructive in the long term. Does this mean there is no escape, or does it point to a different way of defining winning?
