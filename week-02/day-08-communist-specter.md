# Day 8: The Communist Specter
**Episode:** #8 | **Watch:** [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=axqDLhWs93Q](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=axqDLhWs93Q) | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang opens with a puzzle: if capitalism and communism are polar opposites — a true dialectic — how did China transition from one to the other seamlessly, without the violence that marked every other great ideological shift in history (feudalism to capitalism, Catholic to Protestant Europe)? His answer upends the standard Cold War narrative. Communism and capitalism are not opposites. Communism is, in his reading, a weapon created by capitalism to destroy capitalism's real enemies: monarchy, theocracy, nationalism, and social democracy. The episode traces this argument from the 1848 revolutions through Marx's funding, the Bolshevik seizure of power, Wall Street's financing of the Red Army, and China's "communist" revolution that was, in practice, a peasant uprising dressed in Marxist clothing.

## Core Concepts

### The False Dialectic

Prof. Jiang opens by naming the puzzle: China went from communist to capitalist "pretty easily, pretty quickly and extremely successfully" — a transition that was "seamless," with "not much conflict." Compare this to the violent transitions from feudalism to capitalism, or from Catholic to Protestant Europe, which cost millions of lives. If capitalism and communism were true opposites, China's trajectory is inexplicable. His conclusion: "communism and capitalism are actually more similar than they are different."

### Capitalism's Four Real Enemies

Prof. Jiang identifies what capitalism actually fears — not communism, but four other forces:

1. **Monarchy.** Kings redistribute wealth. When a king comes to power he typically cancels debts and redistributes land. Capitalists do not want that.
2. **Theocracy / Religion.** All religions teach that money is evil. Catholic Europe was poor because people focused on salvation, not profit. Religion is an obstacle to capital accumulation.
3. **Nationalism.** Capitalism wants capital to flow freely across borders. Nationalism — loyalty to people and nation over capital — blocks that flow.
4. **Democracy / Social democracy (socialism).** If workers could vote freely, they would redistribute wealth — taxing the rich, expanding the welfare state. Capitalists fear genuine democratic power.

### Communism as Weapon

Communism attacks all four enemies in ways capitalism cannot do openly:

- **Against monarchy:** Communism forbids the rise of a single ruler — power goes to a party, which is functionally an oligarchy, a form compatible with capital.
- **Against religion:** Marx called religion the opiate of the people, false consciousness to be destroyed. If religion is gone, money becomes the only remaining organizing principle.
- **Against nationalism:** "Workers of the world, unite." International class consciousness explicitly overrides national identity.
- **Against social democracy:** The working class has "false consciousness" — they cannot govern themselves. A vanguard party must lead. This reasoning eliminates genuine democratic participation.

Prof. Jiang's formulation: "Communism was a tool used by the elite, by the nobility to make the movement of democracy, socialism, and liberalism illegitimate."

### Socialism vs. Communism: A Strategic Distinction

This is the hinge of the episode. Prof. Jiang argues that socialism — the natural evolution of industrial capitalism — was actually inevitable and broadly popular. With three classes (rich, middle, poor), redistribution wins a natural majority: the poor want it, and the middle class wants it because it raises their relative power and status. He says: "Just the natural course of human history, socialism will become inevitable." Post-WWII Europe proved this — it became social-democratic, not communist or purely capitalist.

Communism corrupts this natural evolution:

- **Socialism** = redistribution of property (take from rich, give to poor; middle class gains)
- **Communism** = abolition of property entirely (no one owns anything; both rich *and* middle class lose)

Under communism, the middle class joins the rich in opposing the workers — destroying the broad coalition that made socialism politically powerful. Prof. Jiang's argument: "When an idea becomes more extreme, the intention is to discredit it, to make it illegitimate, to destroy the idea." Communism was socialism made extreme and therefore toxic.

### The 1848 Revolutions and the European Nobility's Problem

The Concert of Europe (the post-Napoleonic agreement among monarchies to resolve disputes through negotiation rather than war) created stability — but stability that allowed the underlying tension to grow. The bourgeoisie, workers, and middle class formed an alliance calling for democracy, liberalism, and socialism. The 1848 revolutions erupted across Europe.

After 1848, the European nobility recognized the threat: the alliance of bourgeoisie, middle class, and workers would eventually destroy the old order. Their solution: divide them. Create the idea that the real conflict is not between nobility and capitalists, but between capitalists and workers — class conflict rather than order conflict. Make the movement seem extreme and fanatical (abolish all property). And internationalize it as a conspiracy (secret society funded by Jews, the British, foreigners) — not a legitimate domestic demand for rights.

The Communist Manifesto, written after 1848, serves this function. It declares workers part of a global conspiracy for world domination — precisely what those in power needed to delegitimize the reform movement.

### Marx's Strange Biography

Prof. Jiang raises three anomalies about Karl Marx:

1. **Britain's hospitality.** Marx was exiled from Germany for advocating world revolution. Britain — the most capitalist country in the 19th century — let him live freely and write in the British Museum. Why would the world's leading capitalist power shelter its self-declared enemy?
2. **Engels's funding.** Marx never earned money. His family lived middle-class lives — private school for the children, a comfortable London house. The money came from Friedrich Engels, whose father was a wealthy industrialist. Why would a factory owner fund someone calling for the destruction of factory owners?
3. **The vanguard party problem.** Marx argued the party, once it creates a communist society, will voluntarily give up power. Prof. Jiang's response: "Why would the party give up power? If the party is capable of creating consciousness, why don't they just switch to a different false consciousness?" This is an internal contradiction that reveals the system's actual purpose.

### 1917: The Bolshevik Victory

Prof. Jiang notes that the Bolsheviks won only 23% of the popular vote in elections after seizing power — a minority faction that had taken control by force. They should not have been able to survive. Yet they did, for reasons that point again to capitalist-communist cooperation:

- **The Romanovs' death.** The Romanovs — beloved by peasants, connected to European royalty, part of the Orthodox tradition — inexplicably stayed put when revolution came, believing they were safe. The Bolsheviks killed all of them. This alienated peasants, European royal families, and the Orthodox Church — three groups whose support the Bolsheviks needed. The only explanation Prof. Jiang finds coherent: the Romanovs held billions in foreign banks. When they died, those assets remained with the foreign banks — "the government of Russia has been trying for a long time to get this money back."
- **Wall Street funding.** The Bolsheviks had no money and no popular base. They hired 200,000 foreign mercenaries — Latvians, Chinese, Hungarians — to fight the civil war. These were paid with money from Wall Street and the City of London. The Bolsheviks raised that money through the Red Terror: extorting wealth from the Russian nobility at gunpoint. Prof. Jiang cites historian Richard Spence's *Wall Street and the Russian Revolution*: "Wall Street wants to destroy the Russian economy because Russia is a threat, and then it wants to steal the riches and the resources from the Russians."
- **Allied intervention.** Britain, France, America, and Japan sent armies to Russia — supposedly to destroy communism. Prof. Jiang's reading: they went to collect debt. Once the Bolsheviks paid, the Allies left. "The Allies helped the Bolsheviks."

The outcome: from 1918 to 1921, the Bolsheviks "reduced Russia to an economy based on barter and robbery," looting palaces, bank vaults, churches, and museums — stripping the country of an estimated $450 million in valuables to finance the war. In destroying Russia's tradition, wealth, religion, and nobility, they "made the Soviet Union now much more amenable to capitalism. That's what happened when the Soviet Union fell."

### China: A Peasant Uprising in Communist Clothing

A student asks whether China's "socialism with Chinese characteristics" is real communism or a facade. Prof. Jiang's answer:

Marx did not consider China or Russia ripe for revolution — both were agricultural/feudal, not industrial. Lenin modified Marx by arguing a vanguard party could speed up the process. Mao went further: you don't even need workers, just peasants, and you industrialize after winning. "Mao wasn't really leading a communist revolution. It had the facade of communism, but it was really a peasant uprising." Mao was a military commander, not a Marxist theoretician — comparable to earlier peasant rebel leaders like Zhu Yuanzhang of the Ming dynasty.

What communism gave Mao was: (1) legitimacy with the Soviet Union, (2) a framework for rapid industrialization. When the United States supported Chiang Kai-shek and embargoed China, Mao was forced fully into the communist camp — but "for Mao, communism was not that important. What's important is to industrialize very quickly" and to maintain national sovereignty. The Sino-Soviet split of 1957 followed from exactly that priority.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| False dialectic (假对立) | Prof. Jiang's term for the supposed capitalism-communism opposition — two things that appear to be opposites but are not |
| Capitalism's real enemies | Monarchy, theocracy/religion, nationalism, and social democracy — the four forces that genuinely threaten the free accumulation of capital |
| Socialism (社会主义) | Redistribution of wealth; the natural political evolution of industrial capitalism; broadly popular because it benefits both the poor and the middle class |
| Communism (共产主义) | Abolition of all property; in Prof. Jiang's reading, socialism made extreme in order to discredit it |
| False consciousness (虚假意识) | Marx's concept: workers are brainwashed by capitalist schools, media, and religion into accepting a system that harms them |
| Vanguard party (先锋队) | The elite party that leads the workers toward communism — and which, Marx claimed, would voluntarily relinquish power once communism was achieved |
| Bourgeoisie (资产阶级) | The industrial capitalists and factory owners; the new power class that emerged with industrialization |
| Concert of Europe | The post-Napoleonic agreement among European monarchies to resolve disputes through negotiation; preserved peace but allowed social tensions to accumulate |
| 1848 revolutions | Europe-wide uprisings by the coalition of bourgeoisie, middle class, and workers demanding democracy, liberalism, and socialism |
| Red Terror | The Bolshevik campaign of extortion against the Russian wealthy to finance the civil war; funded Wall Street's mercenary army |
| Asset stripping | Prof. Jiang's characterization of Bolshevik looting — destroying Russia's accumulated wealth and cultural inheritance to convert it into hard currency |
| Sino-Soviet split | The 1957 break between China and the USSR, rooted in Mao's insistence on national sovereignty over ideological solidarity |

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's argument is a masterclass in asking the Players / Rules / Incentives question beneath the surface narrative. When an idea becomes suddenly popular, or when a movement is widely funded, the question to ask is not "what does this movement say it wants?" but "who benefits from this movement's success — and from its failure?"

The same logic applies in everyday life. When your organization introduces a new initiative — diversity training, a productivity framework, a restructuring — ask: whose interests does this actually serve? Who funded or promoted it? Does it solve the stated problem, or does it reframe the problem in a way that benefits someone else?

Prof. Jiang's reading of communism as a tool to discredit socialism asks you to consider: what ideas do you hold that may have been made extreme or distorted precisely so you would reject the more moderate version? The radicalization of any movement — whether religious, political, or professional — often serves those who most fear the movement's modest, achievable goals.

## Synthesis

**The Machiavellian move: make the reasonable seem fanatical.** *The Prince* describes how a ruler can neutralize a threat by associating it with something extreme or dangerous — making moderates distance themselves from their natural allies. The European nobility's sponsorship of communism to discredit socialism follows this logic precisely: take the popular demand for redistribution, wrap it in the abolition of all property and a global conspiracy narrative, and watch the middle class run back to the old order.

**Thucydides on the uses of ideology.** In the *History of the Peloponnesian War*, Thucydides repeatedly shows city-states using the language of justice and democracy as cover for straightforward power plays. "The strong do what they can; the weak suffer what they must." Prof. Jiang's reading of communism fits this pattern — the ideological language of liberation concealed a game being played by capitalists and nobility against the middle class and workers.

**Art of War (孙子兵法): win without fighting.** Sun Tzu's highest art is to defeat the enemy before battle begins — through deception, alliance, and making the enemy destroy itself. In Prof. Jiang's account, the nobility and capitalists did not defeat social democracy on the battlefield of ideas; they infected it with communism, causing it to self-destruct.

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang says capitalism's real enemies are monarchy, religion, nationalism, and social democracy — not communism. Look at a major political conflict today. Who are the real players, and what are their real enemies? Does the stated conflict match the underlying one?
2. Socialism, Prof. Jiang argues, was the "natural evolution" of industrial capitalism — broadly popular and historically inevitable. What disrupted that evolution? Do you find his argument convincing, or are there other explanations for why social democracy retreated in the late 20th century?
3. The Bolsheviks won with 23% of the vote, foreign mercenaries, and Wall Street money. What does this tell us about the Rules of the game in 1917 Russia? What rule changes would have been needed for the socialists' 40%+ vote share to actually translate into power?
4. Marx's vanguard party was supposed to give up power voluntarily once communism was achieved. Prof. Jiang finds this implausible on game-theoretic grounds. Why? Can you think of any historical cases where a ruling party actually chose to dismantle its own power?
