# Day 9: Why the US-Iran War Cannot Work
**Episode:** #9 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jIS2eB-rGv0 | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang analyzes the US-Iran conflict as a game in which two players are operating with completely different rule sets — and one player doesn't know it. He makes three arguments. First, regime change by air power alone has never worked in history; you always need ground troops, and a ground invasion of Iran would be catastrophic. Second, the US is trapped by three illusions — precision strikes, technological superiority, and shock and awe — that don't work against an asymmetric opponent who prepared for exactly this scenario. Third, and most important for game theory: Trump's personal game is not the same as America's national game. His personal incentives — financial bribes from Saudi Arabia and Israel, emergency war powers, third-term ambitions — make the war rational for him even if it is disastrous for the country. A fourth factor, the eschatological agenda of secret societies, may explain why decision-makers ignore rational cost-benefit analysis entirely.

## Core Concepts

**The Historical Rule: Air Power Cannot Change Regimes**

Prof. Jiang opens with a flat empirical claim: "No regime in history has ever been changed by air power alone. Every single successful regime change required ground troops." He uses Serbia as a test case — Milošević survived the NATO bombing campaign and only fell a year later due to internal political pressure, not air strikes. Germany, Japan, and Iraq all required ground occupation. This is the rule. Precision strikes, no matter how advanced, cannot physically remove a leadership from a capital city.

**The Three Illusions**

Prof. Jiang identifies three beliefs the US relies on — and explains why each fails against Iran:

1. **Precision strikes illusion**: "We have precision bombs. We can surgically remove targets. We don't need ground troops." But no regime in history has been changed this way. People believe it because ground invasions are "unpopular, expensive, and result in American casualties." The illusion is emotionally convenient, not factually grounded.

2. **Technological superiority illusion**: Advanced technology becomes a liability because it is expensive and you cannot afford to lose it. The result is risk aversion. "Meanwhile, the asymmetric side has nothing to lose. They can take risks. They can sustain losses and eventually outlast you." Prof. Jiang illustrates the cost asymmetry: the US spent $11–33 million in interceptors to stop a single $100,000 Iranian missile — and failed. At scale (1,000 missiles), the bill is $11–33 billion, and most interceptors miss anyway.

3. **Shock and awe illusion** (Prof. Jiang calls it "shock and stall"): "Shock and awe only works if the enemy fights on your terms. If they line up their army and destroy it, yes, they might surrender. But if they refuse to fight conventionally, shock and awe is just expensive fireworks." Iraq 2003 destroyed the Iraqi army in three weeks — then produced 20 years of fighting. Iran watched the June 2024 12-day conflict and analyzed exactly how Israel and the US strike. Iran is not surprised. Its critical military assets are distributed and hidden. Its strategy is to absorb the shock and strike back asymmetrically.

**Iran's Actual Counter-Strategy**

Iran is not primarily targeting US military bases. Its main strategy is to target the critical infrastructure of the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) countries — Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain — because these countries are "the linchpin of the American economy." The mechanism: GCC countries sell oil, receive payment in dollars, and recycle those petrodollars back into the US economy (stock market, real estate, AI investments). If Iran can destroy the GCC countries, the petrodollar system collapses. The two instruments: destroy desalination plants (60% of GCC water comes from desalination; destroying the Riyadh plant leaves 10 million people without water in two weeks) and attack oil infrastructure. Prof. Jiang's frame: "The United States is trying to play the 20th century game while Iran is playing the 21st century game."

**Strategic Depth Miscalculation**

The US sees 40 years of sanctions and assumes Iran is weak: "This will be easy." But Iran has been preparing for this war for 20 years — since George Bush named them part of the "axis of evil." Iran had a practice run in the June 2024 conflict. In its own religious worldview, this is a war against the Great Satan; it is the most important war in their eschatology. Iran is not playing a cost-benefit game about whether to fight. The war is a foregone conclusion in their framework.

**The Personal Game vs. the National Game**

This is the episode's central game-theory insight. Prof. Jiang: "Even if the war is bad for America as a country, the war might be good for Trump personally. And leaders optimize for their personal interest, not national interest."

Trump's personal incentives to pursue the war:
- Saudi Arabia invested $2 billion in Jared Kushner's private equity fund. "That's a bribe." In exchange for Trump being aggressive against Iran (the Saudis want regime change).
- Miriam Adelson, a major Israeli-American billionaire who has financed Trump's political career, offered $250 million if Trump runs for a third term — contingent on supporting Israeli interests, including regime change in Iran.
- Emergency war powers: a major war allows the president to delay elections, expand executive authority, and suppress opposition. If Trump is worried about midterm losses, "people rally around the flag during war. Opposition becomes unpatriotic."

The lesson Prof. Jiang draws: "You can't understand why wars happen by asking, 'Is this good for the country?' You have to ask, 'Is this good for the leader?' And often the answer is yes for the leader, even when it's no for the country."

**The Eschatological Factor**

Prof. Jiang introduces a third layer: secret societies with religious end-times beliefs that may be driving decisions beyond any rational strategic calculation. He points to the Epstein files as evidence that "networks of extremely powerful people" exist who influence governments, militaries, and intelligence agencies. He identifies three groups with overlapping interests: the Jesuits (Vatican and Catholic institutions), the Salvation Front (modern Israel, Messianic prophecies), and the Freemasons (American national security apparatus). These groups hold eschatological beliefs — that a major war in the Middle East centered on Israel must happen to fulfill biblical prophecy. "If you believe this, rational cost-benefit analysis doesn't matter. It doesn't matter if the war is unwinnable. It doesn't matter if it destroys American economy. It doesn't matter if millions die — because you're following a religious script that you believe is divinely ordained." Prof. Jiang acknowledges he cannot prove this is happening, but argues it would explain why American leaders proceed "despite all the evidence that the strategy won't work."

**The Inevitable Endgame: Ground Troops or Failure**

Prof. Jiang lays out a binary: the US either pays Iran ~$5 trillion in indemnity (which it cannot afford) or eventually sends ground troops. Why inevitable? The GCC countries are being hit by Iranian strikes on oil facilities and water plants and will demand the US protect them. Public opposition doesn't matter — "70% opposed the initial strikes and they happened anyway." A ground invasion of Iran would be catastrophic: Iran has 85 million people (vs. Iraq's 25 million in 2003), mountainous terrain, and a defensible capital of 9 million. Russia and China would arm the Iranian resistance. "The war would drag on for decades, cost tens of trillions of dollars, result in hundreds of thousands of American casualties, and probably still fail."

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Precision strikes illusion (精确打击幻觉) | The false belief that surgical air strikes can achieve regime change without ground troops |
| Shock and awe (震慑与威慑) | US military doctrine of overwhelming initial force intended to paralyze an enemy's will to fight; Prof. Jiang calls the result "shock and stall" |
| Technological superiority illusion (技术优势幻觉) | The false belief that advanced weaponry compensates for asymmetric opponents who absorb losses and outlast expensive equipment |
| Asymmetric warfare (不对称战争) | Conflict in which a weaker party avoids direct confrontation and instead exploits the stronger party's cost structure and risk aversion |
| Strategic depth (战略纵深) | A player's capacity to absorb punishment and continue fighting, built through geography, institutions, and preparation |
| Petrodollar system (石油美元体系) | The mechanism by which GCC oil revenues are recycled into the US economy, sustaining dollar dominance |
| Personal game vs. national game (个人博弈与国家博弈) | Prof. Jiang's distinction between what benefits a leader personally and what benefits the country — often divergent |
| Eschatological agenda (末世论议程) | Decision-making driven by religious end-times beliefs, which renders rational cost-benefit analysis irrelevant |
| Emergency war powers (战时紧急权力) | Executive authority expanded during declared war or national emergency, allowing delay of elections and suppression of opposition |
| GCC (Gulf Cooperation Council, 海湾合作委员会) | The six Gulf states (Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, UAE, Oman) whose petrodollar recycling props up the US financial system |

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's central insight — that leaders optimize for their personal game, not the national game — applies everywhere you encounter someone in authority making decisions that seem strategically senseless.

When a manager pushes a failing project forward, ask: what is their personal incentive? Admitting failure may cost them their job or reputation, even if killing the project is objectively right for the company. When a politician pursues a policy that polls badly, ask: who is funding them, and what do those funders actually want? The announced reason and the real reason are often different games.

The practical move: whenever you see apparently irrational behavior by a decision-maker, apply Prof. Jiang's question — "Is this good for the leader?" — before concluding they are simply stupid or confused. Most of the time, the behavior becomes entirely rational once you identify the right game.

This also applies to yourself. When you find yourself defending a decision that you privately know is wrong, ask: am I playing the personal game (protecting ego, avoiding embarrassment) or the actual game (achieving the best outcome)? They diverge more often than we like to admit.

## Synthesis

**Sun Tzu on knowing the enemy and knowing yourself**

*Art of War* (孙子兵法): "Know yourself, know your enemy; in a hundred battles, you will never be defeated." Prof. Jiang's episode is a case study in the US failing both halves. The US misreads its own position — believing its technological advantages translate to strategic dominance — while misreading Iran, treating it as an economically weakened target rather than a 20-year-prepared adversary with a religious motivation that makes conventional deterrence meaningless. Sun Tzu's point was not inspirational. It was structural: miscalculation about the nature of the game produces defeat regardless of military power. **(Synthesis)**

**Thucydides on the Sicilian Expedition**

In 415 BCE, Athens launched an invasion of Sicily it believed would be quick and decisive — a demonstration of overwhelming power against a weaker opponent. It destroyed the Athenian empire. The parallels Prof. Jiang draws to Hitler's invasion of the Soviet Union follow the same pattern: recent success creates overconfidence, overconfidence produces catastrophic miscalculation about what game you are actually playing. Prof. Jiang explicitly cites the Hitler/France/Soviet Union sequence. Thucydides called this *hybris* — the pride that follows great success and leads to ruin. **(Synthesis)**

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang argues that public opinion — 78% against the initial strikes, probably 90% against ground troops — is simply irrelevant to whether the war happens. Does this mean democracy has failed, or does it mean democracy was never the actual game being played? What would have to be true for public opinion to actually constrain a leader's war-making?

2. The eschatological argument is the most speculative part of the episode. Prof. Jiang says he cannot prove it. But he argues it is the only explanation for decisions that otherwise look irrational. When is an unfalsifiable explanation useful? When does invoking hidden actors become an intellectual escape hatch that lets you avoid harder structural analysis?

3. Iran's counter-strategy targets the GCC rather than US military assets directly. Apply Prof. Jiang's framework: who are the players, what are the rules, what are the incentives? How does targeting the petrodollar system change who is actually at risk in this game?
