# Day 15: The Return of History
**Episode:** #15 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zAer-Mqe7tQ | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang argues that we are living through the end of the unipolar moment — the era of American global dominance that began when the Berlin Wall fell. He identifies three pillars of that moment (Pax Americana, the supremacy of science, the universality of the US dollar), explains how each has decayed through hubris and corruption, and then maps the structural crises — energy, food, water, aging, migration — that will shape what comes next. The episode closes with his predictions for the emerging world order: regional trading blocs, resource wars, the rise of religion, the power shift to younger generations, and his case for why Japan is the only country likely to navigate the generational transition voluntarily.

## Core Concepts

**Fukuyama's "End of History" and the Unipolar Moment (单极时刻)**

When the Berlin Wall fell, US State Department official Francis Fukuyama published an essay arguing that the triumph of capitalism over communism meant humanity had reached its destination: liberal consumer democracy. Prof. Jiang does not engage with Fukuyama's philosophical argument — he uses it as a label for a specific historical configuration. The unipolar moment is defined by one power dominating the entire globe, and that moment is now ending. The war involving Iran is, for Prof. Jiang, not the cause of this ending but its symptom and accelerant.

**The Three Pillars of Pax Americana (美国治世)**

Prof. Jiang identifies three mechanisms through which American dominance was maintained:

1. **Pax Americana** — enforced through aerial supremacy, special forces, and the CIA. The CIA identified and promoted loyalists inside every government worldwide; nations that proved problematic (Syria, Libya) were destabilized by special forces and then struck from the air. This system brought genuine peace and prosperity to East Asia, Europe, and South America — but it was always a form of empire.

2. **The supremacy of science** — science became what Prof. Jiang calls "the dominant religion of the whole world." Scientists are "transnational priests" loyal to an international brotherhood (publication in *Nature*, Nobel Prizes) rather than to their nation-states. America controlled this brotherhood. Prof. Jiang uses COVID vaccination debates as his example: questioning the safety of rapidly developed vaccines drew the response "How dare you question science?" — the same reflex as religious orthodoxy.

3. **The universality of the US dollar** — the dollar functions like a belief system. Its value depends entirely on shared faith. Because America can print dollars at will, it has done so — funding corruption and making inequality worse. Young people, unable to accumulate wealth through work, shift to gambling (Bitcoin, sports betting) or quiet quitting.

**How Each Pillar Decayed**

The first generation of American hegemony understood the value of multilateralism. The second generation became, in Prof. Jiang's word, "arrogant" — exhibiting hubris. America began bombing countries (Libya, Syria, Iran) without seeking international approval, undermining the very rules-based order it built. Science became orthodoxy rather than innovation; Prof. Jiang argues there has been very little fundamental scientific innovation in 20–30 years, only the "scaling out" of existing American technology. The dollar enabled corruption rather than growth, producing extreme inequality and stagnancy.

**From Efficiency to Resilience (从效率到韧性)**

This is the central strategic shift Prof. Jiang argues every nation and person must make. The unipolar era was optimized for efficiency: make things as cheaply as possible, distribute them as widely as possible, maximize profit. Resilience means the opposite — planning for the worst case, accepting redundancy, and prioritizing the ability to survive crises. Prof. Jiang gives the global food system as his example: northern nations produce ammonia-based fertilizers and export them to southern nations with large populations and poor soil. If that trade is disrupted for any reason, the south faces famine. That is an efficient system that is structurally fragile. Every part of the modern world — oil supply, the internet (which runs on undersea cables that can be cut), global flights, food — is built on the same fragile efficiency logic.

**The Structural Fault Lines**

Prof. Jiang works through the specific variables that will determine which nations survive the transition:

- **Energy**: Everything in the modern world — computers, cameras, pens, clothing, medicine, food (via fertilizers) — is derived from cheap petroleum. A world of expensive or disrupted oil requires a "massive rebalancing." The China–GCC relationship is the key link: China's industrial rise from the 2000s onwards was powered by GCC oil; GCC surpluses were reinvested globally. If that link breaks, "the global economy is dead."

- **Food and water**: Nations with food or water scarcity that also lack freedom (transparency, accountability) are at highest risk, because their leaders may make selfish decisions without public accountability, and their populations will refuse to make the sacrifices needed to survive. These nations are also the most likely sources of conflict and migration.

- **Megacities**: Megacities (cities over 10 million) exist because of globalization, not because they are inherently sustainable. China and India — both with severe food and water vulnerabilities — have the most megacities. For resilience, populations need to be in the countryside growing food, not in cities specializing for export. China imports a quarter of its food and 75% of its oil; Prof. Jiang asks directly: "How resilient is China?"

- **Aging and the gerontocracy**: Western industrial nations are controlled by baby boomers who hold all the money and power and are living longer than any generation in history. For society to thrive, power must shift to younger people (ideally in their 30s), but baby boomers are selfish and healthy and will not give up power willingly. In most nations, this transfer will only happen through war or revolution. Prof. Jiang's exception: Japan. Because in Japan, the elderly have a moral obligation to the nation, he believes they may voluntarily exit power when the crisis becomes acute enough — making Japan potentially the only nation to solve this problem peacefully.

- **Migration**: As food and water crises deepen in the south, migration to the north will accelerate — potentially 100 times current trends. Northern nations cannot simply build walls, because they need cheap immigrant labor to care for their aging populations. This will produce cultural conflict and civil unrest within Europe and North America.

- **Religion**: The unipolar moment was secular and materialistic. When materialism can no longer deliver improving living standards, governments must shift to spirituality — giving people reasons to make sacrifices that go beyond material reward. Non-religious societies (East Asia: Japan, China, Korea, Vietnam) are at a disadvantage here. Prof. Jiang recommends looking into religion as a practical survival strategy.

**The Emerging World Order**

Prof. Jiang sketches what he expects to emerge:

- **Regional mercantile trading blocs** — a return to the pre-globalization world of the 16th–17th century, where British, Portuguese, French, and Spanish empires maintained separate trade networks. Global trade does not disappear but becomes far more limited. Local hegemons guarantee trade within their sphere.

- **Pax Judaica** — Israel's goal in the war is to knock the US out of the Middle East and establish itself as the regional hegemon. Prof. Jiang believes Israel and Iran, if left to deal with each other without US involvement, would reach a negotiated partition: Iran controls its sphere, Israel controls the Levant. He notes that Persians and Jews have historically gotten along, and that Iran has the largest Jewish population outside Israel in the Middle East.

- **The American Holy Empire** — America is the nation best positioned to survive because of its geography (protected by two oceans, controlling Canada and Mexico effectively), unlimited resources, and the creativity and entrepreneurialism of its people. But it will need a new identity: from secular global Pax Americana to a more nationalistic, community-focused, Christian identity.

- **Russia–Germany alliance** — after an initial period of conflict, Prof. Jiang predicts these two will recognize they are better off cooperating than fighting.

- **Techno-Marxism** — nations with resources will use AI surveillance states to marshal limited resources and control populations, creating a sharp class divide: freedom at the top, serfdom or slavery at the bottom.

- **Dynamic, shifting alliances** — the new world will not settle into stable blocs immediately. Alliances will shift constantly, more like China's Warring States period (战国) than the Cold War's stable bipolarity. The nation-state itself may fragment into city-states.

**Applying the Players / Rules / Incentives Framework**

Prof. Jiang applies his framework at civilizational scale in this episode:

- **Players**: The American empire (declining), China, the GCC states, Israel, Iran, Russia, Germany, and regional powers. Individual citizens and young people are also players — they can choose to adapt or not.
- **Rules**: The rules of the unipolar moment (dollar supremacy, Pax Americana, science orthodoxy) are breaking down. The new rules are not yet set — this is the dangerous transition period. Physical geography, resource distribution, and demographic structure are the boundary conditions that cannot be changed.
- **Incentives**: Baby boomers are incentivized to hold power. Scientists are incentivized to be loyal to the international brotherhood rather than their nation. Young people are incentivized to gamble rather than work. Nations are incentivized to grab resources before others do. Understanding these incentives explains behavior that looks irrational from a "what's good for society" perspective.

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang makes his practical prescription explicit: the world is shifting from efficiency to resilience, and individuals who fail to make the same shift will not survive the transition well. He is direct — almost blunt — about what this means:

- **Real skills over financial abstraction**: "No more cryptocurrency trading for you guys. Learn how to milk a cow or kill a chicken or grow food." This is hyperbole with a point — the skills that retain value in a disrupted world are tangible ones. What tangible skills do you have that would retain value if global supply chains broke down?

- **Community over individuality**: The unipolar era rewarded individual optimization — maximize your own earnings and status. The coming era will reward community-building — networks of people who can rely on each other when institutions fail. Who would help you if the systems you currently depend on were unavailable for a month?

- **Spirituality over materialism**: Prof. Jiang's advice to "look into the possibility of becoming religious" may sound surprising from an academic context, but the underlying point is about motivation: material incentives alone cannot sustain sacrifice, and the coming decades will require sacrifice. What gives you a reason to keep going that is not purely about accumulation?

- **Location matters**: Food security, water access, aging demographics — these are not abstract geopolitical variables. Where you live determines your exposure to these fault lines. Prof. Jiang's maps of food scarcity, water scarcity, and conflict are meant to be read personally, not just academically.

## Synthesis

**Synthesis — Thucydides and the transition problem**: Prof. Jiang does not cite Thucydides in this episode, but the structural dynamic he describes — a declining hegemon that begins breaking its own rules, and a multipolar scramble to replace it — echoes the Peloponnesian War. Athens, at the height of its power, abandoned the restraint that had made it credible, and the resulting spiral of hubris and counter-escalation destroyed the Athenian order. The lesson Thucydides drew was not that hegemons inevitably decline, but that the way they decline — whether with restraint or with arrogance — determines how destructive the transition is.

**Synthesis — Machiavelli on the second generation**: Prof. Jiang's observation that "the first generation appreciates the importance of collaboration" but "the second generation, the children, are arrogant" maps onto Machiavelli's analysis of how political orders decay. In *The Prince* and *Discourses*, Machiavelli argues that founders of orders understand necessity; their successors, born into security, mistake their inheritance for a natural condition and begin to abuse it. The prescription in both cases is the same: a return to founding principles before the decay becomes irreversible.
