# Day 14: The Law of Proximity
**Episode:** #14 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nOQqGy4boBY | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang introduces the **law of proximity** to answer three pressing questions about an escalating regional war: how opposing leaders are being killed so effectively, why killing leaders is counterproductive, and what the civil conflicts inside each nation mean for the world. His central argument is that every player is simultaneously running many games, and the game closest to them — the internal, domestic game — determines behavior in the external game far more than outside observers realize.

The episode uses live war analysis (Israeli strikes on Iranian oil infrastructure, Iranian retaliation against GCC energy systems, the assassination of Ali Larijani) as the concrete setting. But the conceptual lesson is general: to understand why nations act as they do, look first at the conflict *within* the nation.

## Core Concepts

### The Law of Proximity (近邻法则)

Prof. Jiang introduces the law of proximity as his central concept for the episode:

> "Whenever you play a game, you're not just playing one game. You're playing many, many different games. And the game that you choose to play is the one that is most proximate to you, the one that is most close to you. And that impacts your decision-making the most."

He illustrates with everyday life. At any given moment you are simultaneously playing: a family game (competing for parental attention with siblings), a school game (grades, popularity), a work game (favor with bosses, relationships with colleagues), a city game (Beijing vs. Shanghai), and a national game (China vs. Japan). You participate in all of them — but the one you feel most immediately, the one you can see in front of you, is the one that shapes your choices.

The same logic applies to nations. We tend to assume that states are playing the international game above all else. The law of proximity says the opposite: **the conflict within the nation is the one conflict that determines how nations behave against each other.**

### Why Killing Leaders Backfires

Prof. Jiang uses a gang analogy to explain a rule of strategic conflict: opposing sides avoid killing each other's leaders. Two reasons:

1. **Off-ramp**: Wars are fought to achieve political objectives, not to kill everyone. You negotiate a treaty with the other leader because they have the authority to implement it. If you kill the leader, you remove the person who can end the war.
2. **Escalation**: When a leader dies, a competition opens to replace them. In wartime, the most violent and extreme candidate tends to win. Killing a pragmatic, negotiable leader reliably installs a more extreme successor — and eliminates the possibility of a ceasefire.

Prof. Jiang applies this directly to the assassination of Ali Larijani, described as the de facto head of Iran's war effort and a pragmatic figure capable of negotiating across factions: "With him dead, it is now almost impossible to foresee a ceasefire." He argues the Israelis and Americans have "ensured there will be no off-ramp."

### The Civil Conflict Inside America (精英过剩)

Prof. Jiang frames American domestic politics through Peter Turchin's concept of **elite overproduction** (精英过剩): when too many elites compete for power in a zero-sum game, the result is civil conflict. He maps this onto current American politics:

- **Democrats (the elite / the empire)** support the war because they believe it will be so unpopular it destroys Trump and lets Democrats win the midterms and the 2028 presidential election.
- **Republicans (the counter-elite / MAGA)** also support the war, but for different reasons: wartime emergency powers allow the president to suspend the constitution and delay elections; the "Save America Act" (ID requirements for voting) can be used to suppress minority voters; a national draft could cancel elections entirely.

Beneath the Democrat-Republican surface, Prof. Jiang sees a deeper divide: **Wall Street finance** (backers of the current elite, defending the status quo) vs. **Silicon Valley AI** (backers of the counter-elite, seeking a revolution). Both sectors, he argues, are bubbles — the private credit bubble and the AI bubble — that expect a government bailout when they burst. They are fighting over which faction controls Washington when that moment arrives.

### The Civil Conflict Inside Israel (动物灵魂 vs. 神圣灵魂)

Prof. Jiang identifies two centers of gravity in Israel: **Tel Aviv** and **Jerusalem**, representing two irreconcilable visions.

| | Tel Aviv | Jerusalem |
|---|---|---|
| Character | Democratic, cosmopolitan, secular, open | Theocratic, conservative, religious |
| Vision of Israel | A creative, open, modern empire | A nation in repentance and redemption before God |
| View of King David | The king at the height of Israel's glory | The sinner who repented and was forgiven by God |
| View of this war | Catastrophic | Potentially redemptive — suffering precedes the Messiah |

People in Jerusalem, Prof. Jiang explains, frame the conflict as **animal soul** (动物灵魂) — the material drives of pleasure, comfort, and ego — versus **divine soul** (神圣灵魂). In their framing, Tel Aviv embodies the animal soul; the destruction of Tel Aviv is not a tragedy but a purification. Suffering is required before people turn back to God, and turning back to God is what will bring the Messiah. This eschatological logic, Prof. Jiang argues, is why you cannot analyze this war through a purely geopolitical lens — you need an eschatological one.

### The Civil Conflict Inside Iran (伊斯兰神权 vs. 世俗民族主义)

Iran's major internal divide, Prof. Jiang argues, is between the **Islamic theocracy** (伊斯兰神权) and **secular nationalists** (世俗民族主义) — roughly, a rural-urban divide. As the war intensifies, both sides radicalize: the theocracy trends toward Shia eschatology and martyrdom (the return of the Twelfth Imam); the secular nationalists trend toward **Persian exceptionalism** (波斯例外主义) — the belief that Persian civilization is great enough to be worth dying for, independent of the Islamic state. The risk is that Iran cannot afford a civil war while being bombarded externally, but the law of proximity predicts that people will remain more consumed by their internal conflict than by the outside war.

### Answering the Three Questions

Prof. Jiang closes the loop on his opening questions:

- **How are leaders being killed?** Through human intelligence, not electronic surveillance. The most likely source: internal factions within these nations are feeding intelligence about their *own* side's leaders to the enemy, in order to eliminate domestic rivals. "These different factions are providing intelligence to their enemies in order to limit their internal enemies."
- **Why are they getting killed?** Because the civil conflicts within each nation create players with strong incentives to harm their own side's leadership — the external war is a tool for internal power struggles.
- **What does this mean for the world?** The dominant trend is toward theocracy. The global secular financial order is being challenged by nationalist-theocratic movements in Iran, Israel, and America. Over the next 5–10 years, Prof. Jiang expects all three to move away from democracy and toward religiously-framed governance.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Law of proximity (近邻法则) | The principle that players always run multiple simultaneous games, and the game closest to them dominates their decision-making |
| Elite overproduction (精英过剩) | Peter Turchin's concept: when too many elites compete for finite power, civil conflict results |
| Emergency powers (紧急权力) | Presidential authority in wartime to suspend normal constitutional constraints and delay elections |
| Animal soul (动物灵魂) | In Jerusalem's religious framing: the part of a person driven by material desire, pleasure, and ego |
| Divine soul (神圣灵魂) | In Jerusalem's religious framing: the part of a person that seeks union with God |
| Persian exceptionalism (波斯例外主义) | The secular nationalist view that Persian civilization itself is worth fighting for, regardless of the Islamic state's fate |
| Shia eschatology (什叶派末世论) | The belief that the Twelfth Imam (the Mahdi) will return to lead Muslims against evil at the end of times |
| Decapitation strike (斩首行动) | Assassination of an enemy's top leadership to disable command and control |
| Human intelligence (人力情报) | Intelligence gathered through embedded human sources and spies, as opposed to electronic surveillance |
| Signal intelligence (信号情报) | Intelligence gathered through electronic eavesdropping — intercepting communications, tracking IP addresses |
| Mission creep (任务蔓延) | The gradual expansion of a military operation beyond its original objectives, each step justified by the last |
| Off-ramp (退出通道) | A negotiated path to ending a conflict; what is destroyed when both sides pursue maximalist objectives |
| Private credit bubble (私人信贷泡沫) | The accumulation of high-risk company-to-company loans, sustained by the expectation of a government bailout |
| Balkanization (巴尔干化) | Breaking a country into smaller, ethnically or politically divided enclaves |

## In Your Own Life

The law of proximity reframes how you understand your own decision-making. You are always running multiple games — with your family, at work, in your social circles, in your city — and the one that feels most immediate dominates. This is not a flaw; it is how human attention works.

The practical insight: when you cannot understand why someone is behaving in a way that seems to contradict their stated goals, look for the game that is closer to them than the one you are focused on. A colleague who seems to be sabotaging a project may be running a different internal game — a rivalry with another team member, a competition for a promotion — that makes the sabotage entirely rational from where they stand.

Similarly, when you are in a conflict with someone, ask: what game are they actually playing? Is it the game you think you are both in? Or is there a more proximate game that is structuring their behavior? The answer often reveals that what looks like irrational or hostile behavior is internally consistent once you identify the right game.

## Synthesis

**Synthesis:** Prof. Jiang's logic here echoes a classical observation about internal versus external threats. Thucydides, in his account of the Corcyrean revolution, describes how civil conflict within a city reliably enabled external enemies to exploit it — not because the city was weak, but because factions within it prioritized defeating domestic rivals over defending against outsiders. The law of proximity is Thucydides' lesson stated as a general principle.

**Synthesis:** The gang analogy Prof. Jiang uses to explain why you do not kill enemy leaders also appears in Machiavelli's *The Prince*. Machiavelli warns that destroying the enemy's ruling structure leaves you with nothing to negotiate with and forces you to administer a hostile population directly — an outcome far more costly than a negotiated settlement with a defeated but intact leadership. The "off-ramp" logic is identical.

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang argues that factions within Iran, Israel, and America may be providing intelligence about their own side's leaders to the enemy in order to eliminate domestic rivals. What does this claim imply about how we should read "national interests" in wartime? Can a nation have a coherent national interest when its internal factions have stronger incentives against each other than against the external enemy?

2. The law of proximity predicts that people are more consumed by nearby games than distant ones, even when the distant game has larger stakes. Think of a situation in your own life where a highly proximate, lower-stakes game is pulling your attention away from a less proximate, higher-stakes one. What would it take to deliberately shift your focus?

3. Prof. Jiang ends by suggesting that global economic depression and the collapse of material prosperity may force people toward introspection, community, and meaning — and that this could be a net good. Do you find this argument convincing? What game-theoretic logic supports or undermines it?
