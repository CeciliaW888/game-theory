# Day 2: Why Schools Suck
**Episode:** #2 | **Watch:** [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kS-muAuq62E) | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang uses his own experience as a school reformer in Shenzhen (2008) to demonstrate a core game theory method: identifying who the players are, what each player actually wants, and where those interests converge. The episode's argument is that schools fail not because of bad intentions but because the game they produce — shaped by the real, not stated, interests of parents, teachers, and administrators — optimizes for things other than learning. Understanding why schools suck requires analyzing the superstructure (超结构) of society and the convergence of player incentives, not blaming any single party.

## Learning Objectives
- [ ] List the key players in the school game and rank them by power, as Prof. Jiang does
- [ ] Distinguish between what players *say* they want and what they *actually* want
- [ ] Explain Prof. Jiang's core principle: players seek the best result with the least work
- [ ] Describe how superstructure (energy, openness, cohesion) shapes player motivations over time
- [ ] Explain why reform failed — and what "working within the convergence point" means

## Core Concepts

### The Core Principle: Best Result, Least Work

Prof. Jiang opens the analysis with a blunt premise: in game theory, all players are motivated to achieve the best possible result by doing the least amount of work possible. "People are lazy and people are greedy. It's that simple." This is not a moral judgment — it is the assumption that lets you predict behavior. Once you accept it, naive readings of player motivation (students want to learn; teachers want to teach well) immediately break down.

### The Players and Their Real Interests

The players in the school game are: students, parents, teachers, administrators, the government, and colleges. But not all players are equal — Prof. Jiang insists you must rank them by power.

**Parents** are the most important player. They pay to be in the game and can cause serious problems if unhappy. Their real motivation is not their child's growth but *face* (面子) — the social status that comes from a child's credentials. They want white faces (foreign teachers as a status signal), easy high grades, and a famous college admission to brag about. They do not want independent, critically thinking children — that threatens parental control.

**Teachers** are the second most important player, because they implement the rules. Their real motivation, Prof. Jiang says frankly, is to do the minimum required to collect their salary and go home. Most are not passionate; they have families and other pressures.

**Administrators** want to protect their relationship with powerful parents. Their job is to sell the school. Students, the government, and colleges — though nominally important — barely matter in practice. The government wants no problems; colleges want tuition money; students' actual preferences and learning outcomes are at the bottom of the power ranking.

### The Convergence Point: What Schools Actually Produce

When you map where all real interests overlap, the resulting school looks like this: white foreign faces, easy grades for everyone, a handful of top students sent to name-brand universities for marketing purposes, widespread grade inflation and cheating, and high teacher turnover. This is not a school failing — it is a school succeeding at the game it was actually set up to play.

Prof. Jiang's personal story makes this concrete. In 2008 he set up seminars, an English library, a student-run coffee house, and a daily newspaper — a program that genuinely worked. Students got into Yale, Cornell, Wharton. He was fired anyway, and called an 混蛋 (hùndàn — roughly "asshole") and a dictator. Why? Because he stepped outside the convergence point. He insisted on fairness and meritocracy, which threatened powerful parents and disrupted the game everyone else had agreed to play.

The lesson: any reform must operate *within* the convergence point of existing player interests. You cannot unilaterally declare a new game. Change is possible, but only as slow, incremental movement within the space where stakeholder interests already overlap.

### Superstructure and Why Schools Decay

Prof. Jiang introduces three metrics for reading a society's health: **energy** (drive and focus), **openness** (willingness to learn from mistakes), and **cohesion** (seeing yourself as part of a community). When all three are high — as in Finland today or China in the 1980s — schools thrive, because teachers are respected, students are motivated, and the community invests in every child's future. As wealth and inequality grow, cohesion breaks down into zero-sum competition, openness disappears (admitting mistakes gets you fired), and energy collapses (when parents can just buy their child a flat, why work hard in school?). The superstructure determines the players' motivations; the players' motivations determine the game; the game determines what schools look like.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Face (面子) | Social prestige and reputation; a key driver of parent behavior in the school game |
| Superstructure (超结构) | The macro context of a society — demographics, economy, politics — that shapes player motivations |
| Convergence point (收敛点) | The overlap of all player interests; the space where a sustainable game can be constructed |
| Asshole / troublemaker (混蛋) | The label Prof. Jiang was given for insisting on fairness outside the convergence point |
| Ostracization (排斥) | Being expelled from the game; the outcome players fear most, which enforces conformity |

## In Your Own Life

Think of an institution you participate in — a workplace, a team, a family — where the stated purpose and the actual behavior never quite line up. Try Prof. Jiang's method: list every player, rank them by real power (not formal title), and ask what each one *actually* wants, applying the principle that everyone seeks the best result with the least work. Then ask where those real interests converge. Chances are the institution is already optimizing for that convergence point, even if no one ever said so out loud. The question is not whether you can change this single-handedly — you probably cannot, for the same reason Prof. Jiang was fired — but whether you understand the game clearly enough to decide how to play it, or whether to play it at all.

## Synthesis

**Machiavelli on the unarmed reformer.** Prof. Jiang's firing despite genuine success is almost a case study in Machiavelli's warning in *The Prince*: "All armed prophets have conquered, and the unarmed ones have been destroyed." Prof. Jiang had no coercive power over the parents who were the game's real players. Without leverage over the convergence point's dominant actors, reform — however effective — is simply expelled. Machiavelli's lesson is not that reform is impossible, but that the reformer must first secure a base of power among those who control the game before attempting to change it.

**Art of War on securing your position first.** Sun Tzu's Chapter 4 states 先為不可勝 — first make yourself invincible, then seek to defeat the enemy. Prof. Jiang knew the school game's incentive structure (知彼知己 — know the enemy, know yourself), but he entered without first building alliances among the powerful parents. He moved to change the game before making himself safe within it. The sequence matters: understand the convergence point, build allies *at* it, then shift it incrementally — which is exactly what the lesson "reform must operate within the convergence point" is saying in game-theory terms.

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang was fired despite measurable success — top college placements, a program that was widely copied. By his own framework, why was success not enough to keep him? What would he have needed to do differently to reform the school without being expelled from the game?

2. Prof. Jiang argues that students' actual goal of learning ranks last among their three main games (pleasing parents, having friends, doing well academically). Does this match your experience? If the incentive structure changes — say, the job market stops rewarding credentials — which player's behavior would shift first, and why?
