# Day 5: The World Game
**Episode:** #5 | **Watch:** [Predictive History channel](https://www.youtube.com/@PredictiveHistory) | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang opens with a puzzle that repeats across history: the strongest, most resourced state almost never wins. The Qin dynasty was poor and isolated; Athens had everything; the Aztecs built an empire from a swamp; Rome started as a backwater — yet in each case the marginal group conquered the dominant one. He argues this is not an accident but a structural pattern explainable by three metrics — energy, openness, and cohesion — and traces how the game that societies play changes as they rise from startup to empire to decaying equilibrium, ultimately explaining why dominant powers always invite the agents of their own destruction.

## Core Concepts

### The Puzzle: Why the Weakest Wins

Prof. Jiang opens with the Warring States period: by every conventional metric — population, farmland, rivers, technology — Qin should not have won. It was mountainous, isolated, and poor. Yet Qin unified China. He then shows the same pattern repeating: the Macedonians (backward, uncultured) conquered Athens (greatest navy, wealthiest city-state); 500 Spanish conquistadors toppled the Aztec Empire of millions; the Akkadians, tribal mercenaries from an isolated region, conquered Sumer, the world's first great city. Standard historical explanations (disease, divine belief, local alliances) are acknowledged but dismissed as incomplete. The question game theory asks is: what game were the players actually playing, and what changed their incentives?

### Three Metrics: Energy, Openness, Cohesion

Prof. Jiang proposes three metrics to measure how dynamic a society is:

**Energy** — focus, motivation, willingness to work hard toward a clear goal. Rich elites lose energy: they prefer to extract rather than build, and the people they exploit are too worn down to care about the collective project.

**Openness** — humility, resilience, willingness to accept limitations and adapt. Wealthy elites become arrogant and insular; they refuse to admit they're wrong. Stagnation follows.

**Cohesion** — the degree to which people see themselves as one team and are willing to sacrifice for each other. Wealth inequality and corruption atomize societies; people become individualized and divided.

The marginal group, being poor, is forced into all three. This is why poverty produces creativity, not despite hardship but because of it.

This framework draws on **Ibn Khaldun** (ابن خلدون), the 14th-century Muslim historian, who called this quality **asabiyyah** — group solidarity or cohesion. His observation: civilized, wealthy areas always fall to poorer, more unified groups from the margins. Prof. Jiang treats this as close to an iron law of history.

### The Four-Stage Game Cycle

Prof. Jiang maps out how the game a society plays changes as it develops:

1. **Startup (cooperation game):** The founding group needs to survive. The game is cooperation, powered by a dynamic religion that gives people energy and purpose. The elite are poets and priests — those who can articulate the shared mission. Everyone is enthusiastic; it functions like a startup.

2. **Hereditary elite:** Over time, elite status becomes inherited. The new game is: how do I pass my privileges to my children? The religion transforms from an energizing force into a bureaucratic hierarchy — a set of rules that preserves the existing order. Innovation slows.

3. **Elite overproduction:** Too many heirs compete for too few high-status positions. Society fractures into factions organized around competing princes (the prince is a symbol, not necessarily the faction's real leader). Losers are exiled and found new colonies. This continues until there is nowhere left to colonize — and the warring states period begins. Crucially, this is also the period of maximum creativity, because open cooperative competition forces innovation and the sharing of best practices across city-states.

4. **Equilibrium/empire:** The city-states realize they can intermarry rather than destroy each other. Intermarriage among elites creates a new equilibrium — the elite are now transnational, above any single state. Warfare shifts purpose: it becomes a tool of **population control**, a way to resolve elite overproduction by reducing the number of people competing for status positions. Meritocracy ends; what matters now is position in the court hierarchy. The game becomes one of **factional struggle**.

### The Secret Society Game

In the equilibrium phase, court factions face three problems: **secrecy** (you must cheat to win, but you must not be caught), **trust** (how do you trust co-conspirators you cannot openly coordinate with?), and **coordination** (how do you act together without leaving evidence?). The historical solution, Prof. Jiang argues, is the **secret society** — a structure that appears across China, Greece, the Aztecs, and everywhere else.

Secret societies solve these problems through: a compartmentalized hierarchy (only the top knows everything; you enter at the bottom and climb), transgression as a trust mechanism (committing a crime together — historically including ritual sacrifice — makes defection catastrophic for everyone, aligning incentives), and an eschatology (a mythology about why the group's mission matters, ensuring coordination around a shared goal).

The result: every faction forms its own secret society, and the game of empire becomes a struggle between competing secret societies. No faction cares about the empire's external enemies — only about defeating rival factions. The factions become insular, corrupt, and divided. This is precisely what makes them vulnerable.

### Mercenaries and the Transfer of Power

The factions, needing help against each other, invite outsiders — mercenaries from the borderlands. These marginal people are simultaneously traders (learning the empire's wealth flows), bandits (learning its vulnerabilities), and soldiers (learning its best weapons and tactics). Through the secret society's hierarchy and eschatology, their leaders are absorbed into the factions. Then the mercenaries kill their patrons and take over. Prof. Jiang argues this is actually what happened with the Qin: different factions within the other states invited them in, one by one, until the Qin had absorbed enough to conquer everyone. Same with the Macedonians entering the Greek city-states.

### The World Game Exercise

Prof. Jiang illustrates all of this with a classroom exercise he ran in high school: teams representing countries receive unequal resource envelopes (scissors, rulers, paper, glue) and compete to produce commodities for a "World Bank." The United States team, with everything, finishes first. Pakistan — assigned nothing — typically finishes second, because the team is forced to beg, trade, improvise, lie, and work for free. Poverty forces creativity.

He ran a parallel exercise with teachers competing to build toy cars and catapults. The last-ranked team on day one reliably wins on day two, because losing forces reflection and adaptation while winning produces complacency. Crucially, the top team almost never recovers — once societies reach the top, they become too arrogant to reflect, and they simply decline. Prof. Jiang's observation: history offers no examples of a fallen empire where the original people recovered through genuine self-reform. New people may reuse the empire's name, but the original group is gone.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Asabiyyah (عصبية) | Ibn Khaldun's concept of group solidarity; the cohesive force that allows marginal groups to defeat wealthy empires |
| Elite overproduction (精英过剩) | Too many heirs competing for too few high-status positions, fracturing society into rival factions |
| Warring states (战国) | The period of open competitive fragmentation; also the period of maximum creativity and innovation |
| Secret society (秘密社会) | Organizational solution to the factional game: hierarchy for secrecy, transgression for trust, eschatology for coordination |
| Equilibrium (均衡) | The phase where competing city-states intermarry and form a transnational elite; warfare becomes population control rather than genuine competition |

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's three metrics apply far below the level of empires. In any workplace or team, ask honestly: is this group energetic — do people work with focused motivation, or are they just extracting salary? Is it open — do people admit mistakes and change course, or does seniority insulate the top from feedback? Is it cohesive — do people sacrifice for each other, or is it every person protecting their own position? The world game exercise predicts that a scrappy new team with few resources will outperform a well-resourced but complacent one — not because resources don't matter, but because scarcity forces the behaviors that actually drive results. If you find yourself on the well-resourced, highly-ranked team, this is a warning, not a reassurance: the conditions that produced success are also the conditions that produce the arrogance and insularity that end it.

## Synthesis

**Art of War and the five factors.** Sun Tzu opens 孫子兵法 with five factors that determine victory: 道 (moral law / unified purpose), 天 (heaven / timing), 地 (earth / terrain), 將 (command / leadership), 法 (method / organization). 道 — the degree to which the people share one purpose and are willing to sacrifice without fear — is listed first and is the most important. Prof. Jiang's three metrics are a modern restatement: *cohesion* is 道; *energy* is 將; *openness* is 法. This is also Ibn Khaldun's asabiyyah. The repeated convergence of Sun Tzu, Ibn Khaldun, and Prof. Jiang on the same first-order variable — group unity of purpose — across fourteen centuries and three civilizations is itself evidence that it is identifying something real.

**The water metaphor.** Art of War Chapter 6: 兵無常勢，水無常形 (there are no fixed conditions in warfare, just as water has no fixed shape; those who win adapt their tactics to the enemy's dispositions). Prof. Jiang's entire cycle — startup, hereditary elite, factional collapse, marginal takeover — is water finding the lowest point. No empire's advantage is stable because the conditions that produce victory (wealth, complexity, scale) also produce insularity, arrogance, and factional paralysis. The marginal group is water: formless, adaptive, filling the gaps complacency leaves open.

**Sun Tzu on intelligence networks.** Chapter 13 of the Art of War — 用間 (use of agents) — identifies five types of intelligence operatives, all organized through compartmentalization and deniability. The secret society structure Prof. Jiang describes (hierarchy for secrecy, transgression for trust, eschatology for coordination) is the institutionalized, scalable version of Sun Tzu's intelligence cell — but turned inward, against rival factions rather than external enemies. The fact that every empire across unconnected civilizations converges on this structure suggests it is the optimal solution to the game-theoretic problem of coordinating a conspiracy under threat of exposure.

## Think About It

Can you think of any counterexamples? What conditions might allow a dominant group to genuinely reform rather than simply be replaced?

2. The secret society structure — hierarchy for secrecy, transgression for trust, eschatology for coordination — appears across unconnected civilizations. What does this tell us about the game logic of factional struggle inside large organizations? Can you identify any modern equivalents of these three mechanisms in institutions you know?
