# Day 1: The Dating Game
**Episode:** #1 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hE4l9WyLF3U | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang opens the course by arguing that game theory — analyzing any situation through its players, rules, and incentives — is the most powerful framework for understanding human behavior. He illustrates it through a deceptively simple dating scenario: five ranked males and five ranked females trying to find partners. The episode shows why individually rational pursuit of status produces collectively suicidal outcomes, and then uses global fertility data to argue that this same logic is now driving civilizational collapse across the wealthy world.

## Core Concepts

### The Three Components of a Game

Prof. Jiang defines game theory as analysis of any situation through three lenses: **players** (who is participating), **rules** (the constraints and boundary conditions of the situation), and **incentives** (what winning looks like — what players actually want to get out of the game). His central claim for the course: once you correctly identify all three, you can understand the game and predict how it will turn out.

### The 5×5 Matching Game and Nash Equilibrium

To make the framework concrete, Prof. Jiang constructs a thought experiment: five males and five females, each ranked 1–5 on genes, wealth, and status. If players pursue cooperative matching — five with five, four with four, and so on — everyone gets the best available partner and society functions. This is the Nash equilibrium: the stable outcome where no player can improve their result by unilaterally changing strategy. But Prof. Jiang immediately notes that Nash equilibrium is "just a theory we made up." In practice, no one follows it.

### The Real Incentive: Status, Not Sex

Why does real behavior deviate? Because the actual game is not about procreation — it is about status. Prof. Jiang argues that people date to acquire social standing: the partner you can photograph for Instagram, brag about, or be envied for. Once status replaces procreation as the incentive, the math changes. Every player now chases the top-ranked partner, not the best available match. The result: a winner-take-most competition where the highest-ranked partners are mobbed while everyone else — the "incels" in Prof. Jiang's term — disengages from the game entirely.

### Superstructure: How the Game Changes Over Time

The dating game's rules are not fixed. Prof. Jiang introduces the concept of **superstructure** — the big-picture context of demographics, economics, technology, culture, and politics — to explain why mating strategies differ across history. He outlines three superstructures:

1. **Low-population, poor, low-technology societies**: no formal dating; women sleep with multiple men to disguise paternity and distribute child-rearing responsibility across the group, ensuring the child's survival.
2. **Growing-population, competitive societies**: arranged marriages; the imperative is to produce as many children as possible to compete with rival groups. No dating — you marry who your parents choose.
3. **Overpopulated, wealthy, high-technology societies** (our present): the dating game emerges because women now have real choice and the primary payoff from a partner is status improvement, not survival. The result is decreasing fertility and eventual civilizational collapse.

### Fertility Data and the Collapse Argument

Prof. Jiang grounds this in demographic evidence. Replacement rate is 2.1 children per woman. China is at approximately 1.0; South Korea between 0.6 and 0.8 — the lowest in the world. He projects South Korea into demographic collapse by mid-century: a population pyramid inverted so severely that there is no working-age cohort left to sustain the economy or fight wars. His argument is that once wealthy, educated women have genuine choice, they systematically choose status-maximizing partners or no children at all — and no government subsidy can reverse this because the underlying game is about status, which is zero-sum, not money, which is not.

The outlier he flags is Israel: the only wealthy, high-technology Western society with an above-replacement fertility rate (above 2.1). His explanation is game-theoretic: in Israel, bearing children is itself a status signal — an act of patriotism in a society that defines itself as under existential threat. When the superstructure redefines what earns status, fertility behavior changes.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Game theory (博弈论) | Framework for analyzing behavior through players, rules, and incentives |
| Nash equilibrium (纳什均衡) | Stable outcome where no player improves by changing strategy alone; Prof. Jiang's cooperative-matching result |
| Superstructure (超结构) | The big-picture context (demographics, economics, technology, politics) that determines which game is being played |
| Incel (非自愿单身) | Prof. Jiang's term for men who disengage from the dating market entirely when they calculate they cannot win |
| Replacement rate (替代生育率) | 2.1 children per woman; the fertility level required to maintain a stable population |

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's status argument is the one worth sitting with. Think of a competition you are currently in — for a job, a promotion, a social position, a relationship. Now ask: what is the actual incentive structure? If it turns out the real game is status rather than the stated goal (money, skill, connection), then the Nash equilibrium cooperative strategy probably won't hold. Everyone will chase the top position even when the rational move is to aim for their actual best match. Knowing this doesn't automatically let you escape the dynamic, but it tells you why cooperation keeps breaking down even when everyone would benefit from it — and it tells you where to look if you want to change the game rather than just play it better.

## Synthesis

**Art of War on fighting strength directly.** The status-chasing spiral Prof. Jiang describes — every player mobbing the top, leaving everyone worse off — is exactly the trap 孫子兵法 warns against. Chapter 3 argues that 上兵伐謀 (the supreme strategy attacks the enemy's plans, not their strength). Players who chase the highest-ranked partner are doing the opposite: deploying maximum effort against maximum resistance. The rare winner is the one who changes what winning means — as Israel did by making childbearing itself a status signal, reshaping the game's payoffs rather than competing harder within the existing ones.

**Machiavelli on appearances.** In *The Prince*, Machiavelli writes that "men in general judge more by their eyes than by their hands" — what is seen matters more than what is real. This maps precisely onto Prof. Jiang's status argument: the partner you choose is not primarily a resource but a signal to third parties. The dating game is a signaling game before it is a mating game, which is why optimizing for genuine compatibility is so consistently outcompeted by optimizing for visibility.
