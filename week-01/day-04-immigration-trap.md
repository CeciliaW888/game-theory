# Day 4: The Immigration Trap
**Episode:** #4 | **Watch:** check [Predictive History channel](https://www.youtube.com/@PredictiveHistory) | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang opens with a puzzle: East Asian immigrants to the United States do everything "right" — top PISA scores, elite universities, high incomes, no welfare, no jail — yet they remain low-status, hit a ceiling in corporate leadership, and fare worst of all groups in the dating and marriage market. His game-theory explanation: if someone invites you to play their game, the game is designed so that you lose. Playing by the host country's rules is a losing strategy. The episode then identifies what he argues is the actually winning strategy — demographic cohesion rather than individual assimilation.

## Core Concepts

### The Casino Analogy

Prof. Jiang's central argument is built on a single analogy: a casino invites you to play, but if the casino were fair it would go bankrupt. Therefore, if it invites you in, the game is already rigged for you to lose. The same logic applies to immigration. If a country invites you in and you play by its rules, you will always lose — because the rules were written by, and for, people who were already there.

This is the "trap" of the episode title. East Asian men are the test case. Prof. Jiang shows data from PISA rankings (the OECD's Program for International Student Assessment for 14-year-olds in math, science, and reading): East Asian countries — Japan, South Korea, Vietnam, China — score at the top. Yet when he looks at who becomes a CEO in American corporations, East Asian men underperform relative to their academic record. White women, Latino men, and Black men have all seen faster growth in corporate leadership.

### Why High Achievement Doesn't Convert to Status

Prof. Jiang's explanation: corporate success requires qualities that East Asian educational culture systematically selects against. The boardroom rewards assertiveness, high risk tolerance, high emotional intelligence, and the ability to argue and present views publicly. East Asian schooling rewards focus, compliance, obedience to authority, and grade obsession. The skills that make you good at school are different from the skills that make you powerful in an organization.

The result is a specific disparity he traces through dating data from OkCupid: white women are least likely to respond to Asian men of any group; only 22% of Asian women reply to Asian men's first messages versus 29% for white men; 20% of East Asian women marry white men, but only 9–10% of East Asian men marry white women. He summarizes: 75% of East Asian men under 25 in America are single. The quantitative punchline — a white woman requires an Asian man to earn $300,000/year to find him as attractive as the average white man earning $62,000 — is his evidence that East Asian men face a structural penalty that cannot be overcome by individual effort.

### The Winning Strategy: Demographic Cohesion

If playing by the rules is the losing strategy, what wins? Prof. Jiang argues: don't go to school and obey; instead, stick together as an ethnic group, maintain religion and cultural cohesion, and have lots of children. Demographic weight over time is the winning move. He cites two examples:

**Muslims in Europe:** He projects that by 2050, Sweden will have a 30%+ Muslim population, the UK and France 17%, Germany 11%, Italy 12%. Muslims score low on PISA and are more likely to claim welfare — but they are young, cohesive (united by religion), and have high birth rates. Prof. Jiang argues this cohesion, not academic performance, is what ultimately translates to political and social power.

**Hispanics in the United States:** White Americans will no longer be a majority by 2050. The growth is predominantly Hispanic — a group that shares religion (Catholicism), language, and cultural traits, and skews young. Prof. Jiang frames this as the same strategy working on American soil.

### Why Immigration Exists at All: The Historical Account

Prof. Jiang argues that mass immigration is historically new and not "natural." He traces it to a specific sequence: the British Empire → American independence → the need to displace Native populations → welcoming European immigrants as settlers → post-WWII American hegemony imposing open-society norms on Europe. Immigration as a path to citizenship was an American invention driven by a specific colonial problem (small population, large hostile territory). After WWII, America exported this norm to Europe as an ideological claim: closed societies produce fascism; open societies are peaceful. That is why Europe began accepting Muslim immigration.

Prof. Jiang's conclusion: immigration is a game designed under American hegemony. As American hegemony declines, the rules will change — and violent demographic conflict is his predicted outcome.

| Demographic strategy (人口战略) | Prof. Jiang's label for the "winning" immigrant playbook: cohesion + religion + high birth rates rather than individual assimilation |
| Hegemon (霸权国) | The dominant rule-setting power in the international system; Prof. Jiang uses it to explain why immigration norms exist and why they may break down |

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's casino analogy is portable. Before committing to any competitive path — a career track, an institution, an industry — it is worth asking: who wrote the rules of this game, and what outcomes are those rules designed to produce? Working hard within a system and expecting proportional rewards assumes the system is fair. Prof. Jiang's point is that systems are designed by incumbents, and the design reflects incumbent interests. This doesn't mean refusing to participate, but it does mean understanding whose game you are playing before you decide how hard to optimize for its internal metrics.

## Synthesis

**Art of War on fighting on enemy-chosen ground.** Prof. Jiang's casino analogy restates a core Art of War principle: never fight on terrain chosen by your opponent. Chapter 6 states 致人而不致於人 — bring the enemy onto your ground; do not go where the enemy leads. The immigrant who excels within the host country's credential system has allowed the host to choose the battlefield. The demographic strategy — refusing assimilation and building cohesion instead — is the equivalent of forcing the contest onto different terrain, one where birth rates and solidarity matter more than PISA scores. Art of War Chapter 5 puts it plainly: 出奇制勝, win by using the unexpected. The unexpected move is the one the opponent's rules are not calibrated to contain.

**Thucydides on who sets the rules.** In the *Melian Dialogue*, the Athenians tell the Melians: "The strong do what they will; the weak suffer what they must." Prof. Jiang's casino analogy is the same claim applied to immigration: the strong write the rules; the invited player always enters a rigged game. The interesting question the episode raises is whether Thucydides' formulation can be reversed — whether the weak can, over a long enough time horizon, become strong enough to rewrite the rules. Prof. Jiang's answer is yes, but only through the demographic strategy, which is a generational game, not an individual one.

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang argues that East Asian men's low status despite high income is evidence that "playing by the rules" is a losing strategy. Is there an alternative explanation — one that doesn't require the game to be rigged — that could account for the same data? What would that explanation predict differently?

2. The "winning" demographic strategy Prof. Jiang describes (cohesion, high birth rates, religious unity) is premised on a very long time horizon — decades or generations. What are the costs to the individuals in the first generation who adopt this strategy? Does game theory have anything to say about who bears costs now so that descendants can win later?
