# Day 16: Pax Judaica Rising
**Episode:** #16 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0aASxQrJYuo | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang analyses an ongoing US-Iran war and asks: how will it end, and what kind of world order follows? He argues the United States is losing the war — not primarily on the battlefield but across the narrative, political, and economic dimensions — because of structural corruption in the military-industrial complex (军事工业复合体) and a fundamental lack of political will. He then applies the world-system model from previous episodes to argue that the global elite who maintain the current order will need to replace the failing American empire with a new one, and that Israel — by demonstrating unity, determination, and strategic efficiency — is actively auditioning for that role. The episode ends with three explicit laws of geopolitics and a forecast of a post-war Middle East anchored by two regional powers: Israel and Iran.

## Core Concepts

### Four Dimensions of War

Prof. Jiang argues that war is fought across four dimensions simultaneously: narrative (global opinion, how history will judge the war), political (how allies and domestic populations perceive the war), economic (trade, oil prices, sanctions), and military. The key analytical insight is how each side relates these four dimensions to each other.

The American strategy subordinates everything to the military. Narrative, political, and economic considerations are forced to conform to the decapitation-and-surrender military plan. Trump threatens journalists who report bad news; he demands NATO open the Strait of Hormuz; Treasury unsanctions Iranian oil to keep prices low enough to sustain the war — all in service of the military objective. Prof. Jiang calls this a lack of "reflection": the strategy cannot adapt because the military dimension is treated as fixed.

The Iranian strategy is the reverse. Military moves are calibrated to serve economic, political, and narrative goals. Iranian forces selectively allow Chinese ships through the Strait of Hormuz (protecting oil revenue), charge tolls to neutral parties like Japan, Qatar, and Oman (building political allies within the GCC), and shape global opinion. Because the military is a tool rather than the master, Iran retains flexibility — it can adapt military tactics as the economic and political situation evolves.

Prof. Jiang draws three consequences from this structural difference:

1. **Reflection** — Iran can sit down and adjust strategy; the US doubles down because any change looks like admitting the military plan was wrong.
2. **Flexibility** — the US escalates toward self-destructive options (sending ground troops) because it has no other levers. Iran adjusts tolls, alliances, and supply routes as conditions change.
3. **Resilience** — forcing the world to conform to your military strategy means the world resents you, making resupply and coalition-building harder. Iran's strategy builds popular support and foreign sympathy, which replenishes resources and recruits foreign fighters.

### Why America Loses: The Military-Industrial Complex

Prof. Jiang traces US military dysfunction to the military-industrial complex (MIC). The founding generation opposed a standing army as a threat to liberty — Americans fought the Revolutionary War to expel the professional British army. But World War I and II required a standing military, and after 1945 the apparatus remained and sought permanent justification: the Cold War, Vietnam, the War on Terror. Each conflict transfers taxpayer money to private contractors.

Prof. Jiang quotes Julian Assange directly: "The point is not to have successful wars. The point is not to win wars. The point is to have never-ending wars which allows the military-industrial complex to transfer American taxpayer money to a transnational elite."

The evidence he cites:
- America spends 41% of all global military spending; Russia, its stated main enemy, spends 4.1% — one-tenth as much.
- Defense Secretary Rumsfeld announced on September 10, 2001, that $2 trillion was unaccounted for in the Pentagon budget.
- A single Army contractor stole $103 million; the Army only discovered it when the IRS flagged her tax returns.
- The Patriot missile system fails to stop cheap Iranian drones flying over American bases.
- The F-35 — $100 million each, 26 years to develop — has been shot down by Iran using low-radar technology.
- The USS Gerald Ford — $13 billion, more than Iran's entire annual defense budget of $10 billion — left the war theater after three weeks, officially for laundry-room fire damage, possibly because it proved useless in combat.

The result: three structural constraints on American war-making.
1. **Lack of political will** — only 40% of Americans support the war; Trump would need to raise $20 billion and potentially reinstate the draft.
2. **Lack of manufacturing capacity** — if planes and bombs are consumed faster than they can be produced, the military runs out of materiel.
3. **Unwillingness to sustain casualties** — the Pentagon suppresses death counts for fear of political backlash, which prevents the honest strategic assessment war requires.

### The Venture-Capital Analogy: What an Empire Must Prove

Prof. Jiang argues that the global elite — finance, multilateral institutions, intelligence networks — need an empire to provide the "muscle" that keeps the system running. When an empire becomes corrupt and incompetent, they must replace it. But the replacement must prove three things: **unity**, **capacity**, and **determination**.

He frames this as a venture-capital thought experiment. Company A has 100 profitable businesses but no passionate stake in a new venture. Company B is young, has already failed, but has pledged everything — 16-hour days, total commitment, existential stakes. A rational venture capitalist chooses Company B, not because it is more capable, but because Company B's incentive structure guarantees effort. Company A will probably steal the investment money; Company B cannot afford to fail.

Israel is Company B. The US is Company A.

Evidence Prof. Jiang gives:
- Israeli public opinion: 82% support expelling all Palestinians from Gaza; 66% believe the war against Gaza is a war for Israel's very existence. This is unity and determination the US cannot match.
- The 2024 Lebanon pager attack: Israel spent $275 million on a years-long operation that destroyed Hezbollah's communications and psychological confidence — cheap, patient, strategic. The message to the global elite: "We do your dirty work efficiently."
- ISIS does not attack Israel. Prof. Jiang argues this is because ISIS is, in substantial part, a Mossad operation — a demonstration that Israel can infiltrate and weaponize groups across the region far more cost-effectively than American bombing campaigns.

Prof. Jiang notes a historical pattern: mercenaries eventually replace the empire they serve. Romans started as mercenaries for the Etruscans; Aztecs for the Colhuacan; Mongols for the Chinese; Macedonians for the Persians. In each case, the mercenaries concluded the empire was corrupt and weak — and took over. Israel was created by Britain and America to be their "pitbull" and "aircraft carrier" in the Middle East. Now, Prof. Jiang argues, Israel is making the same calculation its predecessors did.

### The Greater Israel Project

Prof. Jiang explains that extremists within Israel believe God promised them territory stretching from the Nile in Egypt to the Euphrates in Iraq, including Saudi Arabia, Jordan, Lebanon, Syria, and parts of Turkey. Crucially, **Iran (Persia) is not on this map**.

This reframes the entire war. Israel's actual enemy for the Greater Israel project is not Iran — it is the United States, whose military bases occupy the territory the project claims. The strategic logic: use the US-Iran war to destroy the American empire. Force America into an overextended ground invasion. America implodes under economic and political pressure and retreats from the Middle East. Israel and Iran — both proved strong — are left as the two regional powers. The weak states of the GCC are absorbed or marginalized. Israel controls the resources of the Middle East and the trade corridors. Iran controls the Strait of Hormuz.

### Post-War World Order: Two Trade Networks

Prof. Jiang sketches what the post-war order looks like, grounding it in his earlier model of world structure (empire → finance → global economy → multilateral institutions → culture/media/law).

**Israel's network (Pax Judaica):** Control of Middle Eastern oil and gas fields, plus the India-Middle East-Europe trade corridor — the route by which India accesses Europe and vice versa. Israel's geographic position also connects to Africa and to Ukrainian/Russian energy and food supplies. On top of physical trade, Israel leads the Middle East in data centers and IT talent, positioning it as the AI surveillance backbone for the region. Pax Judaica is not a military empire in the traditional sense; it is a trade-and-data network with Israel at its center.

**Iran's network:** The North-South Transport Corridor linking Russia to India via Iran, and the Belt and Road Initiative linking China to Europe via Iran. This war has inadvertently ended Iranian oil sanctions — the US unsanctioned Iranian oil to keep prices stable, instantly delivering Iran $14 billion (more than its entire annual military budget of $10 billion). Iran is now embedded in the global economy rather than cut off from it. Prof. Jiang quotes a US Iran expert: "Trump and Israel's war has ended up delivering Iran de facto sanctions relief. Before the war, Iran was cut off from the global economy. Because of this war, Iran now is becoming embedded into the global economy."

### Three Laws of Geopolitics

Prof. Jiang states these explicitly near the end of the episode:

1. **The strong respect each other and prey on the weak.** Strong players have no incentive to fight each other when they can cooperate and exploit weaker parties instead. If Iran and Israel both prove strong while the US and GCC prove weak, Israel and Iran will eventually align against everyone else — regardless of current ideology.

2. **Ideological enemies can become strategic partners.** Iran and Israel appear to be enemies. But ideology is not destiny. The game-theoretic logic is that Iran controlling the Strait of Hormuz and Israel controlling Middle Eastern resources creates two complementary networks that benefit from cooperation more than from mutual destruction.

3. **The weak do not work well together; the weak must ally with the strong for protection.** The GCC (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Bahrain, Oman, Kuwait, Qatar) is individually weak and collectively unable to cooperate because, as Prof. Jiang bluntly says, "they're weak because they're stupid." Rather than combining resources, each will seek shelter under a stronger patron. As the US weakens, Qatar and Oman may align with Iran; Saudi Arabia and UAE may align with Israel. The GCC as a geopolitical bloc ceases to matter.

## In Your Own Life

The episode turns on a single diagnostic question Prof. Jiang applies to both armies: are you forcing reality to conform to your strategy, or are you calibrating your strategy to respond to reality?

This pattern appears everywhere. A student who decides in September that they will get an A by studying three hours every Sunday, and then refuses to adjust when the first exam reveals the method is not working, is running the American strategy. A student who treats the first exam as information — what topics am I weak on, what study method is actually working — and recalibrates is running the Iranian strategy.

The venture-capital analogy is equally portable. In any competitive situation — a job application, a startup pitch, a negotiation — the question is not just "what resources do I have?" but "what do my incentives signal to the other party?" The candidate who has nothing to fall back on and says so credibly is often more persuasive than the one with more options, precisely because their commitment is structurally verifiable.

## Synthesis

**Synthesis — Sun Tzu (孙子兵法):** The distinction Prof. Jiang draws between US and Iranian strategy maps directly onto Sun Tzu's concept of *shi* (势) — the shaping of conditions before the decisive engagement. Sun Tzu writes: "Supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting." Iran is not trying to defeat the US on the battlefield; it is using the battlefield to reshape economic and political conditions until American will collapses from within. This is *shi* — using position, timing, and environment to make the outcome inevitable before the climactic confrontation.

**Synthesis — Thucydides:** Prof. Jiang's account of why America cannot stop the war even when it is losing echoes the Athenian expedition to Sicily in the *Peloponnesian War*. Athens knew the campaign was going badly but could not disengage because the political cost of admitting defeat outweighed the military cost of continued failure. The sunk-cost trap is not modern — it is a structural feature of any empire that has built domestic political coalitions around the promise of victory.
