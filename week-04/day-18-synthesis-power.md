# Day 18: The Trump World Order

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang opens with a live news situation: Trump has just addressed the American people about "Operation Epic Fury," the ongoing war against Iran, promising to bomb Iran "to the stone ages." Meanwhile, Secretary of Defense Peter Hegseth has announced a "Greater North America" doctrine stretching from Greenland to Ecuador. Most observers call Trump erratic and stupid. Prof. Jiang's move is to ask the opposite question: *what if Trump actually wants to lose this war?* What if the apparent chaos is a coherent long-term strategy?

The episode applies the Players / Rules / Incentives framework to explain why Trump's war in the Middle East, his aggression toward Canada, Denmark, Venezuela, and Cuba, and Russia's war in Ukraine might all be parts of the same geopolitical logic — a deliberate effort to collapse the existing global order and replace it with two rival resource blocs: Russia's "Third Rome" and America's "Technate."

## Core Concepts

### The "What If He Wants to Lose?" Pivot

Prof. Jiang's central game-theory move in this episode is a deliberate inversion. The conventional analysis: Trump is an idiot, the Iran war is unwinnable (Iran is too large to invade, the Zagros mountains allow guerrilla warfare, the desert makes approach from Pakistan impossible, 50,000 troops is insufficient), and starting it while alienating NATO is self-defeating. Therefore Trump is a buffoon.

Prof. Jiang's counter-analysis: *if* Trump actually wants to destroy the American empire — to collapse the old global financial order and rebuild America as a resource-exporting fortress — then the same actions that look stupid in the old framework look brilliant in the new one. The question is not "is Trump competent at running the old system?" but "does Trump want to run the old system at all?"

This is a fundamental game-theory skill: before evaluating a player's moves, confirm you have identified their actual incentives. Misidentifying incentives produces wrong predictions.

### Players

**Trump and the American counter-elite:** Trump's two constituencies, per Prof. Jiang, are Christian evangelical nationalists and the AI/Silicon Valley tech elite. These are the counter-elites challenging the existing financial establishment (Wall Street, the "new world order" finance class). Their incentive is not to preserve the existing system but to capture the coming transition.

**Peter Hegseth and the Pentagon:** Hegseth announces the "Greater North America" doctrine — every sovereign nation north of the equator from Greenland to Ecuador is America's "security perimeter." This is the Monroe Doctrine, aggressively restated and expanded.

**Putin and Russia:** Prof. Jiang argues Putin is running a parallel strategy, drawn from Alexander Dugin's 1996 book *Foundations of Geopolitics*. Russia's incentive is not to win Ukraine quickly but to industrialize for permanent war, unify its population through nationalism and religion, and wait for Western civilization to collapse under its own contradictions.

**Europe, East Asia (China, Japan, South Korea, India):** These are the players who hold most US Treasury debt and who import most of their oil from the Middle East. Their incentive is to maintain access to energy and food — which makes them structurally dependent on whoever controls those supplies.

### Rules

**The old rules — the New World Order (George H.W. Bush, September 11, 1991):** Three pillars: (1) America becomes the financial capital of the world, offshoring manufacturing and focusing on Wall Street; (2) secular multiculturalism — the world unites around consumerism, erasing borders and identity; (3) Pax Americana — America guarantees global security so everyone can trade freely.

**The new rules Trump is trying to impose:** (1) Resources and manufacturing replace finance as America's primary economic base; (2) Christian nationalism replaces secular multiculturalism; (3) MAGA — national rejuvenation — replaces Pax Americana. Trump's argument, which Prof. Jiang says he holds "intuitively," is that the old system is a Ponzi scheme: America went from creditor to debtor, offshored its manufacturing to China, became lazy and corrupt, and created massive domestic inequality that produced a political oligarchy. The system will collapse; the question is who benefits.

### Incentives

**The commodity leverage map:** The Strait of Hormuz closure produces seven shortages simultaneously — oil, LNG, phosphate, ammonium, sulfur, urea, and helium. The first two are energy. The next four (phosphate, ammonium, sulfur, urea) are fertilizer precursors — food production. Helium and sulfuric acid are inputs to semiconductor manufacturing — AI. Prof. Jiang's point: this is not just an energy disruption. It is a global supply chain crisis touching food and technology.

**Who gets hurt by a Middle East collapse:** Europe and East Asia — specifically China, Japan, South Korea, India — import most of their oil from the GCC. These same countries are the largest holders of US Treasury debt (Japan, China, Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong, India, UK, Belgium, Luxembourg, France). If they now need to buy energy and fertilizer from North America instead of the Middle East, they cannot easily stop financing American debt. The $39 trillion US debt problem becomes manageable because the entire world is structurally dependent on North American resources.

**Who benefits:** North America and Russia. Venezuela (seized by the US in January, per Prof. Jiang), Canada (threatened with annexation), and the United States hold massive oil reserves. Russia controls the other large reserve outside the Middle East and is sitting on Ukraine, which gives it one-third of the world's carbohydrates and additional energy. Both become indispensable exporters in a world where Middle Eastern supply is disrupted.

**The water stability map:** Prof. Jiang adds a third resource layer — fresh water. North America and Russia are water-rich, meaning they are structurally resilient. Africa, Europe, the Middle East, and parts of Southeast Asia face water stress. China sits on Tibetan plateau water that flows downstream to India and Southeast Asia — a potential future conflict flashpoint.

### Russia's Third Rome Strategy

Prof. Jiang traces Russia's grand strategy to Dugin's *Foundations of Geopolitics* (1996). Dugin's argument: the unipolar American moment will collapse because its foundations — secularism, individualism, liberalism — destroy community and are ultimately anti-human. When Western civilization collapses, Moscow can become the Third Rome if Russia stays coherent, unified, and resilient. Dugin predicts civil war in the United States, collapse of China, collapse of Europe, collapse of the Middle East.

Russia's implementation: the Ukraine war is not a response to NATO expansion. It is the vehicle for industrializing Russia's economy around war production (military industry share of GDP rising sharply), building domestic drone manufacturing capacity (replacing Iranian drone imports with domestic production, then exporting to Iran), unifying the Russian population through nationalism and religion, and securing control of Ukrainian farmland and Black Sea energy export routes.

### America's Technate

The American parallel to the Third Rome is what Prof. Jiang calls "the technate" — the concept behind Hegseth's "Greater North America." The premise: North America is self-sufficient in energy, food, and water. As the global order collapses, build a fortress. The Monroe Doctrine is the historical precedent; the technate is the updated version for a world of permanent war.

This explains why Trump simultaneously started a war in Iran and threatened Canada, Denmark (Greenland), Colombia, Mexico, Cuba, Honduras, and Nicaragua. In isolation, these look random. As a single strategy — consolidating North American resources before the global collapse accelerates — they form a coherent game.

### The Three-Vector Analytical Method

In the Q&A, a student asks how the eschatological (religious) explanation from a previous episode connects to the geopolitical/economic analysis here. Prof. Jiang's answer: there are three analytical vectors — eschatological, geopolitical/economic, and historical. History is driven by all three. Religion is memory — ancient human experience encoded in myth. Geopolitics and economics drive historical forces. When all three vectors point in the same direction, a prediction becomes highly reliable. Prof. Jiang's method is to analyze events through multiple lenses and act with high confidence when they converge.

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's "what if he wants to lose?" pivot is directly applicable to any situation where someone's behavior looks irrational. Before concluding that a player is incompetent, ask: incompetent *at what goal?* A person who keeps failing to get promoted may not be bad at their job — they may be optimizing for something else (avoiding responsibility, keeping time for family, collecting a paycheck with minimum stress). A company that keeps losing market share may be deliberately harvesting cash before exiting an industry. A student who keeps failing tests may be avoiding the expectations that come with success.

The game-theory discipline here is: nail down the incentives before evaluating the moves. The incentive structure you assume shapes every prediction you make. If you assume someone is trying to preserve the old system and they are actually trying to destroy it, every prediction will be wrong — and you will keep being surprised.

Second application: the resource dependency map. Prof. Jiang shows that who holds power in a collapse scenario is determined by who controls the basics — energy, food, water — not who controls financial instruments or information. In your own life: if the systems you depend on (employer, platform, supply chain) were disrupted, which of your resources would retain value? Physical skills, local networks, land, and durable goods hold value in disruption scenarios that financial assets do not.

## Synthesis

**Synthesis — Machiavelli on controlled chaos:** In *The Prince*, Machiavelli argues that a new ruler must destroy the old order quickly and completely — half-measures leave enemies who can recover and retaliate. Trump's strategy as Prof. Jiang describes it fits this logic: a gradual unwinding of Pax Americana would give its beneficiaries time to organize resistance. Accelerating the collapse through apparent recklessness — the Iran war, simultaneous fights with NATO, Canada, Greenland — may leave no coherent opposition capable of restoring the old order.

**Synthesis — Thucydides on resource-based power:** Thucydides' account of the Peloponnesian War emphasizes that Sparta's ultimate strength was not naval or financial but agrarian — a self-sufficient base that could sustain a long war of attrition while Athens, dependent on imported grain through the Hellespont, could not. The technate/Third Rome logic is structurally identical: build a self-sufficient resource fortress, then wait for the trade-dependent powers (Europe, East Asia) to exhaust themselves.

**Synthesis — Sun Tzu on winning without fighting (不战而屈人之兵):** Sun Tzu's highest principle is to subdue the enemy without direct battle. If the technate strategy works, the United States never needs to defeat China militarily — it simply makes China dependent on North American energy and food during the transition period, constraining Chinese options without a single direct confrontation.
