# Day 17: The Great Reset
**Episode:** #17 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VbowBTJWKBo | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang argues that financial collapses do not happen naturally — they are engineered by the people who control the game. He traces the origin of this system to the 1688 Glorious Revolution and the founding of the Bank of England in 1694, explains how the same model was exported to America via the Federal Reserve in 1914, reconstructs what actually happened in the 2008 financial crisis (and who profited from it), shows how transnational capital orchestrated China's rise after 2008, and closes with a prediction: the same players who engineered past collapses are now positioning to engineer the next one — and the likely destination for global capital is Israel, not China.

## Core Concepts

### The Boom-Bust Cycle Is Not Natural

Standard economics teaches the boom-bust cycle as a law of gravity — overconfidence inflates a bubble, the bubble must eventually pop. Prof. Jiang accepts the description but rejects the explanation. His argument: **bubbles do not have to collapse**. Private banks can roll over bad debt indefinitely (and do — he points to the private credit bubble and the AI bubble today, neither of which has collapsed despite being obvious Ponzi schemes). Bubbles collapse when it is profitable for a few insiders to make them collapse.

### Money as a Collective Hallucination

Prof. Jiang frames money as "a collective hallucination" — it is not real in any physical sense; it works because everyone agrees it works. Banks do not simply lend out deposits: when a bank lends $1 million, it still has $2 million (the deposit and the loan as an asset), meaning each bank prints its own money. The system is coordinated, not by transparent rules, but by a signaling mechanism.

### The Signaling Mechanism: Interest Rates and Exchange Rates

- **Interest rate (within a nation):** The central bank's interest rate is not primarily a signal to consumers about whether to buy houses. It is a signal to banks about whether to expand or contract liquidity — lend more or lend less.
- **Exchange rate (between nations):** The exchange rate plays the same role internationally. When the Chinese renminbi (人民币) strengthened after 2008, it was a signal to the world: sell to China. China responded by importing commodities and building infrastructure funded by bank loans.

Both are coordination devices controlled by institutions most people believe are neutral or natural.

### Who Are the Game Masters?

Prof. Jiang identifies the structural architecture of the global game:

- **The empire** (dominant nation-state, currently the US)
- **The game masters** (those who control how US dollars move): Bank of International Settlements (BIS), World Bank, IMF, Wall Street, City of London
- **The legitimizing layer** (the rules-based international order): WTO, UN — designed to make the game appear fair and impartial
- **The reinforcement layer**: media, education, culture — creates the values and norms that make people believe the game is open and transparent

The key insight: transnational capital (金融资本) is simultaneously the game master and a player. It is both the parasite and operates within the host. This dual position is the source of structural corruption.

### The Bank of England and the Three Characteristics of the System (1694)

The Glorious Revolution of 1688 was a merger between the Dutch Republic (wealth from the spice trade) and England (military and geographic security). The result was the Bank of England (英格兰银行), founded in 1694 — a private bank that prints money sold to Parliament.

This system has three defining characteristics Prof. Jiang identifies:

1. **Profits are prioritized; losses are socialized.** Rich investors profit when the empire wins wars. If it loses, the nation-state (the people) pays. There is no downside for the capital owner.
2. **Wealth requires activity.** Capital must generate returns. The mechanism is war — the British Empire expanded (Napoleonic Wars, the Great Game, conquest of India, conquest of China) to generate profits for the bankers.
3. **Transnationalism (open borders for capital).** Capital must be able to move freely. The system builds institutions to facilitate this movement.

The ideological infrastructure for this system was built by sponsored intellectuals: John Locke (private property as a god-given right), David Hume (skepticism — undermine all knowledge claims), Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill (utilitarianism — if it gives pleasure it must be good, i.e. spending money is the ultimate good). Later additions: Marx (dialectical materialism, removes the divine), Darwin (we are just animals), Freud (sex and money as the real drives). The goal of this intellectual tradition, in Prof. Jiang's reading, is to make "money is God" the operating principle of society.

### The Federal Reserve and America (1914)

Transnational capital entered America through agents: Rockefeller (oil), Carnegie, JP Morgan, Vanderbilt — funded by the City of London. These agents then created an American version of the Bank of England: the Federal Reserve system (联邦储备系统), established in 1914. After its creation, three events followed in sequence: US entry into World War I, the 1929 stock market crash and Great Depression, US entry into World War II. Prof. Jiang presents this sequence as evidence that the system operates by the same logic: activity (war) generates returns, losses are socialized.

America won WWII and exported the system to Japan and Europe, then won the Cold War, achieving a "unipolar moment." During this period, manufacturing shifted to China and America focused on finance — setting up the 2008 crisis.

### The 2008 Financial Crisis Was Engineered

The structural setup: Bill Clinton (a) encouraged minority home ownership and (b) repealed the Glass-Steagall Act in 1999, merging retail and investment banking. This created mega-banks that needed to generate returns on massive inflows of global capital (from the GCC, China, Europe, and Japan via the yen carry trade). The vehicle was CDOs (collateralized debt obligations) — essentially subprime mortgages packaged as investment products.

The standard explanation: too many defaults, the bubble popped. Prof. Jiang's counter: **the banks could have rolled over the defaults indefinitely** — just extend payment timelines, as they do with the private credit bubble today. The collapse happened because it was profitable to collapse it.

Evidence: John Paulson made $20 billion by betting the housing market would collapse. The mechanism is simple — if you hold the mortgage and you also bet (via credit default swaps) that the borrower will default, you profit more from forcing a default than from keeping the borrower current. JP Morgan and others used the collapse to consolidate the banking industry — buying distressed competitors cheaply. Post-2008, institutional ownership of homes (firms owning 1,000+ properties) surged, because the collapse allowed bulk purchase of foreclosed assets at distressed prices.

The 2008 crisis destroyed millions of lives. It made a few powerful individuals and institutions enormously wealthier.

### China's Rise Was Orchestrated by the BIS

After 2008, the Bank of International Settlements (BIS, 国际清算银行) — described by Prof. Jiang as the central bank of central banks, based in Basel, Switzerland, and unaccountable to the public — needed to shift the center of global economic gravity. Its solution: allow China to rise.

The mechanism was the exchange rate (汇率). The renminbi had traded above 8 to the dollar for a long time. Starting around 2008, it was allowed to appreciate — a signal to the world to trade with China, and a signal to China to import more commodities and build infrastructure funded by bank loans. China's bank assets exploded. Today the four largest banks in the world by assets are Chinese — not because China is dominant but because in the banking system, liabilities are also counted as assets. It is all, Prof. Jiang says, an illusion.

The BIS's goal was to make China the new center of economic activity. This failed for two reasons: China does not want to be the global hegemon (it does not want to run military bases abroad or issue the global reserve currency), and the United States will not allow it (hence tariffs). China can manufacture for the world, but it cannot replace America as the system's organizer.

### The Next Collapse and the Shift to Israel

With China ruled out, Prof. Jiang argues transnational capital's next destination is Israel. The logic of the incentive structure:

1. **Wars are the most profitable enterprise** — the Greater Israel project ensures ongoing conflict and reconstruction demand.
2. **Rebuilding after destruction** — massive capital deployment needed.
3. **Control of global trade** — Israel's geographic position gives it leverage over trade routes connecting Africa, Asia, and Europe.

To move capital from America to Israel, transnational capital needs to first collapse the American economy — triggering a crash in the private credit bubble, the AI bubble, or both simultaneously. This would allow capital to exit America and simultaneously buy up distressed American assets (water, oil, infrastructure) at collapsed prices. As Prof. Jiang puts it: in chaos there is profit.

The Iran war is, in his reading, the first step in this transition.

### Trump and the American Paradox

A student asks: if Americans know this is coming, why allow it? Prof. Jiang's answer: certain Americans — including Trump — believe that expelling transnational capital, though enormously painful (he compares it to chemotherapy), is necessary for America's long-term renewal. "Make America great again" is, in this reading, a strategy to drive out the parasite even at the cost of severe short-term damage. This is not a comfortable or easy position — economic collapse could produce civil war conditions and years of suffering.

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's central claim is that the people who appear to be neutral rule-setters — central banks, international financial institutions, ratings agencies — are actually players with their own incentives. The next time you encounter a major institutional announcement (a central bank rate decision, a restructuring plan, a regulatory change), apply his three-question test:

1. **Who are the real players?** Not the organizations named in the press release, but who controls those organizations and benefits from their decisions.
2. **What are the rules?** Which constraints cannot be changed, and which are being changed right now — and by whom?
3. **What are the actual incentives?** Not what the institution says it is trying to achieve, but what outcome would materially benefit the decision-makers.

The 2008 crisis is the clearest case study: the announced incentive was homeownership for minorities (noble); the actual incentive for insiders was to create a bubble large enough that its collapse would be worth more than its continuation. When someone tells you a collapse is nobody's fault — it's just gravity — ask: who made $20 billion betting on it?

## Synthesis

**Synthesis — Art of War (孙子兵法):** Sun Tzu writes: "All warfare is based on deception" (兵者，诡道也). Prof. Jiang's picture of transnational capital is precisely this — the real game is hidden behind a legitimizing layer (the WTO, the UN, the rules-based order) designed to make participants believe the game is fair. The game master who controls the illusion controls the outcome without appearing to fight at all.

**Synthesis — Thucydides:** Thucydides observed that strong players do what they can, while weak players suffer what they must. In Prof. Jiang's framework, transnational capital is the strongest player — it sets the rules, controls the signaling mechanisms, and profits from both expansion and collapse. Nation-states, including the US, are powerful hosts but ultimately constrained by their dependence on the capital system. The question Thucydides would ask: is there any power strong enough to break the system from outside?
