## Core Concepts

### The Ceasefire Is Theater

Prof. Jiang opens with analysis of Donald Trump's announcement of a two-week pause in the US-Iran war. He identifies three structural facts that make the ceasefire meaningless:

**Fact 1 — Actions contradict words.** While Trump announces peace, the US continues sending aircraft carriers (including the USS George Bush) and ground forces to the Middle East. Prof. Jiang's rule: watch behavior, not announcements.

**Fact 2 — The Mosaic Defense (موزائيک دفاع) makes a ceasefire operationally impossible.** Iran has structured its military into 31 independent provincial cells, each responsible for its own strategy and operations, with no centralized command. This is designed so that killing the leadership does not stop the fight — it functions like a dead man's switch. The consequence: even if Iran's political leadership negotiates a ceasefire, the IRGC units in the field will not comply, because they do not trust the Americans or Israelis. Implementing a ceasefire would require Iran to forcibly control its own mosaic units — which would trigger a civil war within Iran. Prof. Jiang's conclusion: the mosaic defense can only end in total Iranian victory or total Iranian defeat. There is no negotiated middle.

**Fact 3 — Israel immediately exploited the loophole.** Within hours of the ceasefire announcement, Israel launched more than 100 strikes across Lebanon killing at least 250 people. The Americans then claimed Lebanon was never part of the deal; JD Vance called it a "legitimate misunderstanding." Prof. Jiang's read: the Americans and Israelis were never serious about the ceasefire and had already found the exit.

His evaluation: Iran's willingness to negotiate reveals a schism between its political leadership and its military — a fissure that will widen over time and eventually cost Iran battlefield advantage.

### How the World Works: The Driver Model

The most-asked category of questions concerns how Prof. Jiang explains global events. He offers a layered model:

1. **Occultists (方士/先知)** — Poets, prophets, and seers who try to read the pattern of history the way astronomers read stars. They turn the chaos of history into a story with a direction and an ending. Prof. Jiang calls this *eschatology* (末世论) — a combination of prophecy and plan. You believe the story will happen, and you believe you are obligated to make it happen.

2. **Secret societies (秘密社会)** — Formed by occultists to understand and promote their eschatological vision. Rich and powerful people join these societies not out of belief but out of self-interest: access to the pattern of history gives them better investment decisions and political positioning.

3. **The rich and powerful (富人和权贵)** — They have the money and institutional capacity to act, but they lack imagination. They can only work within the stories the occultists provide. Their loyalty is to themselves and their networks, not to nations or ideologies. They will switch societies when it is advantageous.

4. **Agents (代理人)** — Figures like Trump and Putin who are appointed (or co-opted) to move events in a direction. They look like puppets, but the key complication is that agents develop their own sense of divine calling. Napoleon, Julius Caesar, Hitler, Stalin — all were supported by secret societies and all eventually diverged from their patrons because they believed they had a higher calling from God. Prof. Jiang expects the same to happen with Trump and Putin.

Prof. Jiang's key point: this is not a top-down conspiracy. Each participant acts to maximize his own benefit, subconsciously contributing to the larger pattern. No single person is in control. That is why it does not look like a conspiracy from the inside.

### China Is a Construct of Empire, Not a Civilization

Prof. Jiang is asked how China fits into the emerging multipolar world. His answer is counter-intuitive:

China is not a competitor to the Western system. It is an extension of it — a vassal state (附庸国) or mirage (海市蜃楼) created by the system. The comparison he draws: just as the empire transformed the GCC from desert into a seemingly cosmopolitan economy by embedding its institutions (universities, financial systems, media), it did the same with China. Chinese students studying overseas, China joining the WTO, the construction of a modern financial economy — these are all mechanisms by which the system replicated itself inside China.

The consequence: when the global system comes under strain, China comes under strain. That is why China is anxious to see peace in the Middle East and is reportedly pressuring Iran to negotiate a ceasefire. A collapse of the global economy collapses China too.

Prof. Jiang's Players / Rules / Incentives read on China:
- **Players:** Transnational capital is the real player; China is the host, not an independent actor.
- **Rules:** China's elite have their wealth and families stored in the Western world — their true loyalties are there.
- **Incentives:** China's elite benefit from the existing system's continuation, not from its overthrow.

### Why Trump's Unpredictability Is a Strategic Asset

Prof. Jiang is asked whether Trump is a genius or a fool. His answer: Trump is a genius at politics and at accumulating power, but not a military or geopolitical genius. His unpredictability serves three strategic functions:

1. **Paralysis** — Enemies cannot formulate a response to someone who changes position daily. Indecision is induced.
2. **Distraction** — Attention is captured by the spectacle. What happens backstage — implementation of policy — goes unnoticed.
3. **Flexibility** — Trump is not bound to any stated strategy and can reverse course at any moment without embarrassment.

Prof. Jiang adds a further argument: Trump's real goal is a third term. The Iran war is the best vehicle for that goal — if the war goes badly enough, Trump can declare martial law and accumulate extraordinary powers. His opponents, the Democratic Party, are paralyzed into passivity, hoping the midterm elections will simply restore them to power.

### Nuclear Weapons Are a Liability for Iran

Prof. Jiang's answer to whether Iran should develop nuclear weapons is unambiguous: no. Two reasons:

1. **Asymmetry is fatal.** Iran could acquire perhaps 10 nuclear weapons. America has thousands. Threatening the US with nuclear weapons gives the US justification to use its arsenal. The threat accelerates Iran's destruction, not its survival.

2. **Complacency is strategically dangerous.** A state with nuclear weapons believes it is safe and stops thinking creatively about resilience and asymmetric strategy. The threat of destruction forces a society to constantly innovate its defense. Nuclear weapons produce complacency; vulnerability produces creativity.

### Why Technology Cannot Win Wars

A recurring thread in the Q&A: can cheaper drones or superior air defense win the war for the US? Prof. Jiang's answer: technology complements people but cannot replace them.

His illustration: an invincible robot with 360-degree vision and machine guns will lose to a human who lures it into a cave and throws rocks or pours water on it. People are dynamic, creative, unpredictable, and willing to die for what they believe in. Technology is static and predictable.

The US is losing the Iran war — in Prof. Jiang's assessment — not because of insufficient technology but because the American people lack the political will and eschatological conviction to sustain total war. Wars are won by people willing to die for a story. Drones are not.

### The Nation-State Is a Fiction

Prof. Jiang is asked repeatedly why Asian, Middle Eastern, and European nations do not act in their apparent self-interest. His consistent answer: the nation-state is a made-up concept. A nation is a collection of elite interests, and most elite in Asia and the Middle East have their wealth and families in the Western world. Their true loyalty is to those networks, not to the populations they nominally represent. These are "comprador" elites — carpetbaggers.

This is why Japan, South Korea, Australia, the GCC, and Pakistan behave as American vassal states even when the war damages their economies: the people who run these countries are co-opted by American institutional networks (he specifically names the CIA). The appearance of independence is just that — appearance.

### How Individuals Survive What Is Coming

The most personal questions in the Q&A ask how ordinary people should navigate global collapse. Prof. Jiang's prescription is consistent throughout:

- **Family.** He has three children. His children, not fame or money, give him the energy and purpose to work every morning. Children make the future personal.
- **Community.** Individuals cannot survive alone, regardless of wealth or preparation. A bunker does not help. Generosity, friendship, and mutual investment in a community do.
- **Learning.** Not for credentials — for genuine understanding. Develop doubt (question your own models), debate (engage with people who disagree), and imagination (make speculative leaps into the unknown; truth is often not where you expect it).
- **Personal transformation.** Prof. Jiang does not promise everyone survives. He says those who undergo the transformation — who stop being materialistically motivated and find a spiritual or purposeful story to live inside — are the ones most likely to make it through.

### How to Combat Bias in Your Own Thinking

Prof. Jiang is asked directly what techniques he uses to stay objective. He names three:

1. **Doubt** — He makes predictions specifically to create a mechanism for self-correction. When predictions fail, they force him to re-examine his model. Prediction is a tool for honest self-reflection.
2. **Debate** — He watches YouTube channels across the political spectrum and seeks out people who disagree with him to understand their perspective.
3. **Imagination** — He is willing to make speculative leaps that are not backed by evidence. Truth is often not in plain sight; finding it requires venturing into the unknown. He acknowledges this is unusual and makes him seem speculative — but he believes it is necessary.

He also acknowledges a personal constraint: he is no longer purely an observer. His predictions now influence global events. He is a player in the game he is analyzing. This compromises his objectivity and is a genuine limitation he names explicitly.

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's mid-term contains the most personal advice he has given in the series. He is asked repeatedly how individuals should prepare for global collapse. His answers, applied to your own situation:

**On community:** Think about the three or four people in your life who you would genuinely rely on in a crisis — not professional contacts, not social media connections, but people you would trust with your family. Are you investing in those relationships, or are you mostly performing relationships for social reward?

**On prediction as a discipline:** Prof. Jiang says he makes predictions specifically to discipline himself into doubt. What is one prediction you have been carrying implicitly — about your career, your relationships, or the world — that you have never explicitly stated and tested? Write it down. Commit to it. That makes it testable.

**On imagination:** Prof. Jiang says truth is often not in front of you. It requires speculative leaps. What is one question about your situation that you have been afraid to ask because the answer might be uncomfortable?

**On being an observer vs. a player:** Prof. Jiang notes that his fame has compromised his objectivity — he is now a player in the game he analyzes. Are there areas of your life where your vested interest in a particular outcome is distorting your analysis of it?

## Synthesis

**Synthesis — Thucydides on the Mosaic Defense:** The structural problem Prof. Jiang identifies with the Mosaic Defense — that it makes ceasefire implementation impossible even when leadership wants peace — has a classical parallel. Thucydides' account of the Sicilian Expedition (415–413 BCE) shows Athens discovering that military success does not translate to political control when the adversary's resistance is diffuse. Athens could defeat Syracusan armies in open battle and still lose the war because the population's resistance was self-organizing and could not be negotiated away. Prof. Jiang's point that "the political systems of both the US and Iran have lost control over the war" is precisely the dynamic Thucydides documents: wars that begin as policy instruments become autonomous processes.

**Synthesis — Sun Tzu on knowing the enemy (知彼知己):** Sun Tzu's foundational principle is that you must know both yourself and your adversary to win (孙子兵法). Prof. Jiang's critique of the US Iran strategy is essentially a Sun Tzu critique: the US does not know Iran (it treats Iran as a nation-state with a central command, when Iran has deliberately dismantled central command), and it does not know itself (it lacks the political will to fight total war). The Mosaic Defense is explicitly designed to defeat an adversary who relies on decapitation strategy — Sun Tzu's "attacking the enemy's strategy" (伐谋) taken to its logical extreme.

**Synthesis — Machiavelli on agents and princes:** In *The Prince*, Machiavelli distinguishes between a ruler who depends entirely on others (and falls when they withdraw support) and a ruler who builds his own foundations. Prof. Jiang's model of Trump as an agent who has developed his own divine calling and may diverge from his sponsors maps closely onto Machiavelli's analysis of the mercenary captain who becomes a prince: initially employed, eventually independent — and therefore unpredictable to his former patrons.

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang says the Mosaic Defense makes a ceasefire structurally impossible — the political system has lost control of the military. Can you think of another historical situation where a political leadership wanted to stop fighting but the military structure prevented it? What eventually ended that conflict?

2. Prof. Jiang's model places occultists (那些构想故事的人) at the top of the driver hierarchy, above money and military power. This is a strong claim — that imagination shapes events more than resources. Do you find this compelling? What evidence would either support or refute it?

3. He argues that nation-states are fictions and that elite loyalty runs to transnational networks, not to populations. If this is accurate, what would it take for a genuine nation — one where elite loyalty aligns with popular interest — to exist? Has one ever existed?

4. Prof. Jiang is explicit that he is no longer purely an observer — his fame has made him a player. He says this compromises his objectivity. How do you think about the objectivity of other analysts you follow? At what point does an observer become a player, and what does that do to their analysis?

5. He predicts that Texas will emerge as the winner of any American civil war, based on cohesion, energy, and openness. Apply the Players / Rules / Incentives framework to that prediction: Who are the real players? What are the rules constraining them? What are their actual incentives? Does the prediction hold up under scrutiny?
