# Day 19: The Hollywood-Pentagon Complex
**Episode:** #19 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0HYET47Cc-E | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang uses a disputed American rescue operation in Iran — and the fabricated narrative built around it — to expose a deeper structural flaw in American military thinking: the Pentagon has fused its strategic culture with Hollywood storytelling, to the point where optics and narrative have replaced economics, organization, and logistics as the criteria for military success. The episode traces this pathology from Abraham Lincoln's Civil War letter through *Saving Private Ryan*, *Black Hawk Down*, and the Jessica Lynch incident, then applies it to the unfolding US-Iran war to argue that America will lose not because it lacks firepower, but because it is fighting a movie rather than a war.

## Core Concepts

### The Rescue Operation That Does Not Add Up

An American F-15 (valued at roughly $90 million) was shot down over Iran. The US military claimed it successfully rescued both pilots in a 48-hour operation that deployed 155 aircraft, built a makeshift landing strip 200 kilometers from the crash site, and lost multiple C-130 transport planes — reportedly worth approximately $300 million in destroyed equipment — to sand and enemy fire, with no acknowledged casualties.

Prof. Jiang walks through the contradictions: an air force with claimed air supremacy losing a jet; a seriously wounded colonel hiking a 7,000-foot mountain ridge for 48 hours; an anonymous rescue subject who was never photographed or interviewed by the press; the need for 155 aircraft to retrieve one man. He cites the analyst Arnon Petron and the earlier *Washington Post* leak of a Pentagon plan to insert special forces near Iran's nuclear facility — a plan involving a makeshift landing strip, hundreds of troops, and seizure of enriched uranium — and argues the evidence points to a failed covert ground invasion, not a pilot rescue.

The key game-theory move here is: **what actually happened matters less than what the narrative accomplishes**. For the Pentagon, a failed invasion reframed as a heroic rescue is a success, because the metric being optimized is not military outcome but domestic optics.

### "No Man Left Behind" as Theology and Propaganda

Prof. Jiang traces the "no man left behind" doctrine to Abraham Lincoln's 1864 letter to a bereaved mother — one of Lincoln's most admired pieces of writing — and to *Saving Private Ryan* (1998), which dramatized the same logic: the home front matters more than the war front; saving one symbolically important soldier justifies sacrificing many others, because the American public must believe the war is worth fighting.

He quotes the Brown University paper by Kennor Merles documenting the formal arrangement between the Pentagon and Hollywood: in exchange for access to military equipment and personnel, producers must submit to Pentagon script approval aligned with recruitment and public relations goals. Over 2,500 war-themed films and TV programs have been made with Pentagon assistance since the industry began. The result is a feedback loop: Hollywood teaches the public that American wars are glorious; the Pentagon, exposed to the same stories, begins to believe it too.

The *Black Hawk Down* case illustrates this directly. In the book by Mark Bowden, the downed helicopter pilot was taken hostage, treated well, and eventually ransomed home. In the movie, Delta Force storms in and rescues him. Prof. Jiang quotes a general's speech in the film warning Delta Force that they are "cowboys" driven by personal glory rather than military discipline — and argues this is not just a dramatic device but an accurate diagnosis of Delta Force culture, where operators can anticipate book deals, speaking fees, and movie rights after the war ends.

The Jessica Lynch case from 2003 Iraq confirms the pattern: Lynch's unit was ambushed, she was injured, treated well in an Iraqi hospital, and the hospital staff tried to return her to American lines. The Pentagon organized a special forces rescue anyway, told the press she had been beaten and raped, and Lynch herself went on television afterward to say the military was lying. The Pentagon, Prof. Jiang says, does not care about the truth. It cares about making a Hollywood movie.

### Applying Prof. Jiang's Framework: Players, Rules, Incentives

**Players in the US-Iran war:**

- United States / Pentagon: the dominant military power, but constrained by domestic politics and the need to sustain narrative credibility at home
- Iran: specifically, the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (伊斯兰革命卫队) pushing for total war versus more cautious factions worried about economic damage and military overreach
- Israel: partner in the attacks, committed to degrading Iran's military capacity
- Russia: pursuing a parallel naval war of attrition against US sea lanes, deploying its "shadow fleet" of roughly 1,000 ships to force degradation of American naval capacity over time
- China: the main beneficiary of oceanic trade, attempting to triangulate between the US and Russia rather than fight either

**Rules (constraints neither side can simply override):**

- The US Air Force is designed for a two-week war, not an extended conflict; maintenance, pilot fatigue, and Iranian adaptive tactics (heat-seeking missiles that substitute for non-functional radar) degrade performance over time
- Fertilizer production depends on energy supply through the Strait of Hormuz; closing the strait threatens the food supply for 6–8 billion people, not just fuel prices
- Iran cannot destroy Israel because Israel has nuclear weapons; the deterrence ceiling is fixed
- America controls four critical maritime choke points — the Panama Canal, the Strait of Hormuz, the Strait of Malacca (马六甲海峡), and the Arctic route via Greenland — but maintaining all four simultaneously requires sustained naval capacity

**Incentives — stated vs. actual:**

Iran's stated goal is self-defense; its actual strategic objectives, as Prof. Jiang explains them, are three: remove the United States from the Middle East permanently, deter Israel, and disrupt the global economy to force a reset that benefits Iran.

America's stated goal is security and nonproliferation; its actual strategic objectives are: destroy Iran as a functional nation-state (power plants, desalination plants, universities, reservoirs), "balkanize" Iran by encouraging ethnic uprisings, and isolate Iran from Russian and Chinese trade networks.

The crucial game-theory insight: **these strategic objectives do not conflict with each other**. America can achieve its goals (Iran destroyed, balkanized, isolated) even as Iran achieves its goals (US out of the Middle East, Israel humbled, global economy disrupted). Both sides can "win" in their own terms. This is why, Prof. Jiang argues, peace negotiations are structurally impossible: each side genuinely believes it is winning, because each is measuring success against different objectives.

### The Three Criteria for Winning a War

Prof. Jiang states explicitly that there are three and only three things you need to get right to win a war:

1. **Economics (经济)** — resource management: winning as cheaply and as quickly as possible. Spending $300 million in military equipment to rescue a single pilot is not economics. It is the opposite.

2. **Organization (组织)** — implementation of strategy: the simpler the strategy, the easier it is to execute. Trying to build an airstrip inside enemy territory to steal uranium from a guarded nuclear facility is not simple strategy. It guarantees organizational failure.

3. **Logistics (后勤)** — supply: ensuring that forces have the equipment and resources they need to fight effectively over the duration of the conflict.

Iran, in Prof. Jiang's analysis, is attending to all three. The US is attending to none of them, because it is focused on optics and narrative instead. This is why, despite overwhelming firepower, he predicts the US will ultimately lose the war in strategic terms — even if it never loses a direct military engagement.

### The Shift in American Grand Strategy

Prof. Jiang describes a broader shift: the US has moved from guaranteeing global trade (as the issuer of the world's reserve currency) to acting as what he calls a "mafia state" — controlling maritime choke points and granting or withholding access to trade routes as a source of leverage. The Venezuela operation (in which the US removed President Maduro) is the model Trump applied: Delta Force performed a spectacular extraction, it worked, and Trump concluded the same playbook would work against Iran. The difference, Prof. Jiang argues, is the opponent. "You can pull this crap when you're fighting Somalis and Iraqis, but against the Iranians, you need total war."

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's three criteria — economics, organization, logistics — are a checklist for any sustained effort, not just military campaigns. Before committing to a major project, ask:

- **Economics:** Am I spending resources proportional to what I am actually trying to achieve? Or am I making expensive moves to signal competence, manage appearances, or avoid admitting failure?
- **Organization:** Is my strategy simple enough to actually execute? The more elaborate the plan, the more points of failure. If your strategy requires everything to go right, it is not a strategy.
- **Logistics:** Do I have reliable access to what I need — time, money, skills, relationships — to sustain this over the full duration? Most projects fail not at the start but when supply runs out mid-execution.

The optics trap is real outside of war. Reframing a failed initiative as a success to preserve credibility — rather than diagnosing what went wrong — is the organizational equivalent of what the Pentagon did with the rescue story. The cost: you repeat the failure, because you never acknowledged it.

## Synthesis

**Synthesis — Sun Tzu on economics and simplicity:** *The Art of War* (孙子兵法) states: "The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting" (不战而屈人之兵). Sun Tzu's entire framework privileges economy of force — winning cheaply — and operational simplicity. His contempt for protracted war is explicit: "There is no instance of a country having benefited from prolonged warfare." Prof. Jiang's three criteria echo this directly. The Hollywood-Pentagon culture violates Sun Tzu's core principle by treating war as performance rather than as a resource-optimization problem.

**Synthesis — Thucydides on narrative and self-deception:** In the *History of the Peloponnesian War*, Thucydides describes how Athens convinced itself it was winning the war in Sicily right up to the catastrophic destruction of its fleet (413 BCE) — in part because Athenian leaders, surrounded by optimistic advisors, could not afford to admit failure. The pattern Prof. Jiang describes — Donald Trump surrounded by people who tell him what he wants to hear, genuinely believing the failed invasion was a success — maps directly onto the Athenian expedition. Thucydides' warning: self-deception at the strategic level is not a personality flaw but a structural feature of democracies fighting expensive wars far from home.

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang says the US and Iran's strategic objectives do not actually conflict — both sides can "achieve their goals" while the other side also achieves theirs. If that is true, what does "winning" mean in this war? And how does this help explain why the two sides cannot negotiate?

2. The three criteria — economics, organization, logistics — describe what you need to sustain any long-term effort. Think of something you are currently working on. Which of the three is your weakest? What would it look like to fail on that dimension?

3. Prof. Jiang argues the Pentagon's Hollywood culture does not just deceive the public — it deceives the Pentagon itself. Can you think of an institution, organization, or profession where the story it tells about itself has come to replace the actual work? What are the consequences?
