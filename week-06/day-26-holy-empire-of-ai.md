# Day 26: The Holy Empire of AI
**Episode:** #26 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RG1clZlrfOo | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Prof. Jiang opens with conspiracy theory — the full Knights Templar-to-Freemasons-to-Illuminati-to-AI-surveillance-state narrative — not to endorse it, but to expose a paradox: the theory has no factual basis, yet it is intuitive, explanatory, and genuinely predictive. He then works through what he calls the "literal" explanation behind the metaphor. Secret societies are not supernatural conspiracies but a structural tendency: in every field — politics, finance, science, religion, military — a minority obsessed with power, order, and control converges and coordinates across disciplinary lines, gaining a decisive advantage over those who cannot coordinate. The episode traces this structural pattern from Albert Pike's Freemasonry and Zbigniew Brzezinski's technocracy to Larry Ellison's Oracle, Peter Thiel's Palantir, and Sam Altman's OpenAI — arriving at the argument that today's AI surveillance state is the contemporary instantiation of the Freemason "holy empire of reason."

## Core Concepts

### The Opening Paradox: Not Factual, But Truthful

Prof. Jiang presents the standard grand conspiracy narrative: the Knights Templar (founded 1118) become the Scottish Rite Freemasons, who create the Protestant Reformation, the scientific revolution, the American and French Revolutions, the Bank of England, World War I and II, and are now building a one-world government based in Jerusalem, enforced by an AI surveillance state — sustained by contracts with interdimensional demons.

He gives three reasons this theory is false: there is no evidence it is true, the elite themselves think it is "complete and utter nonsense," and it is anti-historical (it ignores hubris and the iron law that the powerful rise and fall). It is also anti-game theory: power makes people arrogant and stupid; a thousand-year continuous conspiracy would require a level of coordination that power itself destroys.

Yet the theory is, he argues, **intuitive, explanatory, and predictive** — it tells us something truthful about how the world moves even though its literal claims are fabrications. This paradox is the engine of the entire episode.

### The Literal Explanation: Structural Convergence

Prof. Jiang's "literal" version replaces demonic conspiracy with structural sociology. In any society, each major field — politics, finance, science, religion, military — contains a range of people with a range of values. At one extreme in every field are those who prioritize power, order, and control above all else. **These people find each other across fields.** Their coordination is not a formal conspiracy; it is an emergent convergence of shared incentives.

This convergence gives power-seekers a decisive organizational advantage as societies grow larger and more bureaucratic. Normal people in each field cannot coordinate across fields. Power-seekers can. As society declines and becomes fragmented, the convergence actually grows stronger — because the converging group stays cohesive while everyone else fragments.

To compete with each other for **legitimacy, authority, and unity**, these groups (what the conspiracy theory calls "secret societies") reach backward into history and construct cosmologies showing their continuity with all of history — back to the Knights Templar, back to Rome, back to Babylon. This is why people believe the Freemasons go back a thousand years: elites throughout history have behaved the same way, creating the impression of a continuous agenda even though each generation is simply reinventing the same structure.

### Albert Pike and the Holy Empire of Reason

Prof. Jiang presents Albert Pike (1809–1891), whom he describes as the grand architect of American Freemasonry, and reads from Pike's *Morals and Dogma* — the textbook of Scottish Rite Freemasonry.

Three ideas from Pike's text are central to the episode:

**1. Order out of chaos.** Society is a rough, disordered stone (the "rough ashlar"). The Freemasons' mission is to turn it into a perfect ashlar — a cube — which is their symbol of perfection, of God. "We want to burn down the forest and build a temple with our bare hands." The cube becomes a symbol of divine perfection because it is derived from geometry, and geometry is the language of God.

**2. The hierarchy as a training machine.** Freemasonry has 33 ranks in the Scottish Rite. Each rank teaches a new way of understanding the world. The hierarchy rewards mental flexibility and ideological mastery. It is, in Prof. Jiang's reading, a machine for producing loyal, disciplined, philosophically sophisticated agents of power — "trying to train you into a mastery of knowledge."

**3. The eschatology.** Pike writes that Freemasonry's purpose is "the subjugation of the human that is in man by the divine, the conquest of the appetites and passions by the moral sense and reason." The goal is to create a "holy empire of reason" — a world in which all people have surrendered their emotional, material selves and become geometrically perfect. Prof. Jiang connects this to artificial intelligence directly: "Generative principle, plus geometry, equals God. What does generative and geometry equal? It equals AI." He is not claiming the Freemasons planned AI; he is claiming that the same eschatological impulse — to prove one can create life, to be God — now drives Silicon Valley.

### Brzezinski's Technocracy

Prof. Jiang turns to Zbigniew Brzezinski, national security advisor to Jimmy Carter and founder of the Trilateral Commission (funded by David Rockefeller). He reads from Brzezinski's 1970 book *Between Two Ages*, which he describes as prophetic — written 40-50 years ago but perfectly describing America today:

> "Persisting social crises, the emergence of a charismatic personality, and the exploitation of mass media to obtain public confidence would be the stepping stones in the piecemeal transformation of the United States into a highly controlled society."

Prof. Jiang unpacks this: democracy produces polarization; polarization produces crises; crises produce demand for a Napoleon or Julius Caesar; the result is a technocracy — a bureaucracy run by engineers using data and AI to govern society. The charismatic personality is Trump. The social crisis is the left-right divide. The exploitation of mass media is social media.

Brzezinski also writes that "Marxism represents a further, creative stage in the maturing of man's universal vision" — a victory of reason over belief. Prof. Jiang's gloss: the AI surveillance state is **techno-Marxism**. The Freemason project to create order out of chaos through reason finds its contemporary implementation in communism, and now in AI. Brzezinski was the national security advisor during the Cold War, and he was arguing that Marxism was the correct direction of history.

### Oracle, Palantir, and Operation Stargate

Prof. Jiang then traces three concrete institutions:

**Larry Ellison and Oracle.** Ellison founded what was then called RSI — a database company. In its early years the product did not work: it destroyed data. Yet the CIA and the Navy kept funding it anyway. A CIA employee told an Oracle engineer: "We bought the things knowing damn well it wasn't going to work. We're buying an idea." The Navy: "We were buying a right flip-flyer with the expectations that we would probably have an airline in a few years." Prof. Jiang's point: Oracle and Larry Ellison are, in his reading, creations of the CIA and the Navy. Today Ellison appears with Trump to announce **Operation Stargate** — building data centers across the United States for AI infrastructure.

**Peter Thiel and Palantir.** Thiel is, Prof. Jiang argues, more powerful than Elon Musk — because in 2016 he identified Trump's potential and backed him, identified J.D. Vance and backed him, and got Vance installed as Vice President. Thiel's book *Zero to One* argues that competition is for losers; what you want is monopoly. To get monopoly, you need a secret: "Every great business is built around a secret that's hidden from the outside. A great company is a conspiracy to change the world." Prof. Jiang's reading: if you want to dominate, don't optimize on technology — access the occult; build around secrets of human nature. Thiel tells entrepreneurs: "Start a religion or start a mafia." Thiel founded Palantir with Alex Karp. Palantir's manifesto, *The Technological Republic*, calls explicitly for a technocracy.

**Peter Thiel's influence on Sam Altman and Mark Zuckerberg.** Thiel mentored both. Altman has said: "The most successful founders do not set out to create companies. They are on a mission to create something closer to a religion." This, Prof. Jiang notes, is Thiel's language directly. Altman also said: "People talk about how much energy it takes to train an AI model, but it also takes a lot of energy to train a human — it takes 20 years of life and all the food you eat during that time before you get smart." Prof. Jiang's gloss: "These people literally see no difference between an AI and a human. You're just energy to be exploited."

### The Antichrist Argument

The episode's closing argument is Peter Thiel's recent speeches about the Antichrist. Thiel argues that the Antichrist rises to power by exploiting fears of technology, seducing people with the slogan "peace and safety." Thiel's conclusion: whoever **opposes** the AI surveillance state is the Antichrist. The Antichrist is not the surveillance state — the Antichrist is anyone who resists it. Prof. Jiang's summary: "Whoever prevents us from building the actual surveillance state is pure evil. And once you understand this, we will achieve our holy empire of reason."

A student asks whether Freemasonry is a form of brainwashing. Prof. Jiang's answer: at the heart of the Freemasonic vision is eschatology — a one-world government based in Jerusalem where everyone obeys. The mechanism of compliance is AI: a microchip or algorithmic system that tells you when you should pay your taxes, stop drinking, stop making errors. "It's about alchemy, human perfection, trying to turn you into an obedient robot that can be programmed." He insists this is, in the Freemasonic view, genuinely good: "Once we do that, there'll be peace, there'll be happiness, there'll be safety."

A student objects: if AI fully controls you, what happens to human subjectivity? Prof. Jiang's answer, ventriloquizing the Freemasonic position: "Human subjectivity is bad because it leads to sin. It leads to errors. It leads to confusion. If we all think alike, the world is now perfect. We now achieve a common brotherhood of humanity."

## In Your Own Life

The episode poses one of its sharpest practical challenges through the student's question about human subjectivity. If AI can eliminate error, sin, and confusion — if it can make everyone think alike and the world becomes "perfect" — what is lost?

Prof. Jiang does not answer this directly; he ventriloquizes the Freemasonic position without rebutting it. That deliberate silence is itself an invitation to think.

Apply the framework to your own relationship with systems that promise optimization:
- **Who are the players?** When a platform, algorithm, or institution offers to remove friction from your decisions — what decisions does it actually want to make for you, and who benefits from that arrangement?
- **What are the rules?** Brzezinski describes how "persisting social crises" are the precondition for the transformation of society into a highly controlled state. What crises are being invoked right now to justify expansions of surveillance or control that would not otherwise be accepted?
- **What are the incentives?** Peter Thiel argues that anyone who resists the AI surveillance state is the Antichrist. Notice the structure of this move: it reframes resistance as evil, making compliance feel like virtue. Where else do you encounter that rhetorical structure — where opposition to a powerful system is framed not as legitimate dissent but as moral failure?

The practical question the episode leaves open: is there a way to benefit from AI's genuine capabilities without becoming the "obedient robot that can be programmed"? Or does participating in these systems require accepting their eschatology?

## Synthesis

**The Art of War (孙子兵法) on deception and formlessness:** Sun Tzu writes 兵者，诡道也 — "all warfare is deception." The structural convergence Prof. Jiang describes operates through exactly this principle: power-seekers do not announce their coordination; they operate through the ordinary forms of institutions (a workers' guild, a database company, a venture capital firm, a think tank). The Freemasons hid inside stonemasons' guilds. Oracle hid inside the form of a commercial software company. Palantir calls itself a data analytics firm. The formlessness — 无形 — of the coordination is what makes it durable. Those inside can see the eschatological project; those outside see only a guild, a startup, a commission.

**Machiavelli on the prophet armed:** In *The Prince*, Machiavelli distinguishes between the "armed prophet" and the "unarmed prophet" — the former succeeds; the latter fails. Savonarola had vision but no force; Moses had both. Prof. Jiang's account of the power-seeking convergence fits this structure: the Freemasons failed when they relied on ideological persuasion alone (Savonarola); they succeeded when they combined eschatological vision with financial control (Bank of England) and military power (the Royal Navy, now replaced by data infrastructure and AI prediction). Peter Thiel's move — backing Trump and Vance — is the contemporary version of the armed prophet: the eschatological vision of a technocratic holy empire, combined with actual political power.

**Thucydides on the inevitability of power consolidation:** The Melian Dialogue's logic — "the strong do what they can; the weak suffer what they must" — applies here not between nations but between those inside the structural convergence and those outside it. The student who asks "what about human subjectivity?" is, in Thucydides' terms, the Melians: appealing to a principle (human freedom, moral worth) against a structural reality (the convergence has the coordination advantage). Prof. Jiang does not say the Melians are wrong. He says they will probably lose.

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang says the grand conspiracy theory is "not factual but truthful" — it has no evidence, but it is predictive. Apply his Players / Rules / Incentives framework to explain why: what are the players (power-seekers in each field), the rules (the coordination advantage of convergence), and the incentives (legitimacy, authority, unity) that produce the outcomes the conspiracy theory describes, without requiring any actual conspiracy?

2. Brzezinski wrote in 1970 that "persisting social crises, the emergence of a charismatic personality, and the exploitation of mass media" would be the stepping stones toward a highly controlled society. He was writing about the future. Do you think he was describing a prediction, a plan, or a structural analysis? What difference does it make for how you respond to such a society when you are inside it?

3. Peter Thiel argues that a great company is a "conspiracy to change the world" — a secret society organized around a hidden truth others have missed. Sam Altman says the best founders are on a mission closer to a religion than a business. Prof. Jiang says: if you want to dominate, access the occult, find the secrets of human nature. Is this a cynical description of what power requires — or an instruction manual? Does the answer change your sense of whether you want to participate in these institutions?

4. A student in the class objects: if AI fully controls you, what remains of human subjectivity? Prof. Jiang, voicing the Freemasonic answer, says subjectivity is the problem — it leads to sin, error, confusion. Engage with this seriously: is there a principled response that does not simply assert that subjectivity is valuable, but argues from the episode's own logic why eliminating it would fail on its own terms?
