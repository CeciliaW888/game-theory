# Day 27: Putin Enters the Chat
**Episode:** #27 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=x83HcLWvHI8 | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

Putin has just completed his 25th visit to China. Prof. Jiang uses that diplomatic moment as a doorway into a much deeper question: why, despite all the summit photos and joint statements, is the Russia-China relationship structurally weaker than it looks? He answers by tracing how the US dollar became the global reserve currency — not by design but by historical accident — and how that dollar system created an elite class in China whose interests are bound to the West, not to Russia. From there the episode turns to Putin's grand strategy: how he plans to destroy demand for the US dollar, and what tools he is using across North Korea, Europe, the Global South, India, Iran, and Japan to do it. The episode closes with a blunt prediction: the Russia-China alliance is real for now, but temporary — their long-term interests diverge.

## Core Concepts

### The Opening Puzzle: Best Friends on Paper, Strangers in Practice

Putin's 25th visit to China produced the usual warmth — a concert, a photo unveiling, a joint statement on the multipolar world. But Prof. Jiang draws a sharp contrast between the rhetoric and the reality.

Both Xi and Putin call for a multipolar world and oppose "unilateralism and hegemonism." But their conceptions differ. Xi's vision is conservative: maintain the existing UN-based order, with China and Russia playing a supportive role within it. Putin's vision is activist: Russia and China should take the lead in building the new order, with Russia as a junior partner to China but both driving the initiative.

On the ground the gap is wider. The Power of Siberia 2 pipeline — the flagship of Russia-China energy cooperation — was expected to get an MOU signed at this summit. It did not. China does not want to become too dependent on Russian energy. On Iran, Russia called American bombing "absolute unprovoked aggression" with no justification. China called it "a sudden escalation of tensions" and said military conflict is not a solution. Russia has regional alliances; China avoids participating in disputes among nations.

The trade structure is lopsided. Russia exports mainly energy — coal, LNG, crude oil. China exports vehicles, electronics, and machinery back to Russia. Prof. Jiang's verdict: "This is a very bad deal for Russia." Russia's share of Chinese imports is 5%; its share of Chinese exports is 3%. Russia is now heavily dependent on China (RMB accounts for over 40% of Russian imports since 2022), but China has no equivalent dependence on Russia.

Chinese investors are not going to Russia. Foreign direct investment in Russia fell sharply after the war started. The reasons: Chinese companies respect Western sanctions because they care more about access to Western financial systems than about Russian business. Russia's legal system offers no protection — "if you get screwed over, too bad." And Chinese investors want USD, not rubles.

The deepest indicator is cultural. About 50,000 Chinese are studying in Russia right now. At peak, there were 370,000+ Chinese students in the United States. Only about 60,000 Chinese are learning Russian; effectively everyone else is learning English.

Prof. Jiang's summary: "The relationship between Russia and China seems to be top-down — mandated from the government. But the relationship between the United States and China seems to be bottom-up — where people are choosing to work with the Americans."

### Why Chinese People Choose America: The Dollar System Explained

To explain this bottom-up pull toward America, Prof. Jiang goes back to 1944 and the **Bretton Woods system**.

At the end of World War II, the United States had a problem: factories full of war production capacity with no war to fight. The solution was a deal with Europe and East Asia: the US would lend them dollars to buy American manufactured goods, and those dollars would be backed by gold — the gold standard. From 1950 to 1970, this produced extraordinary prosperity.

But over time, Europe and Japan rebuilt themselves, worked harder, and made cheaper and better products than America. The trade relationship reversed: the US went from creditor to debtor nation. And the US, addicted to easy money, started spending on things it could not afford — the Vietnam War, the moon landings. By 1970, the world recognized the US could not pay off its debts in gold. In 1971, Nixon confirmed it and abandoned the gold standard.

To solve the resulting crisis of confidence, Nixon brought Saudi Arabia and China into the system. Saudi Arabia would sell oil only in US dollars — the **petrodollar**. This created new demand for dollars and kept the system alive.

Prof. Jiang calls the US dollar's position as global reserve currency a "complete accident of history" — the result of winning World War II, not of strategic planning. And he argues it has been **terrible for America** in three structural ways:

1. **Excess liquidity:** To supply the world with dollars, the US must print a lot of them. This causes inflation and is difficult to manage.
2. **Open capital account:** Money can flow in — or flee — the country at any time, making currency management extremely hard.
3. **Financialization:** The economy shifts from making things to managing money — what Prof. Jiang calls "gambling." Factories close; banks multiply.

Yet despite these harms, Americans are addicted to the system because the money is easy. The US keeps expanding the system — going to war to create more demand for dollars — not because expansion is strategic, but because the addiction demands it.

### How the Dollar Monetized Power in China

Why did China join the system? Prof. Jiang's answer is precise and damning: **"The US dollar monetized power."**

Being a political elite in China meant controlling millions of people — but that power had no secure store of value. The dollar system solved this. A Chinese official could take their power over people, convert it into dollars, and export that wealth to the United States, where it would be legally protected. The dollar turned political control into portable, safe wealth.

There were therefore two major beneficiaries of the system: the American financial elite and the Chinese political elite. Both have a deep interest in maintaining it.

This explains why the Russia-China relationship has a ceiling. There is nothing Russia can offer the Chinese elite that the dollar system already provides. If you are a wealthy Chinese family, you send your children to American universities to learn English, earn American degrees, and build connections to the dollar system. The goal of Chinese society, as Prof. Jiang frames it, is to generate as much USD as possible. The Russian ruble cannot compete.

### Putin's Grand Strategy: Destroy the Dollar

If Putin wants to build a new world order, Prof. Jiang argues, there is only one path: **destroy demand for the US dollar as the global reserve currency.**

The mechanism: if foreign countries stop buying US Treasuries, the US government faces a funding crisis. The debt is currently around $39 trillion. At 5% interest, that is nearly $2 trillion in annual interest payments — a figure that grows as the interest rate rises. And the rate rises precisely when fewer people want to buy Treasuries. The US cannot default on its domestic debt without triggering a revolution (depositors would lose everything). So it is forced to find foreign buyers. Putin's strategy is to eliminate those buyers.

Prof. Jiang frames this as a war being fought right now: **Putin is trying to get people to stop buying US Treasuries; Trump is trying to force people to buy them.** The instrument of Trump's approach is war — threatening or destabilizing regions to make everyone more dependent on the dollar system. Putin's instrument is the reverse: creating an alternative pole of gravity.

Putin's coalition of sanctioned states — Russia, Belarus, Iran, and North Korea — forms the nucleus. Syria is destroyed, Venezuela conquered, Myanmar in civil war; only these four remain functional.

**North Korea:** Putin signed a mutual defense pact with Kim Jong-un in June 2024. North Korea had previously relied on a 1961 defense pact with China. Why does Putin add North Korea to his portfolio? Because North Korea can create volatility in the Korean Peninsula — threatening South Korea, alarming Japan, and distracting US forces — at very low cost to Russia. Prof. Jiang notes that North Korean soldiers fight to the end (surrender means consequences for their families at home), making them militarily effective despite poor equipment. North Korea has already sent 10,000 troops to Ukraine. And the demographic arithmetic favors the North: South Korea's fertility rate is 0.81 vs. North Korea's 1.81 — South Korea is demographically dying.

**Europe and Germany:** Russia's invasion of Ukraine forced Germany off cheap Russian gas and onto American LNG at a 50% premium. This has hit the German economy harder than any other in Europe. Simultaneously, immigration has risen sharply across northern Europe — in some Swedish cities, immigrants are 20% of the population. The result: right-wing parties like the AfD are surging. Prof. Jiang argues that Putin's strategy is to drag out the war in Ukraine, create political tension, and allow pro-Russia right-wing parties to come to power — then form an alliance with a Germany that has broken from the US. He draws a historical parallel: war created the political crisis that brought the Bolsheviks to power in Russia in 1917. The longer the war goes, the more pressure builds.

**Global South:** After Russia invaded Ukraine, support for Putin in the Global South actually increased. Prof. Jiang's explanation: from a Western perspective Russia is the aggressor; from a Global South perspective, Russia is standing up against an imperial bully. Russia is active across Africa — trade, military cooperation, nuclear power construction, oil exploration — and is spending significant effort on narrative and propaganda. China is also active in Africa, but focused on economics, not soft power campaigns. Russia is trying to build the narrative that it is liberating the world from Western imperialism.

**India:** Russia and India have grown much closer because of the war. Russia sells sanctioned oil to India, which then sells it on. Russia wants Indian labor — the war has created a manpower shortage — and will need workers to rebuild Ukraine. Prof. Jiang expects Russia-India ties to strengthen significantly over the next 10-20 years.

**Iran:** Prof. Jiang calls Iran "the key to Russia creating a new world order." Iran sits at the center of trade routes connecting Africa, India, and Central Asia. Russia can trade with Iran easily via the Caspian Sea. If Russia controls Iranian access to trade routes, it controls a major alternative artery to the dollar-dominated maritime system. This is why the US has attacked Iran — to block that route and force continued dependence on dollar-mediated trade. Prof. Jiang frames the Iran front and the Ukraine front as the two main battlefronts of what he is calling World War III.

**Israel:** Israel is "the American fortress" in the Middle East — the guarantor against Russian influence spreading across the region. This is why the US supports Israel so strongly: not sentiment, but geopolitics. "Israel is key to keeping Russia in check."

**Japan:** Japan is dependent on energy imports, historically from the Middle East. The war in Iran is forcing Japan to choose between the US and Russia for its energy supply. But Japan's history creates a constraint: before World War II, Japan got 90% of its oil from the US. The US embargoed Japan when Japan became too powerful — which forced Japan to invade Southeast Asia and attack Pearl Harbor. Japan does not want to be completely dependent on the US again. So Japan is buying American LNG but also trying to maintain energy ties with Russia.

Meanwhile Japan has long purchased US Treasuries via the **yen carry trade**: Japanese banks lend yen to corporations at 0% interest; those corporations use the free money to buy US Treasuries at 5%, pocketing the spread. Prof. Jiang calls this a "subsidy or bribe" — Japan paying the US to maintain American troops in Japan and leave Japan alone. But as the geopolitical situation worsens, Japan will need to repatriate that capital, selling US Treasuries — further pressuring the dollar.

### The Structural Limit of the Russia-China Alliance

Prof. Jiang ends with a prediction: the Russia-China alliance will eventually fracture.

Putin knows China's long-term interests are closer to America's than to Russia's. China wants a global policeman — and would rather that policeman be America than Russia, because China's neighbors (Japan, Russia, Vietnam) are historically aggressive. If America steps down, Russia becomes the more immediate threat to China. So China prefers the current American-led order to a power vacuum.

Putin's realistic goal is not to make China a permanent ally, but to keep China neutral — to prevent China from actively joining the US-led effort to contain Russia. "The worst thing that could happen is that China sees Russia as a threat and China and the United States got together to defend the system."

In the short term, both China and Russia benefit from appearing united. In the long term, their interests diverge.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Bretton Woods system (布雷顿森林体系) | The 1944 financial architecture in which the US dollar was backed by gold and became the basis for global trade — the origin of dollar hegemony. |
| Petrodollar (石油美元) | The arrangement following Nixon's 1971 abandonment of the gold standard, by which Saudi Arabia agreed to sell oil exclusively in US dollars, maintaining global demand for the dollar. |
| Global reserve currency (全球储备货币) | A single currency accepted worldwide as the basis for trade and savings. Prof. Jiang calls the dollar's reserve status "an accident of World War II." |
| Financialization (金融化) | The shift of an economy away from productive manufacturing toward financial transactions — what Prof. Jiang describes as a systemic problem caused by reserve-currency status. |
| Open capital account (开放资本账户) | A financial regime in which money can freely enter or leave a country — one of three structural problems Prof. Jiang identifies with the dollar's reserve role. |
| Multipolar world (多极世界) | The vision shared by both Xi and Putin of a world not dominated by a single power — but with different emphases: Xi favors the existing UN framework; Putin favors Russia and China actively leading. |
| Yen carry trade (日元套利交易) | The practice of borrowing yen at near-zero interest from Japanese banks to buy higher-yielding US Treasuries — Prof. Jiang calls it a structural subsidy Japan pays the US. |
| Power of Siberia 2 (西伯利亚力量2号管道) | The planned Russia-to-China natural gas pipeline whose MOU was not signed at the Xi-Putin summit, revealing China's reluctance to become energy-dependent on Russia. |
| Mutual defense pact (共同防御条约) | The June 2024 agreement between Russia and North Korea committing each to militarily defend the other if attacked — previously North Korea's only such pact was with China. |
| Force withdrawal (强制戒断) | Prof. Jiang's term for Putin's strategy: destroying demand for the dollar is, paradoxically, the only way to save America from its own addiction to easy money. |
| US Treasury (美国国债) | US government debt instruments — their demand is the central battlefield of the Russia-America contest; falling demand forces interest rates higher and accelerates America's fiscal crisis. |

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's core move in this episode is to show that what looks like friendship is actually a structural transaction, and that the real relationship is revealed not by what leaders say at summits but by where ordinary people send their children and their money.

Apply this to any alliance or partnership you are part of — professional, organizational, personal. The questions the episode models:

- Where do people actually put their resources (money, time, children's education) when they are not being watched? That reveals the real game.
- Is your alliance or partnership top-down — sustained by institutional mandates — or bottom-up, chosen freely by the people in it? Top-down alliances are fragile; bottom-up ones are durable.
- What does each party actually need from the other? If one party is heavily dependent and the other has alternatives, that asymmetry will eventually reshape the relationship.

The episode also challenges you to identify which systems have designed your own incentives. If you are optimizing for a particular credential, currency, or credential-granting institution — ask who built that hierarchy and why.

## Synthesis

**The Art of War (孙子兵法) — the indirect approach:** Sun Tzu writes that the supreme general wins without fighting — 不战而屈人之兵. Putin's strategy is largely indirect: rather than challenging the US military head-on, he is working to destroy the economic foundation of American power by eliminating demand for its currency. This is the indirect approach applied to grand strategy — attack the enemy's supply lines (in this case, the flow of dollar demand) rather than their army.

**Thucydides on the structure of alliances:** In the Peloponnesian War, Thucydides observed that alliances between powerful states are always uneasy — each partner fears the other will become too strong. The Russia-China relationship displays exactly this dynamic. They are united by a common adversary (US dominance) but divided by the recognition that, once that adversary is weakened, they would be each other's main competitor. Thucydides' Sparta and Athens cooperated against Persia; within a generation, they were at war.

**Machiavelli on the prince who uses others' arms:** In *The Prince*, Machiavelli warns against relying on auxiliary forces — troops provided by an ally — because if they win, you become dependent; if they lose, you are ruined. North Korea's 10,000 troops fighting in Ukraine on Russia's behalf are exactly this kind of auxiliary. Putin is using them because he cannot afford politically to mobilize a full Russian army. Machiavelli would note the danger: Kim Jong-un now has leverage over Putin, and the mutual defense pact is a formal acknowledgment of that leverage.
