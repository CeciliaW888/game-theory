# Day 29: The Final Examination
**Episode:** #29 | **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RE2UribEFIo | **Series:** Predictive History — Prof. Jiang Xueqin

## What This Episode Is About

This is Prof. Jiang's final class at the school where he has taught for four years, and the last session of the Predictive History game-theory series. Rather than a lecture, it is a Q&A: Prof. Jiang and his students Alan and Vincent read questions submitted by Substack founding members and YouTube viewers and he answers them live. The questions span geopolitics (the dollar, Iran, Japan, Russia, China), personal guidance (what to do when the world collapses, how to prepare children), the nature of games and cycles, and Prof. Jiang's own intellectual journey. Through every answer, he returns to the same core argument: geopolitics is a game with players, rules, and incentives; empires rise and fall in cycles; and what makes humans resilient is not money or technology but imagination, creativity, and love. The episode doubles as a window into how Prof. Jiang applies — and openly limits — his own framework.

## Core Concepts

### The Game Has No Friends

Early in the Q&A, Prof. Jiang summarizes the geopolitical situation plainly: "There's no loyalty. There are no friends. It's just a game. Everyone's just looking for the optimal strategy." He applies this to the US, Israel, Russia, and China simultaneously. No country's stated position is the same as its actual incentive. The game-theory frame is not a metaphor — it is, he argues, the literal structure of international relations.

### Elite Overproduction and the Mechanism of Decline

When asked whether the world can reform itself before collapsing, Prof. Jiang gives a specific structural explanation: "A nation is a collection of vested interests ... conflict among these vested interests — what we call elite overproduction — that's what leads to decline." The competing elites are so focused on defeating each other internally that they ignore the larger game. They are "willing to sacrifice everything, anything, in order to win." A billionaire, he says bluntly, "is a person who is willing to kill his own grandmother for money." The implication: the people in charge of declining empires are not making mistakes — they are pursuing their own rational incentives within a system that is destroying the collective.

### How Empires Decline — and Why They Use Force

Prof. Jiang distinguishes soft power from hard power in imperial management. Before decline sets in, an empire controls the world through soft power: "Hollywood, newspapers like the New York Times, through an image that America is a benevolent power." As the world turns against an empire, it is forced to switch to coercion. The problem: once you use force, the world eventually unites against you. "This is not about America shifting strategy, making America great again. This is about an empire in decline [that] will always use force in order to maintain its advantage over the world." And when the empire retreats internally, the elite conflicts that were previously exported abroad become internalized — the faction fights come home.

### Trump Is Not Playing 4D Chess

Prof. Jiang is direct: "Trump is stupid. He's playing 4D chess in American politics where he's trying to get a third term — that's what he wants." The grand strategy narrative is not Trump's plan but a structural pattern: an empire in decline defaults to coercive behavior because it has no other tool left. The people behind Trump — oil companies, Silicon Valley, the military-industrial complex — are using American power to promote their own faction's interests, not the national interest.

### Falsifying the Framework — What Would Change His Mind

One viewer asks the sharpest methodological question of the episode: what concrete, falsifiable 12-month outcome would prove his thesis wrong? Prof. Jiang's answer is unusually specific:

1. The US midterms surprising him in an unexpected direction
2. Trump dying — "that would change a lot of my predictions"
3. The Democratic Party producing a genuinely charismatic, grassroots, revolutionary leader — "I would think that my entire worldview is wrong. That we can avert the collapse of history"

He also admits openly: "Yes, I do oversimplify my analysis. The world is extremely complex ... you're exactly right in that I need to consider more factors, more possibilities, and more data points." Building the skeleton framework comes first; nuance is added iteratively. "Don't be too concerned about being factual ... The skeletal framework [comes first] and then you build from there."

### Pax Silicon vs. Pax Judaica — Trade Networks Need Nation-States

A viewer proposes "Pax Silicon" — AI-backed trade networks — as the successor to Pax Judaica. Prof. Jiang's response clarifies the rules layer of the framework: "Trade networks are often very, very fragile. Trading networks require the cooperation of different nation-states to maintain their coherence. And what we know from history is that power comes from the nation-state." A trade network cannot sustain itself; it requires a military-backed sovereign behind it. Therefore Pax Silicon, if it exists, is backed by Pax Judaica. The network is the instrument; the nation-state is the player.

### Three Criteria for Societal Resilience

When asked about Serbia's geopolitical future, Prof. Jiang sets aside diplomatic maneuvering and offers three diagnostic questions for measuring how resilient any society actually is:

1. **Is it energetic?** — Do people work hard and take work seriously?
2. **Is it open-minded?** — Is it willing to learn from mistakes, be humble, and innovate?
3. **Is it cohesive?** — Is there a shared identity that makes people willing to sacrifice for each other?

He applies the same criteria to Japan — not as the dominant industrial power today, but as the nation with the strongest historical record of adaptation under existential pressure (the Mongol invasions, the Meiji Restoration, post-WWII reconstruction). "The Japanese have a history of resilience, of making changes when their survival is threatened. And that's why I think Japan will probably do it again."

### Winning Can Mean Destroying Yourself

A viewer asks about the US-China trade war and whether American chokepoint strategy might collapse the very Chinese consumer market America wants to extract wealth from. Prof. Jiang's answer is one of the most concentrated expressions of game theory in the entire series: "In a game, your objective is to win the game. Now, by winning the game, it may cause the game to collapse — but you don't care. The point is to win the game. And you'll use whatever tools you have in order to win the game. It's possible that this US-China trade war will end up collapsing both nations, but that's not the point. The point is to win the game. The point is not to survive the game." This is the logic of credible threats and escalation dominance taken to its extreme: the willingness to destroy yourself is what makes your threats credible.

### History Moves in Cycles — Accept It

Multiple questions touch on civilizational decline. Prof. Jiang's consistent answer: stop assigning moral labels to cycles. "Is America good or bad? America is just America. Is Russia good or bad? It's just Russia." The Yuga cycle, samsara, the Fibonacci pattern — different traditions converge on the same insight: rise, fall, up, down. "What is hubris? What is stupid? What is arrogant? It's a belief that you can stop history." Empires declining is like earthquakes coming. You cannot stop the earthquake; you can only prepare for it. The more an empire resists its natural decline, the greater the suffering and the faster the fall.

### What Prepares You for a Collapsing World Order

When asked what advice he gives his children — and by extension anyone — Prof. Jiang's answer is not financial or logistical. He names three things he tells his kids daily:

1. "I will always love you — no matter what happens, no matter what you do."
2. "I believe in you — whatever you do will be good."
3. "Believe in yourself."

His broader advice to viewers: read books — not to accumulate information but to develop the capacity to think critically, independently, and deeply; to hold an inner dialogue with yourself. "It doesn't matter what you study. What does matter is to read books." Imagination, creativity, resilience, and empathy are what make humans capable of rebuilding after collapse — not bunkers, gold, or AI.

### The Monad — Connection to the Divine

The concept of the monad appears across the episode as Prof. Jiang's term for what gives life meaning and what connects humans to the divine. He describes feeling it most strongly when teaching and seeing students learn, when loving his wife, when holding a newborn. "Connection to the monad is the greatest happiness in the world." He also extends it: even someone who is isolated and non-verbal — like Van Gogh — can connect to the monad through color and art. The more tolerant, loving, and generous a society is, the greater its collective creativity. Autistic children in classrooms are his concrete example: a society that medicates non-conformity rather than tolerating it is losing creative capacity.

### Why Games Are Not the Natural State of Humanity

A viewer asks why games are such a useful framework for understanding the world. Prof. Jiang gives a historically grounded answer: games — meaning zero-sum status competition — are not eternal. "You go back a few thousand years when most of the world was actually matriarchal. That was not a game-based society. Matriarchal societies tend to be very cooperative. It really is about balance and harmony, making sure everyone gets along." Patriarchal status competition created the zero-sum game. It is therefore possible that in a different future, games may not be the dominant structure. The framework is descriptive of the current era, not a law of nature.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |

| Elite overproduction (精英过剩) | A condition in which too many ambitious people compete for too few positions of power, producing intense factional conflict that consumes the civilization from within — Prof. Jiang's structural explanation for imperial decline |
| Pax Judaica (犹太和平) | Prof. Jiang's term for an Israeli-centered Middle East order — distinguished from Pax Silicon in that it is nation-state-backed and therefore durable |
| Pax Silicon (硅谷和平) | A proposed AI-backed trade-network order — Prof. Jiang argues it cannot sustain itself without a nation-state behind it, and that nation-state is Israel |
| The monad (一元) | Prof. Jiang's term for the unifying divine consciousness that connects all humans — experienced in love, teaching, art, and genuine connection; the source of creativity and meaning |
| Resilience (韧性) | The capacity of a society to adapt and survive existential pressure — measured by Prof. Jiang through three criteria: energy, open-mindedness, and cohesion |
| Elite overproduction (精英过剩) | See above |
| Soft power (软实力) | Control through culture, media, and narrative rather than force — the tool of empires in their prime, replaced by coercion as they decline |
| Samsara (轮回) | The Vedic-Buddhist concept of cycles of existence — Prof. Jiang cites it alongside Kabbalah and the Yuga cycle as evidence that all traditions recognize historical cycles of rise and fall |
| Meiji Restoration (明治维新) | Prof. Jiang's primary historical example of Japanese resilience — a rapid, self-directed transformation of the entire political and industrial system in response to external threat |
| Imagination (想象力) | For Prof. Jiang, the highest human faculty — the source of creativity, innovation, and adaptability; developed through reading, travel, listening, and connection to the monad, and diminished by technology dependence |

## In Your Own Life

Prof. Jiang's most direct personal guidance in this episode: **make yourself useful through depth of thought, not technological dependence.**

When asked what branch of study has a future in the modern world, he says: "Ignore AI. If you want to become a computer programmer ... you're going to be disappointed. If you really want to prepare for the future, read books. The skill that you will need in the future is the capacity to think critically, think independently, and think deeply about the world. By reading books, you can start to engage in an inner dialogue with yourself."

This is a direct application of the Players / Rules / Incentives framework to your own life:

- **Players:** You are one player. AI companies, governments, and corporations are players trying to make you dependent on their systems.
- **Rules:** The rule is that attention and cognitive labor are finite. Every hour spent outsourcing thinking to AI is an hour not spent developing your own capacity.
- **Incentives:** Their incentive is dependence; yours is independence. These are opposed.

The practical checklist Prof. Jiang implies throughout the episode:

1. Read books — not for information, but to develop your inner monologue
2. Travel and experience viewpoints different from your own
3. Do things outside your comfort zone
4. Build community with the people immediately around you — know your neighbors
5. Invest in relationships, not assets; the monad is experienced through love, not through material security

On the draft question: if a national draft comes, his advice is not to run but to make yourself useful now, before it arrives. The draft's real purpose, he argues, is to train people who have become economically useless due to passive consumption. The antidote is not to wait for the draft but to develop real skills and real depth beforehand.

## Synthesis

**On cycles and Thucydides:** Thucydides' *History of the Peloponnesian War* opens with the claim that the same pattern will recur because human nature does not change. Prof. Jiang's cyclical argument — rise, hubris, overextension, collapse — is in this tradition. His specific addition is the mechanism: elite overproduction converts external expansion into internal factional warfare, accelerating collapse. Thucydides saw the same dynamic in Athens after the Sicilian Expedition, where factional conflict at home (the oligarchic coup of 411 BCE) compounded the military disaster abroad.

**On Machiavelli and the prince's dilemma:** Machiavelli argued that a prince must sometimes perform acts of cruelty decisively, because half-measures create enemies without eliminating threats. Prof. Jiang's analysis of Trump and the American elite's use of force maps onto this: they are using coercion because soft power has failed, but doing so in a way that is neither decisive nor strategically coherent — "not for the national interest, but to promote the interest of certain factions." This is Machiavelli's failure mode: cruelty deployed for factional benefit rather than political consolidation.

**On the Art of War (孙子兵法) and self-knowledge:** Sun Tzu's most famous strategic principle is 知己知彼，百战不殆 — "know yourself and know the enemy, and in a hundred battles you will not be defeated." The deepest practical lesson of this episode is epistemological: Prof. Jiang models intellectual honesty about the limits of his own framework. He acknowledges oversimplification, names specific conditions that would falsify his predictions, and distinguishes between building a skeleton model and refining toward nuance. This is precisely the kind of self-knowledge Sun Tzu demands — not just of opponents, but of yourself and your own analytical limitations.

## Think About It

1. Prof. Jiang says "the point is to win the game, not to survive the game" — and that winning can mean destroying yourself. Apply the Players / Rules / Incentives framework to the US-China trade war: what are the actual incentives of the specific players (not the nations, but the factions within them), and does his analysis hold?

2. Prof. Jiang gives three criteria for measuring societal resilience: energy, open-mindedness, and cohesion. Apply them to a community or organization you know well. Which criterion is strongest? Which is the binding constraint on its future?

3. He argues that frameworks matter more than facts: "Don't be too concerned about being factual ... start with a framework." But he also accepts the critique that he oversimplifies. When is a framework a productive skeleton, and when does it become a bias that filters out the evidence that would falsify it? What would a discipline of healthy framework use look like?

4. Prof. Jiang says games — zero-sum status competition — are not a permanent feature of human nature but a feature of patriarchal societies. If he is right, what would a non-game-based social order look like, and does the course's framework still apply there?

## What's Next

This is the final episode of the Predictive History series as captured in this course. Prof. Jiang closes by announcing his next project: a two-week line-by-line seminar on Dante's *Divine Comedy*, which he describes as the work that has most shaped his thinking over the past twenty years. His ambition, stated in the final minutes of class: "I want to be doing this for the next 50 years." The course ends, but the framework does not. Every situation you encounter can be read through the same lens: who are the players, what are the rules, what are the actual incentives? The rest is application.
